# Poisson process

A Poisson process $(N_t)_{t\ge0}$ with rate $\lambda>0$ models the random arrival of discrete events over continuous time. It is the natural probabilistic model whenever one counts occurrences that happen at a constant average rate and do not interact with each other. Applications range from customer arrivals at a service desk and radioactive decay particles hitting a detector to the accumulation of insurance claims and the number of connection requests to a web server. The process is specified by the distribution of the count $N_t$ at each time $t$, and the defining assumption is that what happens in disjoint time intervals is independent — the past gives no information about the future beyond the current accumulated count, a property closely related to the memoryless property of the exponential distribution.

The Poisson process is intimately connected to the exponential distribution because the waiting time between successive events turns out to be exponential. This duality between the counting process and the interarrival times is the central structural fact about Poisson processes: one can construct the entire process either by specifying the increment distribution or by specifying that the gaps between jumps are i.i.d. exponential. Understanding both viewpoints is the main goal of this section.

One of the most useful features of the Poisson process is its mathematical tractability. Because the Poisson distribution has mean equal to variance, and because the exponential interarrival times are memoryless, many quantities can be computed in closed form. Moreover, the independent-increments assumption makes it easy to analyze systems where multiple independent streams of events interact — the superposition of independent Poisson processes is again a Poisson process, and a Poisson process that is thinned by independent Bernoulli selections splits into independent Poisson processes. These properties, together with the Poisson–binomial approximation, explain why the Poisson process appears throughout probability, statistics, and operations research.

## definition

Let $(N_t)_{t\ge0}$ be a collection of integer-valued random variables indexed by time $t\ge0$, where $N_t$ is the number of events that have occurred by time $t$. One says that $(N_t)_{t\ge0}$ is a Poisson process of rate $\lambda>0$ when it satisfies four conditions:

1. $N_0=0$ almost surely — the process starts at zero.
2. The sample paths $t\mapsto N_t(\omega)$ are right-continuous — at a jump time, the process takes the value after the jump, which is the usual convention for counting processes.
3. Increments over disjoint time intervals are independent — for any $0\le t_1<t_2<\cdots<t_k$, the random variables $N_{t_2}-N_{t_1},N_{t_3}-N_{t_2},\dots,N_{t_k}-N_{t_{k-1}}$ are mutually independent.
4. For every $0\le s<t$, the increment $N_t-N_s$ follows a Poisson distribution with mean $\lambda(t-s)$, written $N_t-N_s\sim\operatorname{Pois}(\lambda(t-s))$.

The intuition for condition (iii) is that the process has no memory: knowing how many events have occurred up to time $s$ gives no information about how many will occur between $s$ and $t$, beyond what the Poisson distribution already encodes. Condition (iv) says that the mean number of events in an interval is proportional to its length (rate $\lambda$), and the Poisson shape of the distribution comes from the law of rare events — each infinitesimal subinterval contributes a tiny probability of an event, and the total count is the sum of many such near-independent Bernoulli trials.

From these conditions one obtains the basic moment properties of the process. The mean and variance of the count at time $t$ are both equal to $\lambda t$, so $E[N_t]=\lambda t$ and $\operatorname{Var}(N_t)=\lambda t$. The sample paths $t\mapsto N_t(\omega)$ are nondecreasing and piecewise constant, with jumps of size exactly $1$ occurring at the random event times. The equal mean-and-variance property is a signature of the Poisson distribution; for a renewal process with a non-exponential interarrival distribution, the mean and variance would generally differ.

It is worth emphasizing that conditions (i)–(iv) uniquely determine the finite-dimensional distributions of $(N_t)$. The proof of uniqueness uses the independent-increments structure: the joint distribution of $(N_{t_1},N_{t_2},\dots,N_{t_k})$ is determined by the distributions of the increments $N_{t_1},N_{t_2}-N_{t_1},\dots,N_{t_k}-N_{t_{k-1}}$, each of which is Poisson with known mean. Conversely, one can construct a process satisfying these conditions — for instance, by taking i.i.d. exponential interarrival times as described below — so the definition is both necessary and sufficient.

## memoryless property and the exponential connection

The exponential distribution is the only continuous distribution on $[0,\infty)$ that is memoryless: for any $s,t\ge0$, $$P(T>s+t\mid T>s)=P(T>t).$$ In words, if a component with lifetime $T$ has survived for $s$ units of time, its remaining lifetime is still distributed as $T$ — it does not "age." For the exponential distribution $T\sim\operatorname{E}(\lambda)$, the survival function $P(T>t)=e^{-\lambda t}$ satisfies this identity: $$P(T>s+t\mid T>s)=\frac{P(T>s+t)}{P(T>s)}=\frac{e^{-\lambda(s+t)}}{e^{-\lambda s}}=e^{-\lambda t}=P(T>t).$$ Conversely, if a distribution on $[0,\infty)$ is memoryless and non-degenerate, it must be exponential; this can be proved by solving the functional equation $g(s+t)=g(s)g(t)$ for $g(t)=P(T>t)$, which forces $g(t)=e^{-\lambda t}$.

Why does this matter for the Poisson process? The interarrival times $T_1,T_2,\dots$ turn out to be i.i.d. $\operatorname{E}(\lambda)$, and their memoryless property is what makes the Poisson process "restart" at each jump time. After the $k$-th event, the time until the next event has the same exponential distribution and is independent of everything that happened before — this is exactly the independent-increments condition (iii) in action. The point is that the memoryless property and the independent-increments assumption are two sides of the same coin: if the waiting time until the next event does not depend on how long we have already waited, then the number of events in disjoint intervals must be independent.

__Worked example.__ Suppose a radioactive counter records particles as a Poisson process with $\lambda=2$ per second. Given that no particle has been detected for the last $3$ seconds, what is the probability of waiting another $2$ seconds or more before the next particle? By the memoryless property, $$P(T_1>2+3\mid T_1>3)=P(T_1>2)=e^{-2\cdot2}=e^{-4}\approx0.0183.$$ The answer is the same as if we started from scratch — the $3$ seconds of waiting do not "use up" any of the remaining lifetime.

## interarrival times

Define $T_1=\inf\{t\ge0:N_t>0\}$, the time of the first jump, and for $k\ge2$ let $T_k$ be the time between the $(k-1)$-th and $k$-th jumps. These are the interarrival times of the Poisson process.

The fundamental connection between the Poisson process and the exponential distribution is given by the construction from i.i.d. exponentials: if $T_1,T_2,\dots\sim\operatorname{E}(\lambda)$ are i.i.d., then setting $$X_n=\sum_{k=1}^n T_k,\qquad N_t=|\{n\ge0:X_n\le t\}|$$ defines a Poisson process of rate $\lambda$. Conversely, for any Poisson process, the interarrival times $T_1,T_2,\dots$ are i.i.d. $\operatorname{E}(\lambda)$.

The proof of the forward direction (counting definition $\Rightarrow$ exponential interarrivals) uses the survival-function computation $P(T_1>t)=P(N_t=0)=e^{-\lambda t}$, then applies the independent-increments property to show that $T_2$ has the same distribution and is independent of $T_1$.

The reverse direction (exponential construction $\Rightarrow$ counting process satisfying conditions) verifies each of the four conditions. The key computation is the distribution of $N_t$: because $N_t=k$ iff the $k$-th arrival occurs by time $t$ and the $(k+1)$-th occurs after $t$, we have $$P(N_t=k)=P(X_k\le t<X_{k+1}).$$ Since $X_k=\sum_{i=1}^k T_i$ is the sum of $k$ i.i.d. $\operatorname{E}(\lambda)$ random variables, it follows a gamma distribution $X_k\sim\operatorname{Gamma}(k,\lambda)$ with density $$f_{X_k}(s)=\frac{\lambda^k s^{k-1}e^{-\lambda s}}{(k-1)!},\qquad s\ge0.$$ Conditional on $X_k=s$, the next interarrival time $T_{k+1}$ is independent of $X_k$ and has survival $P(T_{k+1}>t-s)=e^{-\lambda(t-s)}$, hence $$\begin{aligned} P(N_t=k) &=\int_0^t f_{X_k}(s)\,P(T_{k+1}>t-s)\,ds =\int_0^t\frac{\lambda^k s^{k-1}e^{-\lambda s}}{(k-1)!}\,e^{-\lambda(t-s)}\,ds\\[4pt] &=e^{-\lambda t}\frac{\lambda^k}{(k-1)!}\int_0^t s^{k-1}\,ds =e^{-\lambda t}\frac{\lambda^k}{(k-1)!}\cdot\frac{t^k}{k} =e^{-\lambda t}\frac{(\lambda t)^k}{k!}, \end{aligned}$$ which is the $\operatorname{Pois}(\lambda t)$ probability mass function $P(N_t=k)=e^{-\lambda t}(\lambda t)^k/k!$. Thus the counting process constructed from i.i.d. exponential interarrivals satisfies condition (iv). The independent-increments property then follows from the memoryless property of the exponential: after any fixed time $t$, the residual waiting time until the next event is still $\operatorname{E}(\lambda)$ and is independent of everything that happened before $t$.

A useful corollary is the distribution of the $n$-th arrival time $X_n=\sum_{k=1}^n T_k$. Since $X_n$ is the sum of $n$ i.i.d. $\operatorname{E}(\lambda)$ random variables, it follows a $\operatorname{Gamma}(n,\lambda)$ distribution with cumulative distribution function $$P(X_n\le t)=1-e^{-\lambda t}\sum_{j=0}^{n-1}\frac{(\lambda t)^j}{j!}.$$ This formula exhibits the relationship between the gamma and Poisson distributions: the probability that the $n$-th arrival occurs by time $t$ is exactly the probability that a Poisson random variable with mean $\lambda t$ is at least $n$. This duality is often called the gamma–Poisson relationship or the Poisson–Erlang distribution.

## further properties

The Poisson process enjoys several structural properties that make it a versatile building block for more complex stochastic models.

### superposition

If $(N_t^{(1)})$ and $(N_t^{(2)})$ are independent Poisson processes with rates $\lambda_1$ and $\lambda_2$, then their superposition $N_t=N_t^{(1)}+N_t^{(2)}$ is a Poisson process with rate $\lambda_1+\lambda_2$.

__Proof sketch.__ The sum of two independent Poisson random variables is Poisson with the sum of their means, so for any interval $(s,t]$, $N_t-N_s\sim\operatorname{Pois}((\lambda_1+\lambda_2)(t-s))$. Independent increments follow because each component process has independent increments and they are independent of each other. The superposition property extends to any finite number of independent Poisson processes.

This property is useful in queuing and network theory, where two independent streams of customers (say, online orders and in-store customers) merge into a single queue. It also implies that if a Poisson process is observed indirectly as a component of a larger sum, the component process cannot be recovered from the aggregated data alone.

### thinning

Suppose $(N_t)$ is a Poisson process with rate $\lambda$, and each event is independently classified as type A with probability $p$ and type B with probability $1-p$. Let $N_t^{(A)}$ be the counting process of type-A events and $N_t^{(B)}$ that of type-B events. Then $(N_t^{(A)})$ and $(N_t^{(B)})$ are independent Poisson processes with rates $\lambda p$ and $\lambda(1-p)$ respectively.

__Proof sketch.__ For any interval $(s,t]$, conditional on $N_t-N_s=n$, the number of type-A events is $\operatorname{Bin}(n,p)$ and the number of type-B events is $\operatorname{Bin}(n,1-p)$, and they are independent of events in other intervals. Unconditionally, by the law of total probability or probability-generating-function arguments, $N_t^{(A)}-N_s^{(A)}\sim\operatorname{Pois}(\lambda p(t-s))$ and $N_t^{(B)}-N_s^{(B)}\sim\operatorname{Pois}(\lambda(1-p)(t-s))$. The independence of the two thinned processes follows from the fact that the binomial counts within each interval are independent across intervals, and the Poisson decomposition of the binomial sum.

Thinning is the reverse operation of superposition: if two independent Poisson processes are superposed, splitting them via a Bernoulli trial recovers two independent Poisson processes whose rates sum to the original rate. This property is widely used in simulation — to generate a Poisson process with rate $\lambda p$, one can generate a Poisson process with rate $\lambda$ and then thin it.

__Worked example.__ A Poisson process of particles with rate $\lambda=10$ per second hits a detector that registers each particle independently with probability $p=0.3$. The registered particles follow a Poisson process of rate $3$ per second, and the missed particles follow an independent Poisson process of rate $7$ per second. If a second detector records the missed particles, the two detectors observe independent Poisson processes.

### PASTA property <!-- check: ignore-line[header_style]: PASTA is an acronym for Poisson Arrivals See Time Averages -->

PASTA stands for _Poisson Arrivals See Time Averages_. In a queuing system where arrivals follow a Poisson process, the fraction of arrivals that find the system in a given state equals the time-average fraction of time the system spends in that state (in steady state).

The intuition is that a Poisson process has independent increments, so the state of the system at an arrival instant is independent of the arrival itself — the arrival does not "know" what state the system is in. More formally, let $X(t)$ be the state of the system at time $t$, and let $A_t$ be the event that an arrival occurs at time $t$. Then $P(X(t)\in S\mid A_t)=P(X(t)\in S)$ for any set $S$, because the increment in $[t,t+dt)$ is independent of the past up to time $t$. If $X(t)$ is stationary, the right-hand side equals the time-average probability, so arriving customers see the same distribution as a random time sample.

PASTA is a powerful tool for analyzing $M/M/1$ and $M/G/1$ queues: it allows one to write balance equations based on what an arriving customer finds, rather than what a random observer sees.

### compound Poisson process

A compound Poisson process is a stochastic process $S_t=\sum_{i=1}^{N_t}Y_i$, where $(N_t)$ is a Poisson process of rate $\lambda$ and $Y_1,Y_2,\dots$ are i.i.d. random variables independent of $(N_t)$. The value $S_t$ is the cumulative sum of the jump sizes up to time $t$, where each event contributes a random amount $Y_i$ instead of a unit increment.

The mean and variance of $S_t$ follow from Wald's equation and the law of total variance: $$E[S_t]=\lambda t\,E[Y_1],\qquad \operatorname{Var}(S_t)=\lambda t\,(E[Y_1^2]).$$ Derivation: $E[S_t]=E[E[S_t\mid N_t]]=E[N_tE[Y_1]]=\lambda t E[Y_1]$. For the variance, $\operatorname{Var}(S_t)=E[\operatorname{Var}(S_t\mid N_t)]+\operatorname{Var}(E[S_t\mid N_t])=E[N_t\operatorname{Var}(Y_1)]+\operatorname{Var}(N_tE[Y_1])=\lambda t\operatorname{Var}(Y_1)+(\lambda t)(E[Y_1])^2=\lambda t E[Y_1^2]$.

Compound Poisson processes model cumulative claim amounts in insurance (where each claim has a random size), total rainfall from discrete storm events, and the total number of infections in a disease outbreak. They are the building blocks of Lévy processes, and every Lévy process with finite jump activity can be represented as a compound Poisson process plus a drift and a Brownian component.

## queuing applications

__Worked example (post office).__ Customers arrive at a post office as a Poisson process with rate $\lambda=1/2$ per minute, so the expected number of arrivals in any ten-minute period is $E[N_{10}]=5$. The probability that at most four customers arrive in ten minutes is $$P(N_{10}\le4)=e^{-5}\sum_{k=0}^4\frac{5^k}{k!}\approx0.4405.$$ Because increments over disjoint intervals are independent, the joint probability of three arrivals in the first ten minutes and five more in the following twenty minutes factors as $$P(N_{10}=3,N_{30}-N_{10}=5)=P(N_{10}=3)\,P(N_{30}-N_{10}=5)\approx0.0053,$$ where each factor is a Poisson probability computed from the respective interval lengths.

__The $M/M/1$ queue.__ A classic application of the Poisson process is the $M/M/1$ queue: customers arrive as a Poisson process with rate $\lambda$, service times are i.i.d. exponential with rate $\mu$, and there is a single server with infinite buffer. The "M/M/1" notation means Markovian (memoryless) arrivals, Markovian service times, and one server. The arrival process being Poisson ensures that the interarrival times are exponential, and the exponential service times make the number of customers in the system a continuous-time Markov chain.

The $M/M/1$ queue is stable when $\lambda<\mu$ (traffic intensity $\rho=\lambda/\mu<1$). In steady state, the probability that the system contains $n$ customers (including the one in service) is $P_n=(1-\rho)\rho^n$, a geometric distribution. The expected number of customers in the system is $E[L]=\rho/(1-\rho)$, and the expected waiting time in the queue (including service) is $E[W]=1/(\mu-\lambda)$, by Little's law $E[L]=\lambda E[W]$.

__The waiting-time paradox (inspection paradox).__ For a Poisson process with rate $\lambda$, consider a randomly chosen time $t$ and let $L$ be the length of the interarrival interval that contains $t$. Intuitively one might expect $E[L]=1/\lambda$, the mean interarrival time. However, because longer intervals are more likely to contain a randomly chosen point, $E[L]=2/\lambda$ — twice the mean. More precisely, if the interarrival distribution has density $f$, the length-biased sampling density is $tf(t)/E[T]$, where $t/E[T]$ weights the intervals, and for the exponential distribution this gives $E[L]=E[T^2]/E[T]=(2/\lambda^2)/(1/\lambda)=2/\lambda$. This phenomenon is called the inspection paradox or waiting-time paradox, and it is a common pitfall when estimating interarrival times from random-time observations.

For example, if buses arrive at a stop according to a Poisson process with rate $\lambda=2$ per hour (mean interarrival $0.5$ hours), a passenger arriving at a random time finds themselves in an interarrival interval whose expected length is $E[L]=2/2=1$ hour — twice the true mean gap. This bias occurs because longer gaps occupy more clock time and thus have a higher probability of containing the randomly chosen arrival instant; a passenger is proportionally more likely to land in a $1$-hour gap than in a $0.1$-hour gap. The perceived "waiting time" therefore feels longer than the average gap across all scheduled interarrivals.

## relationship to the binomial distribution

The Poisson process is intimately connected to the binomial distribution through the law of rare events. Partition the interval $[0,t]$ into $n$ subintervals of length $t/n$. For large $n$, the number $N_t$ of events can be approximated by the sum of $n$ independent Bernoulli random variables, each with success probability $\lambda t/n$ (the approximate probability of an event in that tiny subinterval). The sum of these Bernoulli variables is $\operatorname{Bin}(n,\lambda t/n)$, and as $n\to\infty$ with $np=\lambda t$ fixed, the binomial distribution converges to $\operatorname{Pois}(\lambda t)$.

This limiting argument provides an alternative construction of the Poisson process: for each $n$, define the counting process $N_t^{(n)}$ that places at most one event in each subinterval $((k-1)t/n,kt/n]$ with probability $\lambda t/n$, independently across subintervals. Then $N_t^{(n)}\Rightarrow N_t$ weakly as $n\to\infty$, where $N_t$ is the Poisson process of rate $\lambda$. The approximation implies that the Poisson process is the correct model for counting events that occur independently in continuous time when the probability of an event in any very short interval is proportional to the interval length.

Numerical illustration: for $\lambda=2$ and $t=1$, the exact Poisson probability $P(N_1=0)=e^{-2}\approx0.1353$. The binomial approximation $\operatorname{Bin}(n,2/n)$ gives $P(N_1^{(n)}=0)=(1-2/n)^n$, which converges to $e^{-2}$ from below, reaching $0.1330$ at $n=100$ and $0.1348$ at $n=1000$. Even moderate $n$ yields close agreement.
