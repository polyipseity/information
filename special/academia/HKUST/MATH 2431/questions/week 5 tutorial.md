> In a 52-card deck, an opponent draws 5 cards. Find the probability that the hand contains at least two queens under the following conditions:
>
> - (a) The hand contains at least one queen.
> - (b) The hand contains the queen of clubs.
>
> Solution:
>
> - Define $A_j$ to mean {@{exactly $j$ queens in 5 cards, so $P(A_j)=\dfrac{\binom4j\binom{48}{5-j}}{\binom{52}5}$}@}.
> - (a) Conditioning on {@{at least one queen gives $P(A_{\ge2}\mid A_{\ge1})=\frac{1-P(A_0)-P(A_1)}{1-P(A_0)}\approx0.1222$}@}, where {@{the numerator removes the 0-queen and 1-queen cases from the conditioned sample space}@}.
> - (b) If $D$ means {@{the queen of clubs is included}@}, then {@{the conditional probability is $P(A_{\ge2}\mid D)=\frac{P(A_2\cap D)+P(A_3\cap D)+P(A_4\cap D)}{P(D)}\approx0.2214$}@}; here the conditioned event already {@{contributes one queen}@}, so {@{only hands with at least one additional queen count}@}. <!--SR:!2026-05-06,14,290!2026-05-06,14,290!2026-05-11,16,290!2026-05-11,16,290!2026-05-09,14,290!2026-05-06,14,290!2026-05-06,14,290-->

> A simple random walk on $\mathbb{Z}^2$ starts at $(0,0)$ and moves to a nearest neighbor uniformly at each step. Give an expression for the probability of returning to the origin after $2n$ steps.
>
> Solution: A return to the origin after $2n$ steps requires {@{vertical and horizontal cancellations}@}: if there are {@{$k$ up-steps then there must also be $k$ down-steps}@}, leaving {@{$2n-2k$ steps split as $n-k$ right and $n-k$ left}@}. Thus the count for fixed $k$ is {@{the multinomial number $\binom{2n}{k,k,n-k,n-k}$, and each path has probability $4^{-2n}$}@}, and summing over $k=0,\dots,n$ gives {@{the return probability $P(X_{2n}=0)=\frac1{4^{2n}}\sum_{k=0}^n\binom{2n}{k,k,n-k,n-k}$}@}. <!--SR:!2026-05-10,15,290!2026-05-10,15,290!2026-05-06,14,290!2026-05-06,14,290!2026-05-07,15,290-->

> Let $\mathcal{B}_1=\sigma(\{(a,b]:a<b\})$. Show that $\mathcal{B}_1=\mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R})$. In this question, recall that the definition being used for $\mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R})$ is {@{the sigma-algebra generated by open intervals $(a,b)$}@}, and note also that the course often uses $[a,b)$ as an equivalent generating family.
>
> Solution: We prove both inclusions. First, {@{$\mathcal{B}_1\subseteq\mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R})$}@}: since $\mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R})=\sigma(\{(a,b):a<b\})$, it is enough to build each generator $(a,b]$ from open intervals. For $a<b$, $(a,b]=\bigcap_{n=1}^{\infty}(a,b+1/n)$. Each set on the right is {@{open}@}, so it is in $\mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R})$; sigma-algebras are closed under {@{countable intersections}@}, hence $(a,b]\in\mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R})$. Therefore every generator of $\mathcal{B}_1$ is Borel, so $\mathcal{B}_1\subseteq\mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R})$. For the reverse inclusion, it is enough to recover {@{open intervals}@} from the generators of $\mathcal{B}_1$. If $a<b$, then $(a,b)=\bigcup_{n=1}^{\infty}(a,b-1/n]$, where terms with $b-1/n\le a$ are simply {@{empty and harmless}@}. Thus every open interval belongs to $\mathcal{B}_1$, so {@{$\mathcal{B}_1=\mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R})$}@}. The course's family $[a,b)$ is another equivalent generator, but for this proof it is enough to pass back and forth between $(a,b)$ and $(a,b]$. <!--SR:!2026-05-06,14,290!2026-05-09,14,290!2026-05-07,15,290!2026-05-11,16,290!2026-05-06,14,290!2026-05-06,14,290!2026-05-06,14,290-->

> Derive the Poisson distribution from the usual small-time assumptions of a Poisson process.
>
> Solution: Write $P_n(t)=P(N_t=n)$. The small-time assumptions say that over a very short interval $(t,t+h]$ the count behaves like a {@{continuous-time state process that either stays put or jumps up by 1}@}: with probability about $1-\lambda h$ nothing happens, with probability about $\lambda h$ exactly one jump occurs, and the chance of two or more jumps is only $o(h)$. For state 0 this gives $P_0(t+h)=P_0(t)(1-\lambda h)+o(h)$, so {@{$P_0(t)=e^{-\lambda t}$}@}. For $n\ge1$, the only first-order ways to be at $n$ at time $t+h$ are: already at $n$ and no jump, or at $n-1$ and one jump, so $P_n(t+h)=P_n(t)(1-\lambda h)+P_{n-1}(t)(\lambda h)+o(h)$. Solving the resulting recursion gives {@{$P_n(t)=e^{-\lambda t}(\lambda t)^n/n!$}@}. Therefore {@{$N_t\sim\mathrm{Pois}(\lambda t)$}@}. <!--SR:!2026-05-06,14,290!2026-05-09,14,290!2026-05-06,14,290!2026-05-07,15,290-->

> For die-roll events $A=\{6\}$ and $B=\{2,4,6\}$, compute $\sigma(A)$ and $\sigma(B)$, and prove that if $A\subseteq B\subseteq\mathcal{P}(\Omega)$ then $\sigma(A)\subseteq\sigma(B)$.
>
> Solution: With $\Omega=\{1,2,3,4,5,6\}$, the generated sigma-algebras are {@{$\sigma(A)=\{\varnothing,\{6\},\{1,2,3,4,5\},\Omega\}$ and $\sigma(B)=\{\varnothing,\{2,4,6\},\{1,3,5\},\Omega\}$}@}. For monotonicity, if $A\subseteq B$ as set-families then every sigma-algebra containing $B$ also contains $A$, so taking intersections over all such sigma-algebras gives {@{$\sigma(A)\subseteq\sigma(B)$}@}. <!--SR:!2026-05-10,15,290!2026-05-10,15,290-->

> If $X\sim\mathrm{Pois}(12)$, compute $P(X<7)$, $P(X=7)$, and the probability that exactly 2 of the next 3 acres each have fewer than 7 field mice.
>
> Solution: From the Poisson pmf $P(X=k)=e^{-12}12^k/k!$, compute {@{the two numerical probabilities $P(X<7)=\sum_{k=0}^{6}e^{-12}12^k/k!\approx0.0458$ and $P(X=7)=e^{-12}12^7/7!\approx0.0437$}@}. Then set $p=P(X<7)$, model the {@{count across 3 acres as $Y\sim\mathrm{Bin}(3,p)$}@}, and obtain {@{the binomial probability $P(Y=2)=\binom32p^2(1-p)\approx0.0060$}@}. <!--SR:!2026-05-06,14,290!2026-05-06,14,290!2026-05-06,14,290-->

> If an error rate is about $1/1000$ and 10,000 tax forms are inspected, use a Poisson approximation to find the probability that exactly 8 contain errors.
>
> Solution: Start from $X\sim\mathrm{Bin}(10000,0.001)$; because {@{the regime $n$ large, $p$ small, and $np=10$ moderate supports a Poisson approximation with parameter $\lambda=10$}@}, we get {@{the approximation $P(X=8)\approx e^{-10}10^8/8!\approx0.1126$}@}. <!--SR:!2026-05-07,15,290!2026-05-06,14,290-->

> An unfair coin has $P(H)=p$. Toss until the first head and let $Y$ be the total number of tosses. Find the distribution of $Y$, and evaluate $P(2\le Y<5)$ when $p=1/4$.
>
> Solution: The event $Y=k$ means {@{the first $k-1$ tosses are tails and toss $k$ is head, so $P(Y=k)=(1-p)^{k-1}p$ for $k\in\mathbb N$, i.e. $Y$ is geometric}@}. At $p=1/4$, {@{the interval probability is $P(2\le Y<5)=P(Y=2)+P(Y=3)+P(Y=4)=\frac{111}{256}\approx0.4336$}@}. <!--SR:!2026-05-06,14,290!2026-05-06,14,290-->

> A television show contains the word "Smurf" according to a Poisson model with mean 7 in 60 minutes. Compute the following:
>
> - (a) The probability of hearing it at least 3 times in the first 26 minutes.
> - (b) The probability of hearing it zero times in the first 18 minutes.
> - (c) The probability that at least 5 of the next 6 episodes each contain at least 3 occurrences.
>
> Solution:
>
> - (a) Use {@{Poisson rate scaling}@}. For 26 minutes the mean is $\lambda_{26}=7\cdot26/60=91/30$, so {@{the probability is $P(X\ge3)=1-\sum_{k=0}^2e^{-91/30}(91/30)^k/k!\approx0.5842$, i.e. one minus the probabilities of 0, 1, or 2 occurrences}@}.
> - (b) For 18 minutes the mean is $\lambda_{18}=2.1$, hence {@{the zero-count probability is $P(X=0)=e^{-2.1}\approx0.1225$ because a Poisson zero-count probability is $e^{-\lambda}$}@}.
> - (c) For one full episode define {@{the single-episode success probability $p=P(\mathrm{Pois}(7)\ge3)=1-\sum_{k=0}^2e^{-7}7^k/k!\approx0.9704$}@}. Then with {@{$Y\sim\mathrm{Bin}(6,p)$}@}, {@{the event 'at least 5 of 6' gives $P(Y\ge5)=\binom65p^5(1-p)+p^6\approx0.9878$}@}, because {@{'at least 5 of 6' means exactly 5 or exactly 6 successful episodes}@}. <!--SR:!2026-05-06,14,290!2026-05-11,16,290!2026-05-06,14,290!2026-05-09,14,290!2026-05-07,15,290!2026-05-07,15,290!2026-05-07,15,290-->
