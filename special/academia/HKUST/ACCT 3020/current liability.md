# current liability

This note covers the definition of a liability, the classification of [current liability](../../../../general/Current%20liability.md) under IFRS (operating cycle and 12-month criteria), notes payable (short-term and long-term interest-bearing), refinancing and when debt is classified as current versus long-term, income tax payable, and compensated absence (e.g. vacation pay). It is the short-term-liabilities home for ACCT 3020. Provisions and contingencies (liabilities of uncertain timing and amount) are covered in [provisions and contingencies](provisions%20and%20contingencies.md).

## definition of liability

A liability has three essential components under the conceptual framework. First, there must be a __present obligation__: the entity is obliged to transfer resources now, even if the actual payment occurs later. Second, the obligation must __arise from past events__: something that already happened (e.g. borrowing money, purchasing goods on credit) creates the obligation. Third, settlement is expected to require an __outflow of resources__ (e.g. cash or goods) from the entity. Without a past event, there is no present obligation; for example, a claim that "you owe me money" must be supported by a past event (e.g. a loan or purchase) that gave rise to the obligation. In a typical purchase on credit, the past event is the purchase of merchandise; from that day the company has a present obligation to pay the supplier, and the transfer of cash will occur in the future.

## current liability

Under IAS 1.69, a liability is classified as __current__ if __any one__ of four conditions applies: (1) it is expected to be settled within the entity's __normal operating cycle__; (2) it is __held primarily for trading__ purposes; (3) it is due to be settled within __12 months__ after the reporting date; or (4) the entity does __not__ have an __unconditional right to defer settlement for at least 12 months__ after the reporting date (e.g. a callable loan or a loan whose covenant has already been breached). If none of the four applies, the liability is non-current.

In practice, conditions (1) and (3) are most commonly encountered. The operating cycle is the time from purchasing inventory through selling it, collecting cash from customers, and paying suppliers. For operating or working-capital items, the __operating cycle__ criterion is tested first because "current" here means __part of the normal turnover of current assets and current liabilities__, not necessarily "within one calendar year". For example, if the cycle is six months (one month to receive goods, two months to sell, one month to collect cash, two months to pay the supplier), a liability due within that six-month window is current under criterion (1). If the liability is not going to be settled as part of that normal cycle, the entity then applies the __12-month criterion__ (condition 3): is it expected to be settled within 12 months after the reporting date? If yes, it is still current.

The longer-operating-cycle case is important. Suppose a shipbuilder or property developer has an __18-month operating cycle__: from buying materials, through construction, to collecting from the customer and paying suppliers. A liability expected to be settled in __15 months__ can still be __current__ if it is part of that normal 18-month operating cycle, because current classification is based on normal operating turnover in that case. By contrast, a liability that is __not__ part of the operating cycle is judged by the 12-month rule instead. So the key question is not just "Is it within 12 months?" but first "Is it part of the normal operating cycle?"

This is why introductory and intermediate treatments can sound different. Principles courses often emphasize the easy memory rule "within 12 months = current." Intermediate accounting adds the more precise working-capital logic: operating-cycle items are current even when the cycle is longer than 12 months, and the 12-month test is the fallback for obligations outside that operating-cycle turnover.

## notes payable

Notes payable are more formal than ordinary supplier balances because they are documented by a note that fixes the principal amount, maturity date, and sometimes an explicit interest rate. In this note, the topic is split into three recurring patterns: short-term interest-bearing borrowing, conversion of an overdue account payable into a note, and implicit-interest notes issued at a discount. The common accounting idea is that once the note exists, the entity must track not only the principal obligation but also the interest mechanism built into the contract, whether that interest is stated directly or hidden in the discount.

### accounts payable versus notes payable

Accounts payable typically involve amounts owed to suppliers with __no stated interest__ and short credit terms (e.g. 30 days). Notes payable arise from a __separate contract__ (a note) that states the principal, interest rate, and due date. A supplier may require the conversion of an account payable into a note payable (with interest) if the amount is owed for too long; the journal entry is Dr _Accounts payable_, Cr _Notes payable_.

### short-term interest-bearing notes

When the entity borrows cash and signs a short-term interest-bearing note, it records Dr _Cash_, Cr _Notes payable_ at the borrowing date. The stated interest rate is usually an __annual__ rate; interest for a fraction of a year is prorated (e.g. principal × rate × 4/12 for four months). Under the __accrual__ concept, interest expense is recognised in the period it is incurred. At year-end, the entity records Dr _Interest expense_, Cr _Interest payable_ for the accrued interest even if cash is not yet paid. When the note is settled at the due date, the entity records Dr _Notes payable_, Dr _Interest payable_, Cr _Cash_.

### implicit-interest notes (notes issued at a discount)

Some notes are issued at a discount: the entity receives less cash than the amount it will repay at maturity. No separate interest rate is stated; instead, the difference between the cash received and the maturity amount is __implicit (embedded) interest__. For example, the entity receives 68&nbsp;400 and must repay 75&nbsp;600 at the end of 12 months; the 7&nbsp;200 difference is interest over the 12 months. (The same concept applies to longer-term notes and zero-interest-bearing bonds; see [non-current liabilities](non-current%20liabilities.md#zero-interest-bearing-notes-and-implicit-interest-rate) for an extended treatment.) At each reporting date, interest expense is allocated over the term (e.g. 7&nbsp;200 × 3/12 for three months). Importantly, this accrual is credited to __Notes payable__, not _Interest payable_, so that the carrying amount of the note increases toward the maturity amount (75&nbsp;600). If it were credited to _Interest payable_, the note would remain at 68&nbsp;400 and would not reach the amount due at maturity. Subsequent periods continue to record Dr _Interest expense_, Cr _Notes payable_ until the note reaches the maturity amount; at the due date, Dr _Notes payable_ 75&nbsp;600, Cr _Cash_ 75&nbsp;600.

## current maturities of long-term debt

## refinancing and classification as current or long-term

Debt that would otherwise be current (e.g. due shortly after the reporting date) may be __excluded from current liabilities__ if two conditions are __both__ met: (1) the entity expects to refinance the obligation on a long-term basis (e.g. extend the due date or replace it with long-term financing), and (2) the entity has an __unconditional__ right to defer settlement __for at least 12 months after the reporting date__. If the refinancing agreement contains __covenants__ or conditions (e.g. the entity must maintain a current ratio above 1, or a certain debt ratio, or minimum cash), the right to defer is __conditional__. In that case, the liability remains classified as __current__, because the lender could demand immediate repayment if the condition is breached. Classification matters: moving a large amount from long-term to current can sharply reduce the current ratio and trigger liquidity concerns, supplier demands, or loan covenants.

The __timing__ of refinancing also matters. If the refinancing (e.g. a new loan agreement extending the due date) is __finalised before the balance sheet date__, the entity had the right to defer at the reporting date and may classify the obligation as long-term. If the refinancing is completed __after__ the balance sheet date (e.g. one month later), then at the reporting date the entity did not yet have the right to defer; the obligation is still classified as current at year-end.

## compensated absence

Employee benefits such as __vacation pay__ or paid leave that can be converted into cash are a form of __compensated absence__. Some benefits require a __vesting__ period: the employee must work for a required period (e.g. one year) before being entitled to the benefit. Once the vesting period has passed, the employee has a __vested right__ to the benefit. The entity recognises an expense and a liability when the right is earned, even if payment is in a later period.

Example: employees earn two weeks of paid vacation at the beginning of their second year; the company considers the vacation pay to be earned during the first year. At the end of the first year, BrightWave Services Ltd. has employees who have earned 24 unused vacation weeks at 520 per week. The entity records Dr _Salaries and wages expense_ 12&nbsp;480 (24 × 520), Cr _Wages payable_ 12&nbsp;480. In the next year when employees take the vacation and are paid, the wage rate may have increased (e.g. to 590 per week). The entity pays 24 × 590 and records Dr _Wages payable_ 12&nbsp;480, Dr _Salaries and wages expense_ (for the difference to current rate), Cr _Cash_. The liability is settled and the remainder is expense of the period in which payment occurs.

## customer advances and deposits

__Customer advances__ (advance payments) are cash received __before__ the entity satisfies the related performance obligation (e.g. a deposit for an order not yet delivered). ## unearned revenues and gift card breakage

__Unearned revenue__ arises when cash is received before the performance obligation is satisfied. Until the entity performs, the cash is a liability: Dr _Cash_, Cr _Unearned revenue_. As performance occurs, revenue is recognised: Dr _Unearned revenue_, Cr _Revenue_.

__Gift cards__ are a common source of current liabilities. At sale: Dr _Cash_, Cr _Unearned revenue_ (gift card liability). At redemption: Dr _Unearned revenue_, Cr _Revenue_. The complication is __breakage__—the portion of gift cards that customers never redeem. Under IFRS 15, if the entity expects breakage it recognises it as revenue proportionately to the pattern of redemptions (__proportionate method__). Only if the entity cannot reliably estimate breakage does it use the __remote method__: recognise breakage revenue only when redemption becomes remote.

__Proportionate breakage formula__: Breakage revenue per period = Total expected breakage × (Redemptions to date ÷ Total expected redemptions).
