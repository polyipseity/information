# ESG reporting

This note summarises the ESG (environmental, social, and governance) reporting material in ACCT 3020, focusing on Hong Kong listed companies and the course's exam emphasis.

<!-- check: ignore-next-line[header_style]: proper noun -->
## ESG overview

ESG stands for __environmental__, __social__, and __governance__. It grew out of broader sustainability initiatives (e.g. United Nations sustainable development goals) and is now embedded in capital markets: investors, regulators, and other stakeholders expect companies to report how they manage key ESG issues alongside traditional financial information.

Environmental topics include greenhouse gas emissions, energy use and efficiency, waste and recycling, and climate-change strategies (e.g. reducing carbon footprint). Social topics include fair and living wages, working conditions, occupational safety and health, training and development, diversity and inclusion, and how the firm engages with communities and an ageing workforce. Governance topics include the composition and effectiveness of the board, risk management, internal controls, and especially anti–corruption and anti–bribery policies and procedures.

The course focuses on understanding what ESG is, why it matters for risk and valuation, and how it is reported, rather than detailed calculation of emission factors.

## why ESG matters

ESG matters because ignoring it creates real economic risks. On the environmental side, climate change has led to more frequent and severe extreme weather (e.g. stronger typhoons, heavy rainfall and flooding), which can physically damage assets and interrupt operations. On the social side, trends such as population ageing and high accident or fatality rates in certain industries affect workforce availability, productivity, and legal liabilities. On the governance side, corruption or bribery cases can lead to prosecution (e.g. ICAC cases in Hong Kong), large fines, and long‑lasting reputational damage.

Investors increasingly look at ESG scores or ratings when deciding which companies to invest in: firms with poor ESG performance may face higher cost of capital or even be excluded from some investment mandates. Regulators also expect better transparency to combat "greenwashing" (overstating ESG performance) and to ensure markets properly price ESG‑related risks.

## types of ESG risk

The course highlights four broad categories of ESG‑related risk:

- __Reputation risk__: If a company repeatedly violates ESG expectations (e.g. pollution incidents, labour abuses, corruption scandals), customers may boycott products and investors may sell the shares, depressing the share price.
- __Regulatory and reporting risk__: Companies that misstate or "greenwash" ESG performance, or fail to meet disclosure requirements, face enforcement actions, fines, and forced restatements.
- __Physical risk__: Environmental change (e.g. more intense storms or flooding) can physically damage assets, disrupt supply chains, and increase insurance and operating costs.
- __Transition risk__: As economies shift towards low‑carbon and more sustainable business models, some assets or technologies may become obsolete or restricted, leading to impairments or stranded assets (e.g. bans on high‑emission equipment, tighter building codes).

### mandatory versus comply-or-explain

- __Mandatory disclosure__: items that must be disclosed in the ESG or sustainability section of the annual report; companies cannot opt out.
- __"Comply or explain" disclosure__: recommended disclosures where companies are expected either to comply or to explain clearly why they have not yet done so.

### board statement and board oversight

A key mandatory element is a __board statement__ on ESG. This statement should:

1. Acknowledge that the board is responsible for oversight of ESG issues.
2. Describe the board's approach, strategy, and processes for managing ESG risks and opportunities (e.g. committees, policies, and how ESG is integrated into decision‑making).
3. Explain how the board reviewed ESG performance over the year (e.g. frequency of ESG discussions, key topics considered, actions taken).

## reporting principles: materiality, KPIs, and consistency

ESG reporting in Hong Kong follows several reporting principles, especially:

- __Materiality__: focus on the ESG issues that are important to both the business and its stakeholders. Companies use a __materiality matrix__ with two axes: impact on the business (low to high) and impact on stakeholders (low to high). Issues in the high–high quadrant are prioritised in disclosures.
- __Quantitative information (KPIs)__: companies must provide quantitative key performance indicators (KPIs), not just vague statements. Examples include total greenhouse gas emissions, energy consumption, staff training hours, accident and fatality rates, and number of corruption incidents.
- __Consistency__: the company should use consistent KPIs and measurement methods over time so that trends can be analysed. If methods or KPIs change, the company should explain the change and, where possible, provide comparatives.

## applying the materiality matrix

A __materiality matrix__ helps management rank ESG topics by combining two viewpoints:

- how strongly the issue affects the company's business model, operations, cost, risk, or value creation; and
- how important the issue is to key stakeholders such as investors, regulators, employees, customers, and communities.

Consider a listed logistics company. A simplified materiality assessment might look like this:

- __high business impact, high stakeholder concern__: fuel efficiency, greenhouse gas emissions, driver safety, and anti-corruption in procurement;
- __high business impact, lower stakeholder concern__: warehouse automation resilience;
- __lower business impact, high stakeholder concern__: local community volunteering hours; and
- __lower business impact, lower stakeholder concern__: minor office-paper consumption.

The first group belongs in the priority disclosure area because it is material on both axes. The others may still be disclosed, but with less emphasis.

The important exam point is that materiality is __entity-specific__. Data privacy may be central for a bank or platform business, while water consumption may be much more central for a beverage producer than for a software firm.

## materiality and boundary

Two additional ideas in ESG reporting are __materiality__ (what to report) and __boundary__ (which parts of the business are covered):

- The materiality assessment identifies which ESG topics are significant enough to report in detail (e.g. data privacy and cybersecurity might be highly material for an IT company, while some environmental topics are less material).
- The boundary defines which operations, business units, or geographic regions are included in the ESG data (e.g. only major subsidiaries in Hong Kong, Mainland China, and the US). Without a clear boundary, readers may wrongly assume the data covers the entire global group.

Clear disclosure of both material topics and reporting boundary helps users understand what the ESG numbers actually cover and how comparable they are over time.

## current developments: HKFRS S1 and HKFRS S2

The course also points to the move from stand-alone ESG reporting toward broader __sustainability-related financial disclosures__.

Two current standards matter in the slides:

- __HKFRS S1__: general requirements for disclosure of sustainability-related financial information; and
- __HKFRS S2__: climate-related disclosures.

The big idea is integration. Rather than treating sustainability reporting as a separate marketing-style appendix, these standards push companies to explain sustainability-related __risks and opportunities__ in a way that connects to governance, strategy, risk management, and financial effects.

HKFRS S2 is climate-specific, so it focuses on issues such as:

- governance over climate matters;
- climate-related strategy;
- physical and transition risks;
- targets and metrics such as emissions; and
- how climate issues may affect cash flows, financing, and asset values.

For exam purposes, the important takeaway is that ESG disclosure in Hong Kong is moving toward __more structured, decision-useful, and financially connected__ reporting, with climate disclosure receiving especially strong emphasis.
