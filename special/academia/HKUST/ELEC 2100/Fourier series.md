## spectrum concept and frequency-domain viewpoint

The opening frequency-domain discussion is not yet about coefficient formulas.  It first asks why one would want a frequency-domain picture at all.  The answer is that many physical systems are naturally selective in frequency: the human vocal tract, animal hearing systems, communication channels, resonators, and musical instruments do not respond equally to every oscillation rate.  A time waveform shows how a signal evolves, but a spectrum shows which oscillatory components are present and how strongly they contribute.

This is why the course treats spectrum as a representation rather than as a separate signal.  A periodic waveform in time can be redescribed as a collection of spectral lines in frequency.  The time-domain picture emphasizes shape and switching; the frequency-domain picture emphasizes harmonic content, dominant bands, and phase relationships.  Neither picture is more "real" than the other.  They are two coordinate systems for the same signal.

For periodic signals the spectral structure is discrete.  If the signal has period $T$, then its basic angular frequency is $\omega_0=2\pi/T$, and every Fourier-series component appears at an integer multiple $k\omega_0$.  The spectrum is therefore a line spectrum rather than a continuous curve.  This is the frequency-domain signature of exact repetition in time: periodicity forces the allowed frequencies onto a harmonic lattice.

In the exponential Fourier-series notation $f(t)=\sum_{k=-\infty}^{\infty}F_k e^{jk\omega_0 t}$, each coefficient $F_k$ corresponds in the Fourier-transform picture to the impulse $2\pi F_k\,\delta(\omega-k\omega_0)$, where $\omega_0=2\pi/T$.  So the coefficient itself is not yet the transform impulse height; the actual spectral line weight in angular frequency is multiplied by $2\pi$.  Equivalently, the full transform of the periodic signal is $F(\omega)=2\pi\sum_{k=-\infty}^{\infty}F_k\delta(\omega-k\omega_0)$.

This also clarifies plotting conventions for periodic signals.  Two standard representations are commonly used, and they differ in axis choice and $2\pi$ placement.

__Fourier-series coefficient plot:__ Plot $F_k$ on the y-axis and harmonic number $k$ on the x-axis.  Each point represents one Fourier coefficient directly, without any $2\pi$ factors.  This is the simplest discrete representation.

__Fourier-transform-style line spectrum:__ Plot the magnitude $|2\pi F_k|$ on the y-axis and angular frequency $\omega=k\omega_0$ on the x-axis.  Each spectral line sits at a frequency $\omega$ and has weight $2\pi F_k\,\delta(\omega-k\omega_0)$ (understood as a Dirac impulse).  In this style, the $2\pi$ is included in the line weight from the start, and you are viewing the spectrum in the same frequency-domain style used for aperiodic signals via the Fourier transform.  The full transform of the periodic signal is then $F(\omega)=2\pi\sum_{k=-\infty}^{\infty}F_k\delta(\omega-k\omega_0)$.

__Note on alternative Fourier-series representation via Dirac deltas:__ It is also possible (though not standard) to plot a Fourier series using the Fourier-transform impulse notation.  Simply replace each coefficient $F_k$ with the Dirac impulse $F_k\,\delta(\omega-k\omega_0)$ (without the extra $2\pi$ factor that would appear if you were converting the full time-domain signal's transform).  This requires remembering to multiply accumulated line weights by $2\pi$ if you later want to use those weights in transform-style calculations.  Most engineering practice avoids this style for Fourier-series analysis because it adds notational confusion; the harmonic-index plot (y-axis: $F_k$, x-axis: $k$) is preferred.

__Phase plotting:__ For both representations, phase is plotted similarly.  In the coefficient plot, show $\angle F_k$ versus $k$.  In the frequency plot, show $\angle(2\pi F_k)$ or equivalently $\angle F_k$ versus $\omega=k\omega_0$ (the $2\pi$ factor does not change the angle).  Both styles convey the same phase information; only the axis labeling differs.

The musical examples in the lecture are pedagogically important.  A pure tone is close to one dominant sinusoid, while a richer musical note contains a fundamental together with harmonics.  The fundamental sets the perceived pitch; the harmonic amplitudes and phases shape the timbre.  This gives an immediate physical interpretation of why two instruments can play the same note but still sound different.

Amplitude and phase should also be kept conceptually distinct.  The amplitude spectrum records how large each harmonic contribution is.  The phase spectrum records how each harmonic is shifted relative to a reference cosine or complex exponential.  Amplitude often gives the coarse qualitative shape of a waveform, but phase alignment matters for the actual time-domain reconstruction.  Two spectra with the same magnitudes but different phases can produce very different waveforms.

A final comparison helps organize the later material.  Periodic signals lead to discrete spectral lines and are handled naturally by Fourier series.  Aperiodic signals lead to continuous spectra and are handled naturally by the Fourier transform.  The second note builds that bridge explicitly; this note focuses on the periodic side.

## orthogonal decomposition, completeness, and Parseval's theorem

The mathematical backbone behind Fourier series is orthogonal decomposition.  The lecture intentionally compares it with vectors in Euclidean space: if one wants the "amount" of a vector along a direction, one projects onto an orthogonal basis direction.  Signal decomposition follows the same idea, except the vectors are now functions and the dot product is replaced by an integral-based inner product.  Over one period or over a chosen interval of length $T$, a standard inner product is $\langle f,g\rangle=\frac{1}{T}\int_{t_0}^{t_0+T} f(t)g^*(t)\,dt$.

Two functions are orthogonal when this inner product is zero.  Orthogonality matters because it removes interference between basis directions.  If the basis functions do not overlap under the inner product, then extracting one coefficient does not contaminate another.  In modern language, this is already Hilbert-space geometry in miniature: one is working inside an inner-product space of signals, typically a subspace of $L^2[0,T]$, and orthogonal basis functions play the role of perpendicular coordinate axes.

Suppose one approximates a signal by an orthogonal family $\{g_r(t)\}$ as $f(t)\approx \sum_r c_r g_r(t)$.  The error is $\varepsilon(t)=f(t)-\sum_r c_r g_r(t)$, and the mean square error is proportional to $\int |\varepsilon(t)|^2 dt$.  Minimizing that error with respect to one coefficient $c_m$ gives the projection formula $c_m=\frac{\langle f,g_m\rangle}{\langle g_m,g_m\rangle}$.  If the basis is normalized, the denominator is $1$ and the coefficient is just the inner product itself.  This is the function-space analogue of orthogonal vector projection.

The real periodic Fourier basis consists of $1$, $\cos(k\omega_0 t)$, and $\sin(k\omega_0 t)$ for $k\ge 1$.  It is orthogonal over any full period, but it is not orthonormal as written, because $\int_0^T 1\cdot 1\,dt=T$, $\int_0^T \cos^2(k\omega_0 t)dt=T/2$, and $\int_0^T \sin^2(k\omega_0 t)dt=T/2$ rather than $1$.  The constants, cosines, and sines should be thought of as distinct perpendicular directions in signal space: the DC axis, the cosine axis at each harmonic, and the sine axis at each harmonic.

The standard trigonometric orthogonality proof is brief but important.  Over a full period $[0,T]$ with $\omega_0=2\pi/T$, product-to-sum identities give $\sin(n\omega_0 t)\sin(m\omega_0 t)=\frac12\big[\cos((n-m)\omega_0 t)-\cos((n+m)\omega_0 t)\big]$, $\cos(n\omega_0 t)\cos(m\omega_0 t)=\frac12\big[\cos((n-m)\omega_0 t)+\cos((n+m)\omega_0 t)\big]$, and $\sin(n\omega_0 t)\cos(m\omega_0 t)=\frac12\big[\sin((n+m)\omega_0 t)+\sin((n-m)\omega_0 t)\big]$.  When $n\neq m$, every resulting sine or cosine completes an integer number of cycles over $[0,T]$, so the integral is $0$.  When $n=m$, the same formulas reduce to the familiar $\sin^2$ or $\cos^2$ averages, giving $\int_0^T \sin^2(n\omega_0 t)dt=T/2$ and $\int_0^T \cos^2(n\omega_0 t)dt=T/2$.

The complex periodic Fourier basis consists of $e^{jk\omega_0 t}$ for all integers $k$.  It is orthogonal because $\int_0^T e^{jn\omega_0 t}\,e^{-jm\omega_0 t}dt=\int_0^T e^{j(n-m)\omega_0 t}dt$, which equals $T$ when $n=m$ and equals $0$ when $n\neq m$.  The reason the off-diagonal case vanishes is that $e^{j(n-m)\omega_0 T}=e^{j2\pi(n-m)}=1$, so the antiderivative produces identical endpoint values and their difference cancels.

The real and complex bases describe the same geometry in two styles.  The real basis is visually explicit: one sees cosine and sine as separate coordinates.  The complex basis is algebraically compressed: one indexed family handles positive frequencies, negative frequencies, and phase at once.  In this sense the complex basis is the cleaner language for operator algebra, while the real basis is often the cleaner language for physical interpretation.

The Fourier basis is important because it is not just orthogonal but complete.  Completeness means there is no nonzero signal in the intended class that is orthogonal to every basis function.  In practical course language, a complete real orthogonal space means the real-valued signal class has enough mutually orthogonal basis functions that every admissible real periodic signal can be expanded in them.  Likewise, a complete complex orthogonal space means the complex-valued signal class has enough complex orthogonal basis functions that every admissible complex periodic signal can be expanded in them.  In Hilbert-space language, the orthogonal family spans the relevant subspace, so every signal can be resolved into components along those basis directions.

The Riesz-representation hint should be kept modest here.  The main lesson is not the theorem statement itself, but why the complex inner product uses conjugation.  Without the conjugate in $\langle f,g\rangle=\frac{1}{T}\int f(t)g^*(t)dt$, one would lose the positive quantity $\langle f,f\rangle=\frac{1}{T}\int |f(t)|^2dt$, so lengths and energies would no longer behave correctly.  The conjugate is what makes the complex inner product produce real nonnegative norms and makes coefficient extraction agree with geometric projection in complex signal space.

Parseval's theorem is the energy/power bookkeeping law associated with complete orthogonal expansions.  For a periodic signal it states that the average power in time equals the total power contributed by the Fourier-series coefficients.  In trigonometric form, $P=\frac{1}{T}\int_{t_0}^{t_0+T}|f(t)|^2dt=a_0^2+\frac12\sum_{k=1}^{\infty}(a_k^2+b_k^2)$, and in exponential form, $P=\sum_{k=-\infty}^{\infty}|F_k|^2$.  The interpretation is extremely useful: orthogonality removes cross terms, so the total average power becomes the sum of the separate harmonic powers.  Parseval is therefore not just an identity to memorize; it is the quantitative statement that orthogonal harmonic directions do not interfere in the power calculation.

Among the common forms, the exponential Parseval formula is the most natural because it adds the actual two-sided spectral-line powers $|F_k|^2$ directly.  The trigonometric and harmonic forms are folded real descriptions: they avoid explicit negative-frequency bookkeeping by combining the $+k$ and $-k$ lines into one real harmonic.  The factor $1/2$ in the trigonometric or harmonic form therefore deserves explicit intuition.  In the exponential basis, a nonzero real harmonic actually appears as a pair of conjugate spectral lines at $+k\omega_0$ and $-k\omega_0$.  If the real harmonic has cosine-phase amplitude $c_k$, then each complex line has magnitude $|F_k|=|F_{-k}|=c_k/2$.  Their total power is therefore $|F_k|^2+|F_{-k}|^2=(c_k/2)^2+(c_k/2)^2=c_k^2/2$.  Since $c_k^2=a_k^2+b_k^2$, the corresponding trigonometric contribution is $(a_k^2+b_k^2)/2$.  So the factor $1/2$ appears because one real nonzero harmonic is the folded version of two separate equal-power complex spectral lines.  Folding the pair doubles the amplitude description from $|F_k|$ to $c_k=2|F_k|$; if one squared that doubled amplitude naively, the result would overcount the true two-line power by a factor of $2$, so the explicit factor $1/2$ is the correction that restores the real power total.

## trigonometric Fourier series and harmonic form

For a real periodic signal of period $T$, the trigonometric Fourier series is written as $f(t)=a_0+\sum_{k=1}^{\infty}\big[a_k\cos(k\omega_0 t)+b_k\sin(k\omega_0 t)\big]$ with $\omega_0=\frac{2\pi}{T}$.

This formula says that a periodic waveform can be built from one DC term together with cosine and sine harmonics at integer multiples of the fundamental frequency.  Each harmonic adds one more layer of shape refinement.

The coefficient formulas come directly from orthogonality: $a_0=\frac{1}{T}\int_{t_0}^{t_0+T} f(t)\,dt$, $a_k=\frac{2}{T}\int_{t_0}^{t_0+T} f(t)\cos(k\omega_0 t)\,dt$, and $b_k=\frac{2}{T}\int_{t_0}^{t_0+T} f(t)\sin(k\omega_0 t)\,dt$.

The course presents these as projection coefficients: multiply by the target basis function, integrate over one full period, and use orthogonality to isolate the desired term.

The $a_0$ term is special in several deep ways.  First, it is the average or DC component, so it measures vertical offset rather than oscillation.  Second, it corresponds to the zero-frequency basis function $1$, and zero frequency is its own negative, unlike every nonzero harmonic $\pm k\omega_0$.  The clean way to remember this is that the fully symmetric real orthogonal description really wants frequencies $\ldots,-2\omega_0,-\omega_0,0,\omega_0,2\omega_0,\ldots$.  But for a real signal, the nonzero negative-frequency content collapses into the matching positive-frequency content because the $\pm k\omega_0$ pair combines into one real cosine-sine harmonic.  That is why in a one-sided or trigonometric description the nonzero harmonics are effectively doubled or merged, whereas the DC term is not doubled: there is no separate negative-frequency partner at $0$.  So $a_0$ stands alone, while every nonzero real harmonic secretly comes from a two-sided pair.

The lecture also states Dirichlet-type conditions as the usual sufficient conditions for convergence to the intended periodic signal.  They should be read as anti-pathology conditions: they rule out signals that are too singular, too jumpy, or too wildly oscillatory within one period.  A standard engineering summary over one period $[t_0,t_0+T]$ is: $f$ should be absolutely integrable, it should have only finitely many finite discontinuities, and it should have only finitely many local maxima and minima.  These conditions are sufficient rather than necessary, but they cover the common piecewise smooth periodic waveforms used in signals and systems.

Condition 1 is absolute integrability over one period: $\int_{t_0}^{t_0+T}|f(t)|\,dt<\infty$.  Intuitively, one period must contain finite total area in absolute value, so the coefficient integrals are well-defined and not destroyed by a non-integrable singularity.  A standard satisfying example is any bounded piecewise-continuous waveform such as a square wave, triangular wave, or centered rectangular pulse train, because bounded height on a finite interval automatically gives finite area.  A standard counterexample is the period-1 extension of $f(t)=1/t$ on $0<t\le 1$: near $t=0$ the area blows up logarithmically because $\int_0^1 \frac{1}{t}dt=\infty$, so the absolute-integrability condition fails even before one asks about jumps or extrema.

Condition 2 is that within one period the number of discontinuity points must be finite, and in the usual textbook form those discontinuities should be finite jumps rather than essential blow-ups.  Intuitively, the waveform is allowed to jump a few times, as a square wave does, but it is not allowed to keep creating new jump edges infinitely often inside one finite period.  A satisfying example is a rectangular wave: in each period it is constant on a few subintervals and has only finitely many jumps, so Fourier coefficients can still encode the waveform cleanly.  A counterexample, matching the lecture's staircase picture, is a period-8 waveform whose steps keep halving in both width and height, for example heights $1,1/2,1/4,\ldots$ on intervals $(0,4],(4,6],(6,7],(7,7.5],\ldots$.  Its total area in one period stays finite, but its discontinuity points $4,6,7,7.5,\ldots$ are infinite in number and accumulate, so Condition 2 fails.

Condition 3 is that within one period the number of local maxima and minima must be finite.  This condition rules out infinite oscillation packed into a finite interval.  The key point is that a signal may be continuous and absolutely integrable yet still oscillate so rapidly near one point that its turning points become infinite in number.  A satisfying example is a sinusoid such as $f(t)=\sin(2\pi t/T)$, which has one maximum and one minimum per period.  A standard counterexample is the period-1 extension of $f(t)=t\sin(2\pi/t)$ on $0<t\le 1$ together with the natural limiting value $f(0)=0$.  This signal remains bounded and absolutely integrable because $|t\sin(2\pi/t)|\le t$, but it oscillates infinitely often as $t\to 0^+$, so it has infinitely many local maxima and minima accumulating near the origin.

Together, the three conditions exclude three different failure modes: non-integrable singularity, infinitely many jumps, and infinitely many oscillations.  That is the easiest way to remember them.  Condition 1 controls area, Condition 2 controls jump structure, and Condition 3 controls oscillatory structure.

Under those conditions, the Fourier series converges pointwise in a specific way.  If $f$ is continuous at $t_0$, then $S_N(t_0)\to f(t_0)$ as the number of harmonics $N\to\infty$.  If $f$ has a jump at $t_0$, then the Fourier series converges to $\frac{f(t_0^-)+f(t_0^+)}{2}$ rather than to either one-sided limit alone.  So the convergence law is not just "the series converges"; it is a rule that depends on the local regularity of the waveform.

The midpoint rule can also be understood heuristically.  A Fourier series is built from globally smooth sinusoids, so near a jump it cannot reproduce a one-sided discontinuous value exactly with finite harmonics.  Instead, the left and right oscillatory approximations balance each other, and the symmetric kernel behind Fourier partial sums averages the two sides.  That is why the limiting value at a jump is the midpoint rather than the left or right endpoint alone.  For example, if an ideal square wave jumps from $-1$ to $1$ at some edge, the Fourier series converges there to $( -1+1 )/2=0$, which is neither plateau value but exactly the midpoint demanded by the convergence theorem.

The trigonometric form can be merged into a cosine-phase or harmonic form: $a_k\cos(k\omega_0 t)+b_k\sin(k\omega_0 t)=c_k\cos(k\omega_0 t+\phi_k)$, where $c_k=\sqrt{a_k^2+b_k^2}$ and $\phi_k=\operatorname{atan2}(-b_k,a_k)$.  Equivalently, the phase is defined so that $a_k=c_k\cos\phi_k$ and $b_k=-c_k\sin\phi_k$.  This merged form is useful because it puts each harmonic into the physically intuitive language of amplitude plus phase.

There is also a clean unit-circle interpretation.  Once $c_k\neq 0$, the normalized pair $\big(a_k/c_k,-b_k/c_k\big)$ lies on the unit circle because $\big(a_k/c_k\big)^2+\big(-b_k/c_k\big)^2=1$.  So $\phi_k$ is literally the angle of the harmonic phasor, while $c_k$ is the phasor length.  In other words, $a_k$ and $b_k$ are rectangular coordinates, whereas $c_k$ and $\phi_k$ are polar coordinates for the same harmonic component.

The harmonic form explains the difference between coefficient extraction and physical interpretation.  The orthogonal-projection formulas are the mathematically clean way to compute coefficients.  The cosine-phase form is often the physically clean way to explain what those coefficients mean: each harmonic has a size $c_k$, a frequency $k\omega_0$, and a phase shift $\phi_k$.

## exponential Fourier series, symmetry, and discrete spectra

The exponential Fourier series rewrites the same periodic signal as $f(t)=\sum_{k=-\infty}^{\infty}F_k e^{jk\omega_0 t}$, with coefficients $F_k=\frac{1}{T}\int_{t_0}^{t_0+T} f(t)e^{-jk\omega_0 t}\,dt$.

This form is mathematically powerful because one family $e^{jk\omega_0 t}$ handles positive frequencies, negative frequencies, and phase information in one stroke.

It is also the cleanest place to state the bridge to the Fourier transform.  If the signal is periodic with period $T$, then the Fourier-transform representation is $F(\omega)=2\pi\sum_{k=-\infty}^{\infty}F_k\delta(\omega-k\omega_0)$ with $\omega_0=2\pi/T$.  So each coefficient $F_k$ becomes a Dirac impulse line at the harmonic frequency $k\omega_0$, and the actual impulse weight is $2\pi F_k$.

For a real signal, the trigonometric and exponential forms carry the same information.  The conversion for $k>0$ is $F_k=\frac{a_k-jb_k}{2}$, $F_{-k}=\frac{a_k+jb_k}{2}$, and $F_0=a_0$.  This can be remembered directly from Euler's formulas $\cos(k\omega_0 t)=\frac{e^{jk\omega_0 t}+e^{-jk\omega_0 t}}{2}$ and $\sin(k\omega_0 t)=\frac{e^{jk\omega_0 t}-e^{-jk\omega_0 t}}{2j}$: substituting those into the trigonometric series automatically groups the coefficient of $e^{jk\omega_0 t}$ into $\frac{a_k-jb_k}{2}$ and the coefficient of $e^{-jk\omega_0 t}$ into $\frac{a_k+jb_k}{2}$.  For a real signal one also has $F_{-k}=F_k^*$, and that conjugate relation is the quickest way to recover $a_k$ and $b_k$: adding the pair cancels the imaginary sine part and keeps the cosine part, so $a_k=F_k+F_{-k}=F_k+F_k^*=2\operatorname{Re}F_k$, while subtracting the pair isolates the sine contribution, so $b_k=j(F_k-F_{-k})=j(F_k-F_k^*)=-2\operatorname{Im}F_k$.

The benchmark single-harmonic examples are worth fixing in memory because they make the coefficient-to-line-spectrum bridge concrete.  For period $T_0=2\pi/\omega_0$, the cosine $\cos(\omega_0 t)$ has only $F_1=F_{-1}=1/2$ and all other coefficients zero, so its Fourier-transform-style periodic spectrum is $\pi\bigl[\delta(\omega-\omega_0)+\delta(\omega+\omega_0)\bigr]$.  Likewise, $\sin(\omega_0 t)$ has only $F_1=1/(2j)$ and $F_{-1}=-1/(2j)$, so its Fourier-transform-style periodic spectrum is $\frac{\pi}{j}\bigl[\delta(\omega-\omega_0)-\delta(\omega+\omega_0)\bigr]$.  These examples are the cleanest reminder that Fourier-series coefficients are indexed by $k$, while the Fourier-transform-style line spectrum is indexed by $\omega$ and carries the extra factor $2\pi$ in the Dirac line weight.

Equivalently, if $F_k=|F_k|e^{j\angle F_k}$, then the corresponding cosine-phase amplitude is $c_k=2|F_k|$ and the phase is inherited from the complex coefficient.

Negative frequencies deserve explicit interpretation.  For a real physical waveform, the negative-frequency lines are not separate extra oscillators hiding in nature.  They are the conjugate partners required by the complex-exponential representation so that the final reconstructed signal is real.  The useful memory rule is: __two-sided spectra are algebraically natural; one-sided spectra are often visually convenient__.  For a real signal, the pair $F_k$ and $F_{-k}$ carries the same real waveform information in conjugate-symmetric form.

When such a periodic spectrum is drawn as a magnitude or phase diagram, one should remember which object is being sketched.  The most common engineering sketch is the Fourier-transform-style line spectrum, so the impulses at $\omega=\pm k\omega_0$ have weights $2\pi F_{\pm k}$.  Because the factor $2\pi$ is a positive real scalar, it scales the line magnitudes by $2\pi$ but does not change the phases.  So the phase diagram may still be read directly from the angles of $F_k$, while the magnitude diagram should be interpreted as showing $|2\pi F_k|$ if one is following the transform convention.

This is also why the single-sided spectrum only makes sense for real signals.  A real signal satisfies $F_{-k}=F_k^*$, so the two-sided spectrum is conjugate symmetric.  Therefore one may fold the negative side onto the positive side.  For the magnitude spectrum, the nonzero negative side simply duplicates the positive side, so a single-sided magnitude spectrum doubles the nonzero positive lines but leaves the DC line unchanged.  For the phase spectrum, the negative side is the sign-negated mirror of the positive side because $\angle F_{-k}=-\angle F_k$, so folding also keeps only the positive-frequency phase data together with the undoubled DC term.

Symmetry in the time domain translates into major coefficient shortcuts.  Here $T$ is the full fundamental period of the signal, so $T/2$ means a shift by exactly half of one cycle.  If $f(t)$ is even, all sine coefficients vanish, so $b_k=0$, and the exponential coefficients are real and even.  The recall intuition is that an even waveform is mirror-symmetric about the vertical axis, whereas sine is odd, so the left and right contributions cancel in the sine projection integral.  If $f(t)$ is odd, the DC term and cosine coefficients vanish, so $a_0=a_k=0$, and the exponential coefficients are purely imaginary and odd.  The recall intuition is that an odd waveform has built-in sign reversal, so its positive and negative halves balance to zero on average, while cosine is even and therefore cannot match that sign pattern in projection.

If the signal has half-wave antisymmetry, meaning $f(t+T/2)=-f(t)$, then the DC component is zero and only odd harmonics remain.  The intuition is that after half a period the waveform flips sign, so an admissible harmonic must also pick up a minus sign under a $T/2$ shift.  Odd harmonics do that; even harmonics do not.  If the signal has half-wave symmetry, meaning $f(t+T/2)=f(t)$, then only even harmonics can remain.  The intuition is the complementary one: after shifting by half a period the waveform must reproduce itself, so only harmonics that keep the same sign under that shift can survive, and those are the even harmonics.

The half-wave antisymmetry derivation is short and useful.  Start from $f(t+T/2)=-f(t)$ and substitute the exponential series $f(t)=\sum_{k=-\infty}^{\infty}F_k e^{jk\omega_0 t}$.  Then $f(t+T/2)=\sum_k F_k e^{jk\omega_0 (t+T/2)}=\sum_k F_k e^{jk\omega_0 t}e^{jk\pi}=\sum_k F_k(-1)^k e^{jk\omega_0 t}$.  Equating this with $-\sum_k F_k e^{jk\omega_0 t}$ gives $F_k(-1)^k=-F_k$, so $F_k=0$ for every even $k$, including $k=0$.  Thus only odd harmonics survive.

The half-wave symmetry derivation is analogous.  If $f(t+T/2)=f(t)$, then $\sum_k F_k(-1)^k e^{jk\omega_0 t}=\sum_k F_k e^{jk\omega_0 t}$, so $F_k((-1)^k-1)=0$.  This forces $F_k=0$ for every odd $k$, meaning only even harmonics remain.

These symmetry rules are not mere shortcuts for exam speed.  They explain why certain waveforms can never produce certain spectral ingredients.  For example, an even waveform has no odd-function content, so it cannot generate sine coefficients.  A half-wave antisymmetric waveform flips sign after half a period, so even harmonics cannot be consistent with that sign pattern.  The spectrum is therefore constrained by geometry before any integral is carried out.

## periodic summation and transform sampling

The Fourier-series and Fourier-transform notes should be read as two directions of the same bridge.  In the Fourier-transform derivation, one begins with a periodic approximation of an aperiodic waveform and lets the period grow to infinity, so the discrete harmonic lines become denser and denser until they turn into a continuous spectrum.  The reverse move is just as important: if one starts from an aperiodic prototype and repeats it every $T$ seconds, the continuous spectrum is sampled on the harmonic grid and turns back into a Fourier series.  This is why the intuition genuinely goes both ways.

The Dirac comb $\operatorname{III}_T(t)=\sum_{n=-\infty}^{\infty}\delta(t-nT)$ is the ideal impulse train of spacing $T$: infinitely many unit impulses, equally spaced, repeating forever.  It helps to attach two interpretations to it at once.  In the time domain, convolving with $\operatorname{III}_T$ makes infinitely many shifted copies, so it creates periodic summation.  In the frequency domain, multiplying by a comb samples a continuous spectrum on a lattice.  So the comb is the bridge object between repetition and sampling.

There is also a clean self-reciprocal memory point.  At the special spacing $T_*=\sqrt{2\pi}$ one has $\operatorname{III}_{T_*}(t)\longleftrightarrow T_*\operatorname{III}_{T_*}(\omega)=\sqrt{2\pi}\,\operatorname{III}_{\sqrt{2\pi}}(\omega)$, so the comb is an eigenfunction of the Fourier transform in the generalized-function sense.  The general pair can then be recovered from scaling.  First write $\operatorname{III}_T(t)=\frac{\sqrt{2\pi}}{T}\operatorname{III}_{\sqrt{2\pi}}\bigl((\sqrt{2\pi}/T)t\bigr)$.  Next use the explicit scaling rule $f(at)\longleftrightarrow \frac{1}{|a|}F(\omega/a)$.  Applying it to the self-reciprocal pair gives $\operatorname{III}_T(t)\longleftrightarrow \frac{2\pi}{T}\operatorname{III}_{2\pi/T}(\omega)=\omega_0\operatorname{III}_{\omega_0}(\omega)$ with $\omega_0=2\pi/T$.  So the whole impulse-train transform can be remembered from one self-reciprocal comb plus the scaling property.

Let $f_0(t)$ be an aperiodic prototype with Fourier transform $F_0(\omega)$, and form its periodic summation $p(t)=\sum_{n=-\infty}^{\infty}f_0(t-nT)=f_0*\operatorname{III}_T(t)$.  Using the comb transform $\operatorname{III}_T(t)\longleftrightarrow \frac{2\pi}{T}\sum_{k=-\infty}^{\infty}\delta(\omega-k\omega_0)=\omega_0\sum_{k=-\infty}^{\infty}\delta(\omega-k\omega_0)$ with $\omega_0=2\pi/T$, the convolution theorem gives $P(\omega)=F_0(\omega)\,\omega_0\sum_{k=-\infty}^{\infty}\delta(\omega-k\omega_0)=\omega_0\sum_{k=-\infty}^{\infty}F_0(k\omega_0)\delta(\omega-k\omega_0)$.

But the Fourier transform of a periodic signal with exponential Fourier-series coefficients $F_k$ is also $P(\omega)=2\pi\sum_{k=-\infty}^{\infty}F_k\delta(\omega-k\omega_0)$.  Comparing the impulse weights yields the explicit sampling rule $F_k=\frac{\omega_0}{2\pi}F_0(k\omega_0)=\frac{1}{T}F_0(k\omega_0)$.  So a Fourier-series coefficient is a sampled Fourier-transform value multiplied by the factor $1/T=\omega_0/(2\pi)$.  Intuitively, $F_0(\omega)$ is a spectral density in $\omega$, so to turn a density into the weight of one discrete harmonic line one multiplies by the harmonic spacing $\Delta\omega=\omega_0$ and then includes the course normalization factor $1/(2\pi)$.

This also gives an intuitive proof that frequency-domain sampling corresponds to periodic repetition in time.  Multiplying a continuous transform by the comb $\omega_0\sum_{k=-\infty}^{\infty}\delta(\omega-k\omega_0)$ samples it at the harmonic frequencies.  Since that frequency comb is itself the Fourier transform of $\operatorname{III}_T(t)$, the inverse-domain operation is convolution with the time comb, namely $f_0*\operatorname{III}_T(t)=\sum_{n=-\infty}^{\infty}f_0(t-nT)$.  So the statement "Fourier series samples Fourier transform" is not just a slogan: the comb in frequency literally becomes periodic summation in time.

The earlier derivation from Fourier series to Fourier transform now looks more symmetric.  If the period $T$ grows, then the harmonic spacing $\omega_0=2\pi/T$ shrinks, the samples become denser, and the discrete spectrum approaches a continuous transform.  If instead the period is fixed and one repeats the prototype forever, the continuous transform is frozen onto that grid and becomes the Fourier-series line spectrum.  One direction is a densifying limit; the other is a sampling operation.

## approximation, Gibbs phenomenon, and periodic pulse-train spectra

Fourier series is not only an exact expansion tool; it is also an approximation method.  If one keeps only finitely many harmonics, then $f_N(t)=a_0+\sum_{k=1}^{N}\big[a_k\cos(k\omega_0 t)+b_k\sin(k\omega_0 t)\big]$.  That finite sum $f_N$ is the best mean-square approximation among all combinations of those retained orthogonal basis functions.  The reason is the same projection principle discussed earlier: once the basis is orthogonal, truncation simply discards the higher-order orthogonal directions.

This mean-square optimality explains the lecture's emphasis on minimum mean square error rather than pointwise perfection.  A truncated Fourier series may still overshoot near discontinuities, but globally it is the best approximation in the average squared-error sense within the chosen harmonic budget.

The error can be written directly as $\varepsilon(t)=f(t)-f_N(t)$ because approximation error is itself just another signal: at each time instant, subtract the approximation from the target and record what is left.  Squaring and averaging then measures the average power of that residual signal.  In function-space language, subtraction gives the vector from the approximation point to the target point, so mean square error is just the squared norm of the residual vector.

Fourier-series synthesis is the reconstruction viewpoint complementary to analysis.  Analysis starts from a waveform and extracts the coefficients; synthesis starts from the coefficients and superposes harmonics to rebuild the waveform.  The reason the lecture uses a pulse train is that a pulse train is one of the simplest non-sinusoidal periodic switching waveforms: it models repeated on-off signaling, duty cycle, and rectangular gating, and it makes harmonic buildup visually clear.  A centered rectangular pulse train means that the same rectangular pulse repeats every period $T$, centered in each period window, so the waveform is periodic rather than a single isolated pulse.

The first synthesis formula comes from the general exponential Fourier-series reconstruction law itself: $f(t)=\sum_{k=-\infty}^{\infty}F_k e^{jk\omega_0 t}$.  For a centered rectangular pulse train the waveform is real and even, so $F_k$ is real and $F_{-k}=F_k$.  Pair the $+k$ and $-k$ terms to get $F_k e^{jk\omega_0 t}+F_{-k}e^{-jk\omega_0 t}=F_k(e^{jk\omega_0 t}+e^{-jk\omega_0 t})=2F_k\cos(k\omega_0 t)$.  Keeping the DC term separate then gives $f(t)=F_0+2\sum_{k=1}^{\infty}F_k\cos(k\omega_0 t)$.  After that, substitute the previously derived pulse-train coefficients $F_k=\frac{E\tau}{T}\operatorname{Sa}(k\omega_0\tau/2)=\frac{E\tau}{T}\operatorname{sinc}_{\pi}(k\omega_0\tau/2\pi)$ to obtain $f(t)=\frac{E\tau}{T}+\frac{2E\tau}{T}\sum_{k=1}^{\infty}\operatorname{Sa}(k\omega_0\tau/2)\cos(k\omega_0 t)=\frac{E\tau}{T}+\frac{2E\tau}{T}\sum_{k=1}^{\infty}\operatorname{sinc}_{\pi}(k\omega_0\tau/2\pi)\cos(k\omega_0 t)$.  This shows that synthesis is harmonic superposition with weights sampled from the sinc-shaped envelope.

The phase of this centered pulse-train spectrum should also be stated explicitly.  Because the waveform is real and even, every coefficient $F_k$ is real and even.  Therefore the phase is $0$ whenever $F_k>0$, it is $\pi$ or $-\pi$ whenever $F_k<0$, and it is undefined at spectral zeros where $F_k=0$.  In other words, the sign changes of the $\operatorname{Sa}$ or $\operatorname{sinc}_{\pi}$ envelope may be interpreted either as alternating signed amplitudes or as positive amplitudes with occasional phase flips by $\pi$.

This synthesis view explains the duty-cycle examples from the lecture.  For a $20\%$ duty cycle, many harmonics survive, but their amplitudes are weighted by the sampled $\operatorname{Sa}$ or $\operatorname{sinc}_{\pi}$ envelope.  For a $50\%$ duty cycle, $\tau/T=1/2$, so $F_k=\frac{E}{2}\operatorname{Sa}(k\pi/2)=\frac{E}{2}\operatorname{sinc}_{\pi}(k/2)$.  Even harmonics vanish because $\sin(k\pi/2)=0$ for even $k$, while odd harmonics remain with alternating sign.  Thus the synthesis becomes $f(t)=\frac{E}{2}+\frac{2E}{\pi}\left(\cos(\omega_0 t)-\frac{1}{3}\cos(3\omega_0 t)+\frac{1}{5}\cos(5\omega_0 t)-\cdots\right)$, which is exactly the familiar odd-harmonic cosine reconstruction of a centered $50\%$ duty-cycle pulse train.

That overshoot is the Gibbs phenomenon.  Near a jump discontinuity, adding more and more harmonics squeezes the oscillations into a narrower neighborhood, but the peak overshoot does not disappear to zero.  Instead it approaches about $9\%$ of the jump height.  This is a classic warning that mean-square convergence and pointwise convergence are not the same thing.

One useful way to understand Gibbs phenomenon is through ideal low-pass filtering.  Truncating the Fourier series to finitely many harmonics means keeping only spectral lines inside a finite frequency band and discarding the rest.  In the time domain, that sharp spectral cutoff behaves like convolution with a sinc-like oscillatory kernel.  A sinc-like kernel cannot stay localized on only one side of a jump; it rings on both sides.  That is why sharper and sharper spectral truncation produces narrower but persistent oscillations near discontinuities.

Graphically, the phenomenon looks like this: away from a jump, higher partial sums track the target waveform more and more closely; near the jump, the oscillatory ripples get packed into a thinner and thinner region, but the tallest overshoot and deepest undershoot do not fully vanish.  So the graph improves almost everywhere while still keeping a characteristic ringing pattern at sharp edges.

The periodic rectangular pulse train is the lecture's most important spectral case study.  Here $E$ is the pulse height, $\tau$ is the pulse width, $T$ is the period, and $\omega_0=2\pi/T$ is the fundamental angular frequency, so the spectral lines occur at $\omega=k\omega_0$.  For a centered pulse train one may write one period as $f(t)=E$ for $|t|<\tau/2$ and $f(t)=0$ for $\tau/2<|t|<T/2$.  Then the exponential Fourier-series coefficients are

$F_k=\frac{1}{T}\int_{-\tau/2}^{\tau/2} E e^{-jk\omega_0 t}dt=\frac{E}{T}\left[\frac{e^{-jk\omega_0 t}}{-jk\omega_0}\right]_{-\tau/2}^{\tau/2}=\frac{E\tau}{T}\operatorname{Sa}\!\left(\frac{k\omega_0\tau}{2}\right)=\frac{E\tau}{T}\operatorname{sinc}_{\pi}\!\left(\frac{k\omega_0\tau}{2\pi}\right)$,

where the normalized sinc form is defined by $\operatorname{sinc}_{\pi}(x)=\frac{\sin(\pi x)}{\pi x}$.

That direct evaluation can be unpacked more carefully.  Because the waveform is zero outside the pulse within one period, the coefficient integral reduces immediately to $F_k=\frac{1}{T}\int_{-\tau/2}^{\tau/2}E e^{-jk\omega_0 t}dt=\frac{E}{T}\int_{-\tau/2}^{\tau/2}e^{-jk\omega_0 t}dt$.  For $k\neq 0$, integrate the exponential to get $F_k=\frac{E}{T}\left[\frac{e^{-jk\omega_0 t}}{-jk\omega_0}\right]_{-\tau/2}^{\tau/2}=\frac{E}{T}\frac{e^{-jk\omega_0\tau/2}-e^{jk\omega_0\tau/2}}{-jk\omega_0}$.  Using $e^{-j\theta}-e^{j\theta}=-2j\sin\theta$, this becomes $F_k=\frac{E}{T}\frac{2\sin(k\omega_0\tau/2)}{k\omega_0}=\frac{E\tau}{T}\frac{\sin(k\omega_0\tau/2)}{k\omega_0\tau/2}$.  This is exactly $\frac{E\tau}{T}\operatorname{Sa}(k\omega_0\tau/2)=\frac{E\tau}{T}\operatorname{sinc}_{\pi}(k\omega_0\tau/2\pi)$.  For $k=0$, the coefficient must be computed separately from the definition: $F_0=\frac{1}{T}\int_{-\tau/2}^{\tau/2}E\,dt=E\tau/T$, which also matches the sinc limit at the origin.

One can also remember this from the Fourier-transform table, and it is helpful to start from the standard rectangular function itself.  Define $\operatorname{rect}(x)=1$ for $|x|<1/2$, $\operatorname{rect}(x)=0$ for $|x|>1/2$, and optionally $\operatorname{rect}(\pm 1/2)=1/2$ as a boundary convention.  Then one centered pulse of height $E$ and width $\tau$ is $E\operatorname{rect}(t/\tau)$.  Its Fourier transform is $E\tau\operatorname{Sa}(\omega\tau/2)=E\tau\operatorname{sinc}_{\pi}(\omega\tau/2\pi)$.  Periodizing that pulse in time with period $T$ means the Fourier-series coefficients are the transform samples at $\omega=k\omega_0$, where $\omega_0=2\pi/T$, divided by $T$.  This again yields $F_k=\frac{E\tau}{T}\operatorname{Sa}(k\omega_0\tau/2)=\frac{E\tau}{T}\operatorname{sinc}_{\pi}(k\omega_0\tau/2\pi)$.

The key interpretation is not the formula alone but its factorization.  The prefactor $E\tau/T$ sets the overall scale through amplitude and duty cycle.  Since $\tau/T$ is the duty cycle, $E\tau/T$ is the average contribution of the pulse over one full period.  In particular, at $\omega=0$ or equivalently $k=0$, the sinc factor becomes $1$, so the coefficient is exactly $F_0=E\tau/T$.  This shows that the prefactor is the DC component of the pulse train, not just a random scaling constant.  The $\operatorname{Sa}$ term then shapes how that DC-scale level is distributed across the nonzero harmonics through the spectral envelope, including the zeros and the main-lobe width.

The $\operatorname{Sa}$ or $\operatorname{sinc}_{\pi}$ envelope should be interpreted from two complementary viewpoints.  If one first looks at a single aperiodic rectangular pulse $E\operatorname{rect}(t/\tau)$, then increasing the pulse width $\tau$ stretches the pulse in time and compresses its Fourier transform in frequency, so the first envelope zero moves closer to the origin.  In formulas, the first zero of $\operatorname{Sa}(\omega\tau/2)=\operatorname{sinc}_{\pi}(\omega\tau/2\pi)$ occurs at $\omega=\pm 2\pi/\tau$, so increasing $\tau$ decreases the bandwidth and pulls the first zero inward.

For the periodic pulse train, increasing the period $T$ plays a different role.  The envelope from the underlying single pulse is still governed by $\tau$, but the Fourier series samples that envelope only at the harmonic frequencies $\omega=k\omega_0$ with $\omega_0=2\pi/T$.  Therefore increasing $T$ decreases $\omega_0$, so the spectral samples become more closely spaced.  The envelope itself is not compressed by increasing $T$; rather, the same envelope is sampled more frequently, which means narrower frequency spacing between adjacent Fourier-series lines.

Graphically, the coefficient magnitudes form discrete spectral lines that sit under a smooth sinc-shaped envelope.  The center line at $k=0$ has height $F_0=E\tau/T$, which is just the pulse average over one period.  Moving away from the origin, the spectral lines oscillate in sign and decay in magnitude according to the sinc envelope, with zeros where the envelope crosses zero.  So the picture is not a continuous sinc curve itself, but a comb of samples taken from that curve at the harmonic frequencies.

Several qualitative bandwidth rules follow immediately.  Increasing $E$ scales every spectral line proportionally without changing the envelope shape.  Decreasing $\tau$ makes the pulse narrower in time and spreads the spectral envelope wider in frequency, with the first envelope zero moving outward according to $\omega\approx 2\pi/\tau$.  Increasing $T$ decreases the spectral line spacing $\omega_0=2\pi/T$, so the lines become denser while the envelope set by $\tau$ stays the same.  This is the periodic-signal version of the time-width versus bandwidth tradeoff.

Under the lecture's distortion-oriented viewpoint, the bandwidth is often defined by the first zero-crossing of the dominant spectral envelope.  For the rectangular pulse train this gives $\omega_B=2\pi/\tau$.  The main lobe is the central part of the envelope between those first zeros, so in angular-frequency language it is the region $|\omega|<2\pi/\tau$.  In harmonic-index language, the first zero occurs when $k\omega_0\tau/2=\pi$, which means $k=T/\tau$.  So the main lobe contains the low-order spectral lines near the origin up to the first zero of the sampled sinc envelope.

The lecture also emphasizes main-lobe power.  The total average power of the pulse train is $P=\frac{1}{T}\int_{-\tau/2}^{\tau/2}E^2\,dt=E^2\tau/T$.  The power inside the main lobe is estimated by summing $|F_k|^2$ over the spectral lines that lie in that central lobe.  For a $20\%$ duty cycle, $\tau/T=0.2$, so the total power is $P=0.2E^2$ and the first zero occurs at $k=T/\tau=5$.  Summing the lines from $k=-5$ to $k=5$ gives approximately $P_{\text{main}}\approx 0.18E^2$, which is about $90\%$ of the total power.  This is the practical meaning of bandwidth here: most of the signal power is already concentrated in the low-frequency main lobe.

It helps to make that main-lobe power idea computationally explicit.  The total power uses Parseval or direct time averaging: $P=\sum_{k=-\infty}^{\infty}|F_k|^2=E^2\tau/T$.  The main-lobe power is not a new theorem; it is simply a partial power sum restricted to the coefficients inside the first spectral lobe, $P_{\text{main}}=\sum_{k\in\text{main lobe}}|F_k|^2$.  For the $20\%$ duty-cycle example, the first zero is at $k=\pm 5$, so the main-lobe estimate comes from adding the normalized power contributions of the lines $k=-5,-4,\ldots,4,5$.  The ratio $P_{\text{main}}/P\approx 0.9$ then says that about $90\%$ of the signal power is captured before leaving the central lobe.  This is why the bandwidth notion is useful in engineering: it tells us how much of the signal's power or shape is retained if we keep only a limited frequency range.

The lecture's final examples connect this to application bandwidths.  The listed ranges should be read as representative engineering scales rather than as magical universal constants.  A traditional telephone speech channel is roughly $300$ to $3400\text{ Hz}$, which intentionally keeps the most intelligible speech content while sacrificing some low and high frequency detail.  Higher-quality audio transmission may use a much wider range such as about $50$ to $15{,}000\text{ Hz}$, preserving more timbre and brightness.  Human hearing is often summarized as roughly $15$ to $20{,}000\text{ Hz}$ for a healthy young listener.  These examples reinforce the same bandwidth message: different systems preserve different fractions of the available spectrum depending on fidelity, cost, and perceptual goals.

The same section is also the natural home for Fourier synthesis.  Reconstructing the waveform by summing its harmonics shows directly how richer waveforms are built.  One harmonic gives a pure tone.  More harmonics sharpen edges, add asymmetry, or enrich timbre.  This is why the Fourier-series application PDF focuses on musical sound: a periodic note can be interpreted either as one waveform in time or as a harmonic recipe in frequency.

The audio-engineering examples reinforce that frequency analysis is not abstract bookkeeping.  In synthesis, one chooses a fundamental frequency to set pitch and then chooses harmonic amplitudes and phases to shape the sound.  In analysis, one reads those harmonic patterns back out of the waveform or spectrum.  This is the same mathematics viewed once from the perspective of decomposition and once from the perspective of reconstruction.

Most musical tones are approximately periodic because they are produced by vibrating systems that repeat a basic cycle: guitar and violin strings, air columns in flutes, and many voiced-speech mechanisms all create waveforms that are close to satisfying $x(t+T)=x(t)$ over short windows.  Because the waveform is periodic, Fourier series is the natural representation.  Instead of thinking of the sound only as a complicated time waveform, one can think of it as a superposition of harmonically related sinusoids.

In application language, a musical tone is often written as $x(t)=\sum_{k=1}^{\infty} A_k\sin(k\omega_0 t+\phi_k)$, where $A_k$ is the amplitude of the $k$-th harmonic, $k\omega_0$ is the harmonic angular frequency, $\omega_0=2\pi f_0$ is the fundamental angular frequency, and $f_0$ is the perceived pitch or fundamental frequency in hertz.  This formula is not a different theory from Fourier series; it is the audio-engineering reading of the same idea.  Each sinusoid is one harmonic ingredient, and the whole tone is the sum of those ingredients.

This is also why different instruments playing the same note can sound different.  A flute waveform is often close to a sinusoid, so its spectrum is dominated by the fundamental with comparatively weak higher harmonics.  A violin waveform is much richer and less sinusoidal, so it contains stronger higher-order harmonics.  The pitch can therefore be the same while the timbre differs because the harmonic amplitudes and phases differ.

The spectrum of a periodic musical signal is therefore a discrete line spectrum.  Each spectral line corresponds to one harmonic frequency, and the line height records how strongly that harmonic contributes.  In synthesis, adding only the fundamental produces a nearly pure tone like a tuning fork.  Adding a few more harmonics makes the waveform and sound richer.  Adding many harmonics produces a waveform much closer to the shape of a real instrument signal.  This is the practical meaning of Fourier synthesis in audio: complex sound is reconstructed by harmonic addition.

Audio engineering uses this principle in both synthesis and analysis.  A synthesizer may first choose a pitch $f_0$, then assign harmonic amplitudes such as $A_1=1$, $A_2=0.6$, $A_3=0.4$, $A_4=0.3$, and then generate the waveform by summing those sinusoids.  Conversely, spectral analysis reads harmonic structure back out of recorded speech or music.  This is why Fourier-series ideas appear naturally in electronic instruments, speech and music processing, and compression systems that exploit the fact that not all harmonics matter equally to perception.
