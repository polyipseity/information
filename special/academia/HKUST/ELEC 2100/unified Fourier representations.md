## the comb identities used throughout

The time-domain Dirac comb of spacing $T$ is $\operatorname{III}_T(t)=\sum_{n=-\infty}^{\infty}\delta(t-nT)$. In the present Fourier-transform convention it satisfies $\operatorname{III}_T(t)\longleftrightarrow \frac{2\pi}{T}\operatorname{III}_{2\pi/T}(\omega)=\frac{2\pi}{T}\sum_{k=-\infty}^{\infty}\delta\!\left(\omega-k\frac{2\pi}{T}\right)$. If the spacing is instead named in frequency by $\omega_0$, then the frequency comb is $\operatorname{III}_{\omega_0}(\omega)=\sum_{k=-\infty}^{\infty}\delta(\omega-k\omega_0)$ and its inverse-transform partner is $\operatorname{III}_{\omega_0}(\omega)\longleftrightarrow \frac{1}{\omega_0}\operatorname{III}_{2\pi/\omega_0}(t)=\frac{1}{\omega_0}\sum_{r=-\infty}^{\infty}\delta\!\left(t-r\frac{2\pi}{\omega_0}\right)$. So a comb in one domain always corresponds to a periodic comb in the other domain, with reciprocal spacing up to the factor $2\pi$.

Two transform rules are repeatedly needed. If $g(t)=f(t)h(t)$, then $G(\omega)=\frac{1}{2\pi}(F*H)(\omega)$, while if $g(t)=(f*h)(t)$, then $G(\omega)=F(\omega)H(\omega)$. The first rule turns time sampling into spectral replication; the second turns frequency sampling into periodic summation in time. This little two-line toolkit is the hidden skeleton of the whole note.

## the parent corner: Fourier transform

The continuous-time Fourier transform is the parent corner because both time and frequency are continuous. Its analysis formula is $F(\omega)=\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}f(t)e^{-j\omega t}\,dt$, and its synthesis formula is $f(t)=\frac{1}{2\pi}\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}F(\omega)e^{j\omega t}\,d\omega$. The interpretation is that $F(\omega)$ is a coefficient density, not a discrete coefficient list: the signal is reconstructed by integrating infinitesimal spectral contributions $F(\omega)d\omega/(2\pi)$ from all angular frequencies.

The convolution theorem in course convention is obtained in the standard way. If $y(t)=(f*g)(t)=\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}f(\tau)g(t-\tau)\,d\tau$, then substitution into the Fourier transform gives $Y(\omega)=F(\omega)G(\omega)$. The corresponding time-multiplication theorem is obtained by reversing the logic: if $y(t)=f(t)g(t)$, then $Y(\omega)=\frac{1}{2\pi}(F*G)(\omega)=\frac{1}{2\pi}\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}F(\nu)G(\omega-\nu)\,d\nu$. The memory rule is simple: convolution in time becomes multiplication in frequency, while multiplication in time becomes convolution in frequency with the course factor $1/(2\pi)$.

For norm bookkeeping, the natural object here is energy rather than average power. If $f$ is an energy signal, then Parseval's formula is $E=\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}|f(t)|^2\,dt=\frac{1}{2\pi}\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}|F(\omega)|^2\,d\omega$. This is the continuous-spectrum energy identity. An ordinary average-power formula is not the natural companion of the classical Fourier transform pair, because generic power signals are not absolutely integrable and are more naturally handled by Fourier series, line spectra, or power spectral density methods rather than by the plain finite-energy Fourier-transform pair. So in this parent corner the energy Parseval formula is central, while a basic power Parseval formula is not generally applicable.

The recall picture is therefore: Fourier transform is the continuous-continuous corner, convolution becomes multiplication, multiplication becomes convolution with $1/(2\pi)$, and Parseval compares total signal energy with the total spectral energy density.

## frequency discretization of Fourier transform gives Fourier series

To derive Fourier series from the parent corner, discretize the frequency axis first. Multiply the continuous spectrum by the frequency comb of spacing $\omega_0$: $F_{\mathrm{raw,FS}}(\omega)=F(\omega)\operatorname{III}_{\omega_0}(\omega)=\sum_{k=-\infty}^{\infty}F(k\omega_0)\delta(\omega-k\omega_0)$. This is the raw sampled spectrum, and the sampled points are still labeled by the physical angular frequencies $\omega=k\omega_0$.

Now use the convolution theorem in reverse. Since $\operatorname{III}_{\omega_0}(\omega)\longleftrightarrow \frac{1}{\omega_0}\operatorname{III}_{T_0}(t)$ with $T_0=2\pi/\omega_0$, multiplication in frequency corresponds to convolution in time, so $f_{\mathrm{raw,FS}}(t)=f(t)*\frac{1}{\omega_0}\operatorname{III}_{T_0}(t)=\frac{1}{\omega_0}\sum_{r=-\infty}^{\infty}f(t-rT_0)$. So the first effect of frequency discretization is periodic summation in time. This is the cleanest intuition: a line spectrum in frequency can only synthesize a periodic waveform in time.

The same periodic result appears directly from the inverse transform. Since $F_{\mathrm{raw,FS}}(\omega)$ is a sum of impulses, the inverse integral collapses to $f_{\mathrm{raw,FS}}(t)=\frac{1}{2\pi}\sum_{k=-\infty}^{\infty}F(k\omega_0)e^{jk\omega_0 t}$. Only now do we rescale the sampled frequency locations by the spacing $\omega_0$ so that the frequency labels become the integers $k$, and only now do we fix the normalization by defining the course Fourier-series coefficient $F_k:=\frac{1}{2\pi}F(k\omega_0)$. Then the synthesis formula becomes $f_{\mathrm{FS}}(t)=\sum_{k=-\infty}^{\infty}F_k e^{jk\omega_0 t}$.

To derive the course analysis formula, integrate one full period against the $k$-th basis function. Because $f_{\mathrm{FS}}(t)$ is $T_0$-periodic and the complex exponentials are orthogonal over one period, $\frac{1}{T_0}\int_{t_0}^{t_0+T_0}f_{\mathrm{FS}}(t)e^{-jk\omega_0 t}\,dt=F_k$. So the course Fourier-series pair is $F_k=\frac{1}{T_0}\int_{t_0}^{t_0+T_0}f_{\mathrm{FS}}(t)e^{-jk\omega_0 t}\,dt$ and $f_{\mathrm{FS}}(t)=\sum_{k=-\infty}^{\infty}F_k e^{jk\omega_0 t}$, together with the transform-view identity $F_{\mathrm{FS}}(\omega)=2\pi\sum_{k=-\infty}^{\infty}F_k\delta(\omega-k\omega_0)$.

Now derive the convolution pair in course convention. If $h(t)$ is the periodic convolution $h(t)=\int_{t_0}^{t_0+T_0}f(\tau)g(t-\tau)\,d\tau$, then substituting the Fourier-series expansions of $f$ and $g$ and using orthogonality shows that $H_k=T_0F_kG_k$. So periodic convolution in time corresponds to pointwise multiplication of Fourier-series coefficients, but now with the factor $T_0$ because the coefficient normalization contains $1/T_0$. The corresponding time-multiplication theorem is obtained from direct multiplication in time: if $h(t)=f(t)g(t)$ and $f(t)=\sum_m F_m e^{jm\omega_0 t}$ and $g(t)=\sum_n G_n e^{jn\omega_0 t}$, then $h(t)=\sum_k\left(\sum_m F_mG_{k-m}\right)e^{jk\omega_0 t}$, so $H_k=\sum_m F_mG_{k-m}$. Thus multiplication in time becomes discrete convolution of the Fourier-series coefficients.

For norm bookkeeping, the natural object is average power rather than total energy, because a nonzero periodic signal has infinite total energy. The power Parseval formula is $P=\frac{1}{T_0}\int_{t_0}^{t_0+T_0}|f(t)|^2\,dt=\sum_{k=-\infty}^{\infty}|F_k|^2$. There is no finite total-energy Parseval formula here except for the zero signal, because the energy of a nonzero periodic signal diverges. So in the Fourier-series corner, power is the natural norm and energy is not generally applicable.

The recall picture is now complete: a frequency comb creates periodic summation in time, the resulting periodic signal is analyzed by one-period orthogonality, periodic convolution becomes coefficient multiplication with factor $T_0$, pointwise multiplication becomes discrete convolution in $k$, and Parseval measures average power rather than total energy.

## time discretization of Fourier transform gives DTFT

To derive DTFT from the parent corner, discretize the time axis first. Multiply the signal by the time comb of spacing $T$: $x_s(t)=f(t)\operatorname{III}_T(t)=\sum_{n=-\infty}^{\infty}f(nT)\delta(t-nT)$. This is the raw time-sampled impulse train, and the samples are still labeled by the physical times $t=nT$.

Now use the multiplication theorem in time. Since $\operatorname{III}_T(t)\longleftrightarrow \frac{2\pi}{T}\operatorname{III}_{\omega_s}(\omega)$ with $\omega_s=2\pi/T$, multiplication in time corresponds to convolution in frequency, so $X_s(\omega)=\frac{1}{2\pi}\left(F*\frac{2\pi}{T}\operatorname{III}_{\omega_s}\right)(\omega)=\frac{1}{T}\sum_{k=-\infty}^{\infty}F(\omega-k\omega_s)$. Thus the first effect of time discretization is spectral replication. This is the exact dual intuition to Fourier series: a comb in time forces periodicity in frequency.

The same raw spectrum can be computed directly from the impulse train: $X_s(\omega)=\sum_{n=-\infty}^{\infty}f(nT)e^{-j\omega nT}$. Only now do we relabel the sample locations by integers and normalize the frequency variable by defining $x[n]:=f(nT)$ and $\Omega:=\omega T$. Then $X_s(\omega)=\sum_{n=-\infty}^{\infty}x[n]e^{-j\Omega n}$, and renaming this periodic function as $X(e^{j\Omega})$ gives the course forward DTFT $X(e^{j\Omega})=\sum_{n=-\infty}^{\infty}x[n]e^{-j\Omega n}$. The inverse formula comes from $2\pi$-periodicity in $\Omega$: one period is enough, so orthogonality on $[-\pi,\pi]$ yields $x[n]=\frac{1}{2\pi}\int_{-\pi}^{\pi}X(e^{j\Omega})e^{j\Omega n}\,d\Omega$.

The convolution theorem in course convention is the linear-convolution rule. If $y[n]=\sum_m x[m]h[n-m]$, then substituting the sums into the DTFT gives $Y(e^{j\Omega})=X(e^{j\Omega})H(e^{j\Omega})$. The corresponding sequence-multiplication theorem is the pointwise-multiplication rule: if $y[n]=x[n]h[n]$, then $Y(e^{j\Omega})=\frac{1}{2\pi}\int_{-\pi}^{\pi}X(e^{j\theta})H(e^{j(\Omega-\theta)})\,d\theta$. In other words, multiplication in the sequence domain becomes periodic convolution in the digital-frequency domain, with the same course factor $1/(2\pi)$ as in the parent Fourier-transform corner.

For norm bookkeeping, the natural object here is again total energy. If the sequence has finite energy, then Parseval's formula is $E=\sum_{n=-\infty}^{\infty}|x[n]|^2=\frac{1}{2\pi}\int_{-\pi}^{\pi}|X(e^{j\Omega})|^2\,d\Omega$. This is the discrete-time analogue of the parent Fourier-transform energy identity. A general average-power Parseval formula is not the natural companion of the ordinary DTFT pair, because power sequences such as periodic sequences are more naturally represented by line spectra and therefore belong to DTFS/DFS rather than to the ordinary finite-energy DTFT setting.

So the DTFT corner is the discrete-time continuous-periodic-frequency corner: analysis and synthesis look like Fourier transform with the variable $\Omega$ wrapped modulo $2\pi$, linear convolution becomes multiplication, pointwise multiplication becomes periodic spectral convolution, and Parseval compares discrete-time energy with one-period spectral energy.

- derivation / DTFT sequence-multiplication theorem ::@:: Step 1: start from $y[n]=x[n]h[n]$ and form its DTFT $Y(e^{j\Omega})=\sum_n x[n]h[n]e^{-j\Omega n}$. <br/> Step 2: substitute the inverse DTFT of $x[n]$, namely $x[n]=\frac{1}{2\pi}\int_{-\pi}^{\pi}X(e^{j\theta})e^{j\theta n}d\theta$. <br/> Step 3: then $Y(e^{j\Omega})=\sum_n\left(\frac{1}{2\pi}\int_{-\pi}^{\pi}X(e^{j\theta})e^{j\theta n}d\theta\right)h[n]e^{-j\Omega n}$. <br/> Step 4: interchange sum and integral to get $Y(e^{j\Omega})=\frac{1}{2\pi}\int_{-\pi}^{\pi}X(e^{j\theta})\left(\sum_n h[n]e^{-j(\Omega-\theta)n}\right)d\theta$. <br/> Step 5: recognize the inner sum as $H(e^{j(\Omega-\theta)})$. <br/> Step 6: therefore $Y(e^{j\Omega})=\frac{1}{2\pi}\int_{-\pi}^{\pi}X(e^{j\theta})H(e^{j(\Omega-\theta)})d\theta$, which is periodic convolution in frequency.
- derivation / DTFT Parseval energy identity ::@:: Step 1: write $\overline{x[n]}$ using the inverse DTFT as $\overline{x[n]}=\frac{1}{2\pi}\int_{-\pi}^{\pi}\overline{X(e^{j\Omega})}e^{-j\Omega n}d\Omega$. <br/> Step 2: then the sequence energy is $E=\sum_n x[n]\overline{x[n]}=\sum_n x[n]\left(\frac{1}{2\pi}\int_{-\pi}^{\pi}\overline{X(e^{j\Omega})}e^{-j\Omega n}d\Omega\right)$. <br/> Step 3: interchange sum and integral to get $E=\frac{1}{2\pi}\int_{-\pi}^{\pi}\overline{X(e^{j\Omega})}\left(\sum_n x[n]e^{-j\Omega n}\right)d\Omega$. <br/> Step 4: recognize the inner sum as $X(e^{j\Omega})$. <br/> Step 5: thus $E=\frac{1}{2\pi}\int_{-\pi}^{\pi}|X(e^{j\Omega})|^2d\Omega$. <br/> Step 6: so total sequence energy equals one-period spectral energy in course convention.

## the Fourier-series branch reaches DFT by discretizing time again

Once the Fourier-series branch has discrete frequency but continuous time, the remaining continuous axis is time. So the mathematically consistent second discretization on this branch is another time comb. Start with the course Fourier series $f_{\mathrm{FS}}(t)=\sum_{r=-\infty}^{\infty}F_r e^{jr\omega_0 t}$ with period $T_0=2\pi/\omega_0$. Sample one period uniformly with spacing $T_s=T_0/N$, so $\omega_s=2\pi/T_s=N\omega_0$. Multiplying by the time comb gives $x_s(t)=f_{\mathrm{FS}}(t)\operatorname{III}_{T_s}(t)=\sum_{n=-\infty}^{\infty}f_{\mathrm{FS}}(nT_s)\delta(t-nT_s)$.

Now use the multiplication theorem in time. Since $\operatorname{III}_{T_s}(t)\longleftrightarrow \frac{2\pi}{T_s}\operatorname{III}_{N\omega_0}(\omega)$, the sampled spectrum is $X_s(\omega)=\frac{1}{2\pi}\left(F_{\mathrm{FS}}*\frac{2\pi}{T_s}\operatorname{III}_{N\omega_0}\right)(\omega)=\frac{1}{T_s}\sum_{q=-\infty}^{\infty}F_{\mathrm{FS}}(\omega-qN\omega_0)$. Substituting $F_{\mathrm{FS}}(\omega)=2\pi\sum_r F_r\delta(\omega-r\omega_0)$ shows explicitly that harmonics whose indices differ by multiples of $N$ now collapse into the same sampled class.

Define the aliased harmonic-class coefficient by $\widetilde X[m]:=\sum_{q=-\infty}^{\infty}F_{m+qN}$ for $m=0,1,\dots,N-1$. Then the sampled values satisfy $x[n]=f_{\mathrm{FS}}(nT_s)=\sum_{r=-\infty}^{\infty}F_r e^{j2\pi rn/N}=\sum_{m=0}^{N-1}\widetilde X[m]e^{j2\pi mn/N}$. This is already a finite harmonic synthesis formula. Orthogonality on the finite grid gives $\widetilde X[m]=\frac{1}{N}\sum_{n=0}^{N-1}x[n]e^{-j2\pi mn/N}$. Defining the course DFT coefficient by $X[m]:=N\widetilde X[m]$ yields the final course DFT pair $X[m]=\sum_{n=0}^{N-1}x[n]e^{-j2\pi mn/N}$ and $x[n]=\frac{1}{N}\sum_{m=0}^{N-1}X[m]e^{j2\pi mn/N}$.

This route already contains the DFT convolution theorem in embryo. Because DFT is just the finite-grid normalization of the harmonic coefficients, circular time convolution corresponds to pointwise multiplication of the DFT bins: if $y[n]=(x\circledast h)[n]$, then $Y[k]=X[k]H[k]$. The corresponding time-multiplication theorem is the bin-domain circular-convolution rule: if $y[n]=x[n]h[n]$, then $Y[k]=\frac{1}{N}(X\circledast H)[k]$, where the convolution on the right is circular in the bin index. The factor $1/N$ appears because DFT is the sum-first normalization of the finite harmonic decomposition.

For norm bookkeeping, the finite record has a genuine total energy and also an average power when interpreted as one period of its implicit periodic extension. The energy Parseval formula is $E=\sum_{n=0}^{N-1}|x[n]|^2=\frac{1}{N}\sum_{k=0}^{N-1}|X[k]|^2$. If the same record is interpreted as one period of a periodic sequence, then the average power per period is $P=\frac{1}{N}\sum_{n=0}^{N-1}|x[n]|^2=\frac{1}{N^2}\sum_{k=0}^{N-1}|X[k]|^2$. So in the DFT corner both energy and period-averaged power make sense, but they refer to two different viewpoints: the finite record versus its implicit periodic extension.

The recall picture is therefore: time sampling of the Fourier-series branch aliases harmonics modulo $N$, the resulting finite harmonic list becomes the course DFT after normalization, circular convolution becomes binwise multiplication, pointwise multiplication becomes circular bin convolution, and Parseval can be read either as finite-record energy or as average power of the periodic extension.

## the DTFT branch reaches the same DFT by discretizing frequency again

The same DFT can be reached from the DTFT branch. Once the DTFT branch has discrete time but continuous periodic frequency, the remaining continuous axis is frequency. So the second discretization on this branch is a frequency comb. Start with the course DTFT $X(e^{j\Omega})=\sum_{n=-\infty}^{\infty}x[n]e^{-j\Omega n}$. To sample one full $2\pi$ period at $N$ equally spaced points, multiply by the weighted comb $C_N(\Omega):=\frac{2\pi}{N}\operatorname{III}_{2\pi/N}(\Omega)=\frac{2\pi}{N}\sum_{k=-\infty}^{\infty}\delta\!\left(\Omega-k\frac{2\pi}{N}\right)$. The weight $2\pi/N$ is the spacing of the sampled $\Omega$-grid and is exactly what becomes the factor $1/N$ in the inverse finite sum.

The raw sampled periodic spectrum is $X_{\mathrm{raw,DFT}}(\Omega)=X(e^{j\Omega})C_N(\Omega)=\frac{2\pi}{N}\sum_{k=-\infty}^{\infty}X\!\left(e^{j2\pi k/N}\right)\delta\!\left(\Omega-k\frac{2\pi}{N}\right)$. Under the DTFT pair this comb corresponds to the periodic impulse train $c_N[n]=\sum_{r=-\infty}^{\infty}\delta[n-rN]$, so multiplication by the comb corresponds to convolution with $c_N[n]$ in the sequence domain. Hence $x_{\mathrm{raw,DFT}}[n]=(x*c_N)[n]=\sum_{r=-\infty}^{\infty}x[n-rN]$, which is periodic summation modulo $N$. If the original sequence is already supported on one principal interval $0\le n\le N-1$, this periodic summation simply reproduces the stored record on that interval.

Inverting the raw sampled periodic spectrum gives $x[n]=\frac{1}{2\pi}\int_{-\pi}^{\pi}X_{\mathrm{raw,DFT}}(\Omega)e^{j\Omega n}\,d\Omega=\frac{1}{N}\sum_{k=0}^{N-1}X\!\left(e^{j2\pi k/N}\right)e^{j2\pi kn/N}$. Defining the course DFT bin by $X[k]:=X\!\left(e^{j2\pi k/N}\right)$ yields exactly the same DFT pair as before. So the DFT bins can be read either as the normalized harmonic classes of the Fourier-series branch or as the sampled DTFT values of the DTFT branch.

This also shows why the DFT theorems listed in the previous section are not branch-dependent. Once the same DFT pair is obtained, the same circular convolution theorem, time-multiplication theorem, and Parseval formulas follow regardless of which derivation route was taken. The two routes differ only in the interpretation of what the bins came from, not in the final algebra.

## why the two DFT derivations agree

The two DFT derivations must meet because they are just two different ways of discretizing the parent Fourier-transform picture twice. The agreement is explicit. From the Fourier-series branch the sampled sequence is $x[n]=\sum_{r=-\infty}^{\infty}F_r e^{j2\pi rn/N}$. Computing its DFT gives $X[m]=\sum_{n=0}^{N-1}x[n]e^{-j2\pi mn/N}=\sum_{n=0}^{N-1}\sum_{r=-\infty}^{\infty}F_r e^{j2\pi rn/N}e^{-j2\pi mn/N}$. Interchanging the sums yields $X[m]=\sum_{r=-\infty}^{\infty}F_r\sum_{n=0}^{N-1}e^{j2\pi (r-m)n/N}$. The inner sum is $N$ when $r\equiv m\pmod N$ and $0$ otherwise, so $X[m]=N\sum_{q=-\infty}^{\infty}F_{m+qN}$.

But on the Fourier-series branch we already defined the modulo-$N$ harmonic class by $\widetilde X[m]=\sum_{q=-\infty}^{\infty}F_{m+qN}$, so this becomes $X[m]=N\widetilde X[m]$, exactly the DFT normalization used above. On the DTFT branch, the same number is $X(e^{j2\pi m/N})$. Therefore the unified identity is $X(e^{j2\pi m/N})=X[m]=N\sum_{q=-\infty}^{\infty}F_{m+qN}$. One DFT bin is therefore simultaneously a sampled DTFT value and the grouped sum of all continuous-time Fourier-series harmonics whose indices are congruent modulo $N$.

This is the sharpest recall sentence in the whole note: DFT remembers only what survives after one more discretization, and that surviving object can be read either as a sampled periodic spectrum or as aliased harmonic classes.

## one short signal through all four corners

Take the periodic continuous-time signal $f(t)=1+\cos(\omega_0 t)$ with period $T_0=2\pi/\omega_0$. On the Fourier-series branch we have $f(t)=1+\frac12 e^{j\omega_0 t}+\frac12 e^{-j\omega_0 t}$, so the only nonzero coefficients are $F_0=1$, $F_1=1/2$, and $F_{-1}=1/2$. In the Fourier-transform picture the same signal is $F(\omega)=2\pi\delta(\omega)+\pi\delta(\omega-\omega_0)+\pi\delta(\omega+\omega_0)$. This is a useful first memory check: the line weights are $2\pi F_k$, not just $F_k$.

Now sample one period at $N=4$ equally spaced points, so $T_s=T_0/4$. The samples are $x[0]=2$, $x[1]=1$, $x[2]=0$, and $x[3]=1$, so the discrete-time sequence is $[2,1,0,1]$. Its DTFT is $X(e^{j\Omega})=2+e^{-j\Omega}+e^{-j3\Omega}$, which is still a continuous periodic function of $\Omega$. Sampling that DTFT on the grid $\Omega_k=2\pi k/4$ gives $X[0]=4$, $X[1]=2$, $X[2]=0$, and $X[3]=2$, so the DFT is $[4,2,0,2]$.

The Fourier-series branch gives the same answer from the harmonic side. Group the harmonics modulo $4$: $\widetilde X[0]=1$, $\widetilde X[1]=1/2$, $\widetilde X[2]=0$, and $\widetilde X[3]=1/2$. Converting to the course DFT normalization by $X[m]=4\widetilde X[m]$ gives again $[4,2,0,2]$. So this one signal exhibits the whole square in one compact example: line spectrum in physical angular frequency, harmonic coefficients in Fourier series, a continuous periodic DTFT after time sampling, and a finite DFT after one more discretization.

## the four final course-convention formulas

After all the raw comb multiplications, relabelings, and normalization fixes, the final course-convention formulas can be stated in one place. Fourier transform: $F(\omega)=\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}f(t)e^{-j\omega t}\,dt$ and $f(t)=\frac{1}{2\pi}\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}F(\omega)e^{j\omega t}\,d\omega$. Fourier series: $F_k=\frac{1}{T_0}\int_{t_0}^{t_0+T_0}f(t)e^{-jk\omega_0 t}\,dt$ and $f(t)=\sum_{k=-\infty}^{\infty}F_k e^{jk\omega_0 t}$, with $F(\omega)=2\pi\sum_k F_k\delta(\omega-k\omega_0)$. DTFT: $X(e^{j\Omega})=\sum_{n=-\infty}^{\infty}x[n]e^{-j\Omega n}$ and $x[n]=\frac{1}{2\pi}\int_{-\pi}^{\pi}X(e^{j\Omega})e^{j\Omega n}\,d\Omega$. DFT: $X[k]=\sum_{n=0}^{N-1}x[n]e^{-j2\pi kn/N}$ and $x[n]=\frac{1}{N}\sum_{k=0}^{N-1}X[k]e^{j2\pi kn/N}$.

The shortest conceptual summary is now straightforward. Fourier transform is continuous time with continuous frequency. Fourier series is continuous time with discrete frequency. DTFT is discrete time with continuous periodic frequency. DFT is discrete time with discrete periodic frequency. The exponential basis is the same in all four corners; what changes is which axis has been discretized and therefore where the normalization factor must sit. That is why the formulas look similar but not identical.
