{@{__Quantum mechanics__}@} is {@{the fundamental physical [theory](scientific%20theory.md) that describes the behavior of matter and of light}@}; its {@{unusual characteristics}@} typically {@{occur at and below the scale of [atoms](atom.md)}@}.<sup>[\[2\]](#^ref-2)</sup><sup>:&hairsp;1.1&hairsp;</sup> It is {@{the foundation of all __quantum physics__}@}, which includes {@{[quantum chemistry](quantum%20chemistry.md), [quantum biology](quantum%20biology.md), [quantum field theory](quantum%20field%20theory.md)}@}, {@{[quantum technology](quantum%20technology.md), and [quantum information science](quantum%20information%20science.md)}@}.

{@{Quantum mechanics}@} can describe {@{many systems that [classical physics](classical%20physics.md) cannot}@}. {@{Classical physics}@} can describe {@{many aspects of nature at an ordinary \([macroscopic](macroscopic.md) and [\(optical\) microscopic](microscopic%20scale.md)\) scale}@}, but is not {@{sufficient for describing them at very small [submicroscopic](submicroscopic.md#sub-microscopic) \(atomic and [subatomic](subatomic.md)\) scales}@}. {@{Classical mechanics}@} can be derived {@{from quantum mechanics as an approximation that is valid at ordinary scales}@}.<sup>[\[3\]](#^ref-3)</sup>

{@{__Quantum systems__}@} have {@{[bound](bound%20state.md) states that are [quantized](quantization%20(physics).md) to [discrete values](discrete%20mathematics.md)}@} of {@{[energy](energy.md), [momentum](momentum.md), [angular momentum](angular%20momentum.md), and other quantities}@}, in contrast to {@{classical systems where these quantities can be measured continuously}@}. {@{Measurements of quantum systems}@} show {@{characteristics of both [particles](particle.md) and [waves](wave.md) \([wave–particle duality](wave–particle%20duality.md)\)}@}, and there are limits to {@{how accurately the value of a physical quantity can be predicted prior to its measurement}@}, given {@{a complete set of initial conditions}@} \({@{the [uncertainty principle](uncertainty%20principle.md)}@}\).

{@{Quantum mechanics}@} arose gradually from {@{theories to explain observations that could not be reconciled with [classical physics](classical%20physics.md)}@}, such as {@{[Max Planck](Max%20Planck.md)'s solution in 1900}@} to {@{the [black-body radiation](black-body%20radiation.md) problem}@}, and {@{the correspondence between energy and frequency}@} in {@{[Albert Einstein](Albert%20Einstein.md)'s [1905 paper](annus%20mirabilis%20papers.md#photoelectric%20effect)}@}, which explained {@{the [photoelectric effect](photoelectric%20effect.md)}@}. {@{These early attempts}@} to understand {@{microscopic phenomena}@}, now known as {@{the "[old quantum theory](old%20quantum%20theory.md)"}@}, led to {@{the full development of quantum mechanics in the mid-1920s}@} by {@{[Niels Bohr](Niels%20Bohr.md), [Erwin Schrödinger](Erwin%20Schrödinger.md), [Werner Heisenberg](Werner%20Heisenberg.md)}@}, {@{[Max Born](Max%20Born.md), [Paul Dirac](Paul%20Dirac.md) and others}@}. {@{The modern theory}@} is formulated in {@{various [specially developed mathematical formalisms](mathematical%20formulations%20of%20quantum%20mechanics.md)}@}. In one of them, {@{a mathematical entity called the [wave function](wave%20function.md)}@} provides {@{information, in the form of [probability amplitudes](probability%20amplitude.md)}@}, about {@{what measurements of a particle's energy, momentum, and other physical properties}@} may yield.

## overview and fundamental concepts

{@{Quantum mechanics}@} allows {@{the calculation of properties and behaviour of [physical systems](physical%20systems.md)}@}. It is typically {@{applied to microscopic systems}@}: {@{[molecules](molecules.md), [atoms](atoms.md) and [subatomic particles](subatomic%20particle.md)}@}. It has been demonstrated to {@{hold for complex molecules with thousands of atoms}@},<sup>[\[4\]](#^ref-4)</sup> but its application to {@{human beings raises philosophical problems, such as [Wigner's friend](Wigner's%20friend.md)}@}, and its application to {@{the universe as a whole remains speculative}@}.<sup>[\[5\]](#^ref-5)</sup> {@{Predictions of quantum mechanics}@} have been verified {@{experimentally to an extremely high degree of [accuracy](accuracy.md)}@}. For example, the refinement of quantum mechanics for {@{the interaction of light and matter, known as [quantum electrodynamics](quantum%20electrodynamics.md) \(QED\)}@}, has been {@{[shown to agree with experiment](precision%20tests%20of%20QED.md) to within 1 part in 10<sup>12</sup> when predicting the magnetic properties of an electron}@}.<sup>[\[6\]](#^ref-6)</sup>

{@{A fundamental feature}@} of the theory is that it usually {@{cannot predict with certainty what will happen}@}, but only {@{gives probabilities}@}. Mathematically, {@{a probability is found}@} by taking {@{the square of the absolute value of a [complex number](complex%20number.md), known as a probability amplitude}@}. This is known as {@{the [Born rule](Born%20rule.md)}@}, named after {@{physicist [Max Born](Max%20Born.md)}@}. For example, {@{a quantum particle like an [electron](electron.md)}@} can be described by {@{a wave function, which associates to each point in space a probability amplitude}@}. Applying {@{the Born rule to these amplitudes}@} gives {@{a [probability density function](probability%20density%20function.md)}@} for the position that {@{the electron will be found to have when an experiment is performed to measure it}@}. This is {@{the best the theory can do}@}; it cannot say {@{for certain where the electron will be found}@}. {@{The [Schrödinger equation](Schrödinger%20equation.md)}@} relates {@{the collection of probability amplitudes that pertain to one moment of time}@} to {@{the collection of probability amplitudes that pertain to another}@}.<sup>[\[7\]](#^ref-7)</sup><sup>:&hairsp;67–87&hairsp;</sup>

{@{One consequence of the mathematical rules}@} of quantum mechanics is a tradeoff {@{in predictability between measurable quantities}@}. {@{The most famous form of this [uncertainty principle](uncertainty%20principle.md)}@} says that no matter {@{how a quantum particle is prepared or how carefully experiments upon it are arranged}@}, it is impossible to have {@{a precise prediction for a measurement of its position and also at the same time for a measurement of its [momentum](momentum.md)}@}.<sup>[\[7\]](#^ref-7)</sup><sup>:&hairsp;427–435&hairsp;</sup>

> {@{![An illustration of the [double-slit experiment](double-slit%20experiment.md)](../../archives/Wikimedia%20Commons/Double-slit.svg)}@}
>
> {@{An illustration}@} of {@{the [double-slit experiment](double-slit%20experiment.md)}@}

{@{Another consequence of the mathematical rules}@} of quantum mechanics is {@{the phenomenon of [quantum interference](quantum%20interference.md#quantum%20interference)}@}, which is often {@{illustrated with the [double-slit experiment](double-slit%20experiment.md)}@}. In {@{the basic version of this experiment}@}, {@{a [coherent light source](coherence%20(physics).md), such as a [laser](laser.md) beam}@}, illuminates {@{a plate pierced by two parallel slits}@}, and {@{the light passing through the slits}@} is observed {@{on a screen behind the plate}@}.<sup>[\[8\]](#^ref-8)</sup><sup>:&hairsp;102–111&hairsp;</sup><sup>[\[2\]](#^ref-2)</sup><sup>:&hairsp;1.1–1.8&hairsp;</sup> {@{The wave nature of light}@} causes the light waves {@{passing through the two slits to [interfere](interference%20(wave%20propagation).md)}@}, producing {@{bright and dark bands on the screen}@} – a result that would {@{not be expected if light consisted of classical particles}@}.<sup>[\[8\]](#^ref-8)</sup> However, {@{the light is always found}@} to be {@{absorbed at the screen at discrete points}@}, as {@{individual particles rather than waves}@}; {@{the interference pattern}@} appears via {@{the varying density of these particle hits on the screen}@}. Furthermore, versions of the experiment that {@{include detectors at the slits}@} find that {@{each detected [photon](photon.md) passes through one slit \(as would a classical particle\)}@}, and not {@{through both slits \(as would a wave\)}@}.<sup>[\[8\]](#^ref-8)</sup><sup>:&hairsp;109&hairsp;</sup><sup>[\[9\]](#^ref-9)</sup><sup>[\[10\]](#^ref-10)</sup> However, [such experiments](double-slit%20experiment.md#which%20way) demonstrate that particles {@{do not form the interference pattern}@} if one {@{detects which slit they pass through}@}. This behavior is known as {@{[wave–particle duality](wave–particle%20duality.md)}@}. In addition to {@{light, [electrons](electrons.md), [atoms](atoms.md), and [molecules](molecules.md)}@} are all {@{found to exhibit the same dual behavior when fired towards a double slit}@}.<sup>[\[2\]](#^ref-2)</sup>

> {@{A simplified diagram of [quantum tunneling](quantum%20tunneling.md)}@}, a phenomenon by which a particle may {@{move through a barrier which would be impossible under classical mechanics}@}

{@{Another non-classical phenomenon predicted}@} by quantum mechanics is {@{[quantum tunnelling](quantum%20tunnelling.md)}@}: {@{a particle that goes up against a [potential barrier](potential%20barrier.md)}@} can {@{cross it, even if its kinetic energy is smaller than the maximum of the potential}@}.<sup>[\[11\]](#^ref-11)</sup> In {@{classical mechanics}@} this particle {@{would be trapped}@}. {@{Quantum tunnelling}@} has {@{several important consequences}@}, enabling {@{[radioactive decay](radioactive%20decay.md), [nuclear fusion](nuclear%20fusion.md)}@} in stars, and applications such as {@{[scanning tunnelling microscopy](scanning%20tunnelling%20microscopy.md), [tunnel diode](tunnel%20diode.md) and [tunnel field-effect transistor](tunnel%20field-effect%20transistor.md)}@}.<sup>[\[12\]](#^ref-12)</sup><sup>[\[13\]](#^ref-13)</sup>

When {@{quantum systems interact}@}, the result can be {@{the creation of [quantum entanglement](quantum%20entanglement.md)}@}: their {@{properties become so intertwined}@} that a description of {@{the whole solely in terms of the individual parts is no longer possible}@}. {@{Erwin Schrödinger called entanglement}@} "...{@{_the_ characteristic trait of quantum mechanics}@}, the one that enforces its {@{entire departure from classical lines of thought}@}".<sup>[\[14\]](#^ref-14)</sup> {@{Quantum entanglement}@} enables {@{[quantum computing](quantum%20computing.md) and is part of quantum communication protocols}@}, such as {@{[quantum key distribution](quantum%20key%20distribution.md) and [superdense coding](superdense%20coding.md)}@}.<sup>[\[15\]](#^ref-15)</sup> Contrary to {@{popular misconception}@}, entanglement does not {@{allow sending signals [faster than light](faster%20than%20light.md)}@}, as demonstrated by {@{the [no-communication theorem](no-communication%20theorem.md)}@}.<sup>[\[15\]](#^ref-15)</sup>

{@{Another possibility opened}@} by entanglement is testing for {@{"[hidden variables](hidden%20variable%20theory.md)"}@}, {@{hypothetical properties more fundamental than the quantities addressed}@} in quantum theory itself, knowledge of which would {@{allow more exact predictions than quantum theory provides}@}. {@{A collection of results, most significantly [Bell's theorem](Bell's%20theorem.md)}@}, have demonstrated that broad classes of {@{such hidden-variable theories are in fact incompatible with quantum physics}@}. According to {@{Bell's theorem}@}, if nature actually {@{operates in accord with any theory of _local_ hidden variables}@}, then {@{the results of a [Bell test](Bell%20test.md)}@} will be {@{constrained in a particular, quantifiable way}@}. {@{Many Bell tests}@} have been {@{performed and they have shown results incompatible with the constraints imposed by local hidden variables}@}.<sup>[\[16\]](#^ref-16)</sup><sup>[\[17\]](#^ref-17)</sup>

It is not {@{possible to present these concepts in more than a superficial way}@} without {@{introducing the mathematics involved}@}; {@{understanding quantum mechanics}@} requires not {@{only manipulating complex numbers}@}, but also {@{[linear algebra](linear%20algebra.md), [differential equations](differential%20equation.md), [group theory](group%20theory.md), and other more advanced subjects}@}.<sup>[\[18\]](#^ref-18)</sup><sup>[\[19\]](#^ref-19)</sup> Accordingly, {@{this article}@} will present {@{a mathematical formulation of quantum mechanics}@} and survey its {@{application to some useful and oft-studied examples}@}.

## mathematical formulation

- Main article: ::@:: [Mathematical formulation of quantum mechanics](mathematical%20formulation%20of%20quantum%20mechanics.md)

In {@{the mathematically rigorous formulation of quantum mechanics}@}, {@{the state of a quantum mechanical system}@} is {@{a vector $\psi$ belonging to a \([separable](separable%20space.md)\) complex [Hilbert space](Hilbert%20space.md) ${\mathcal {H} }$}@}. {@{This vector is postulated}@} to be {@{normalized under the Hilbert space inner product}@}, that is, it obeys {@{$\langle \psi ,\psi \rangle =1$}@}, and it is {@{well-defined up to a complex number of modulus 1 \(the global phase\)}@}, that is, {@{$\psi$ and $e^{i\alpha }\psi$ represent the same physical system}@}. In other words, {@{the possible states}@} are {@{points in the [projective space](projective%20space.md) of a Hilbert space}@}, usually called {@{the [complex projective space](complex%20projective%20space.md)}@}. {@{The exact nature of this Hilbert space}@} is {@{dependent on the system}@} – for example, for describing {@{position and momentum}@} the Hilbert space is {@{the space of complex [square-integrable](square-integrable.md) functions $L^{2}(\mathbb {C} )$}@},<sup>[\[20\]](#^ref-20)</sup><sup>:&hairsp;13&hairsp;</sup> while the Hilbert space for {@{the [spin](spin%20(physics).md) of a single proton}@} is simply {@{the space of two-dimensional complex vectors $\mathbb {C} ^{2}$ with the usual inner product}@}.<sup>[\[20\]](#^ref-20)</sup><sup>:&hairsp;20&hairsp;</sup>

{@{Physical quantities}@} of interest – {@{position, momentum, energy, spin}@} – are {@{represented by observables}@}, which are {@{[Hermitian](Hermitian%20adjoint.md#Hermitian%20operators) \(more precisely, [self-adjoint](self-adjoint%20operator.md)\) linear [operators](operator%20(physics).md) acting on the Hilbert space}@}.<sup>[\[20\]](#^ref-20)</sup><sup>:&hairsp;17&hairsp;</sup> {@{A quantum state}@} can be {@{an [eigenvector](eigenvector.md) of an observable}@}, in which case it is called {@{an [eigenstate](eigenstate.md#pure%20state), and the associated [eigenvalue](eigenvalue.md)}@} corresponds to {@{the value of the observable in that eigenstate}@}. More generally, {@{a quantum state}@} will be {@{a linear combination of the eigenstates}@}, known as {@{a [quantum superposition](quantum%20superposition.md)}@}. When {@{an observable is measured}@}, the result will be {@{one of its eigenvalues with probability given by the [Born rule](Born%20rule.md)}@}: in {@{the simplest case}@} {@{the eigenvalue $\lambda$ is non-degenerate}@} and the probability is given by {@{$|\langle {\vec {\lambda } },\psi \rangle |^{2}$}@}, where ${\vec {\lambda } }$ is {@{its associated unit-length eigenvector}@}. More generally, {@{the eigenvalue is degenerate}@} and the probability is given by {@{$\langle \psi ,P_{\lambda }\psi \rangle$, where $P_{\lambda }$ is the projector onto its associated eigenspace}@}.<sup>[\[21\]](#^ref-21)</sup> In {@{the continuous case}@}, these formulas give {@{instead the [probability density](probability%20density.md)}@}.

After {@{the [measurement](measurement%20in%20quantum%20mechanics.md)}@}, if {@{result $\lambda$ was obtained}@}, {@{the quantum state}@} is {@{postulated to [collapse](collapse%20of%20the%20wavefunction.md) to ${\vec {\lambda } }$, in the non-degenerate case}@}, or to {@{$P_{\lambda }\psi {\big /}\!{\sqrt {\langle \psi ,P_{\lambda }\psi \rangle } }$, in the general case}@}. {@{The [probabilistic](probabilistic.md) nature}@} of quantum mechanics thus {@{stems from the act of measurement}@}. This is one of {@{the most debated aspects of quantum theory}@}, with different {@{[interpretations of quantum mechanics](interpretations%20of%20quantum%20mechanics.md) giving radically different answers}@} to {@{questions regarding quantum-state collapse, as discussed [below](#philosophical%20implications)}@}.

### time evolution of a quantum state

{@{The time evolution of a quantum state}@} is described by {@{the Schrödinger equation}@}: {@{$$i\hbar {\frac {\partial }{\partial t} }\psi (t)=H\psi (t).$$}@} Here $H$ denotes {@{the [Hamiltonian](Hamiltonian%20(quantum%20mechanics).md)}@}, {@{the observable corresponding to the [total energy](total%20energy.md)}@} of the system, and $\hbar$ is {@{the reduced [Planck constant](Planck%20constant.md)}@}. {@{The constant $i\hbar$}@} is introduced so that {@{the Hamiltonian is reduced to the [classical Hamiltonian](Hamiltonian%20mechanics.md)}@} in cases where {@{the quantum system can be approximated by a classical system}@}; the ability to {@{make such an approximation in certain limits}@} is called {@{the [correspondence principle](correspondence%20principle.md)}@}.

{@{The solution of this differential equation (annotation: $i\hbar {\frac {\partial }{\partial t} }\psi(t)=H\psi (t)$)}@} is given by {@{$$\psi (t)=e^{-iHt/\hbar }\psi (0).$$}@} {@{The operator $U(t)=e^{-iHt/\hbar }$}@} is known as {@{the time-evolution operator}@}, and has {@{the crucial property that it is [unitary](unitarity%20(physics).md)}@}. {@{This time evolution is [deterministic](deterministic.md)}@} in the sense that – given {@{an initial quantum state $\psi (0)$}@} – it makes {@{a definite prediction of what the quantum state $\psi (t)$ will be at any later time}@}.<sup>[\[22\]](#^ref-22)</sup>

> {@{![Fig. 1: [Probability densities](probability%20density%20function.md) corresponding to the wave functions of an electron in a hydrogen atom possessing definite energy levels \(increasing from the top of the image to the bottom: _n_ = 1, 2, 3, ...\) and angular momenta \(increasing across from left to right: _s_, _p_, _d_, ...\).](../../archives/Wikimedia%20Commons/Atomic-orbital-clouds%20spd%20m0.png)}@}
>
> Fig. 1: {@{[Probability densities](probability%20density%20function.md)}@} corresponding to {@{the wave functions of an electron in a hydrogen atom}@} possessing {@{definite energy levels \(increasing from the top of the image to the bottom: _n_ = 1, 2, 3, ...\)}@} and {@{angular momenta \(increasing across from left to right: _s_, _p_, _d_, ...\)}@}. {@{Denser areas}@} correspond to {@{higher probability density in a position measurement}@}. {@{Such wave functions}@} are directly comparable to {@{[Chladni's figures](Chladni's%20figures.md#Chladni%20figures) of [acoustic](acoustics.md) modes of vibration in classical physics}@} and are {@{modes of oscillation}@} as well, possessing {@{a sharp energy and thus, a definite frequency}@}. {@{The [angular momentum](angular%20momentum.md) and energy}@} are {@{[quantized](quantization%20(physics).md) and take _only_ discrete values like those shown}@} – as is the case for {@{[resonant frequencies](resonant%20frequencies.md) in acoustics}@}.

{@{Some wave functions}@} produce {@{probability distributions that are independent of time}@}, such as {@{[eigenstates](eigenstate.md#pure%20state) of the Hamiltonian}@}.<sup>[\[7\]](#^ref-7)</sup><sup>:&hairsp;133–137&hairsp;</sup> {@{Many systems that are treated dynamically}@} in classical mechanics are described by {@{such "static" wave functions}@}. For example, {@{a single electron in an unexcited atom}@} is pictured {@{classically as a particle moving in a circular trajectory around the [atomic nucleus](atomic%20nucleus.md)}@}, whereas in quantum mechanics, it is described by {@{a static wave function surrounding the nucleus}@}. For example, {@{the electron wave function for an unexcited hydrogen atom}@} is {@{a spherically symmetric function known as an [_s_ orbital](atomic%20orbital.md)}@} \([Fig. 1](#fig1)\).

{@{Analytic solutions}@} of the Schrödinger equation are known for {@{[very few relatively simple model Hamiltonians](list%20of%20quantum-mechanical%20systems%20with%20analytical%20solutions.md)}@} including {@{the [quantum harmonic oscillator](quantum%20harmonic%20oscillator.md), the [particle in a box](particle%20in%20a%20box.md)}@}, {@{the [dihydrogen cation](dihydrogen%20cation.md), and the [hydrogen atom](hydrogen%20atom.md)}@}. Even {@{the [helium](helium.md) atom – which contains just two electrons}@} – has defied {@{all attempts at a fully analytic treatment, admitting no solution in [closed form](closed-form%20expression.md)}@}.<sup>[\[23\]](#^ref-23)</sup><sup>[\[24\]](#^ref-24)</sup><sup>[\[25\]](#^ref-25)</sup>

However, there are {@{techniques for finding approximate solutions}@}. {@{One method, called [perturbation theory](perturbation%20theory%20(quantum%20mechanics).md)}@}, uses {@{the analytic result for a simple quantum mechanical model}@} to create {@{a result for a related but more complicated model by \(for example\) the addition of a weak [potential energy](potential%20energy.md)}@}.<sup>[\[7\]](#^ref-7)</sup><sup>:&hairsp;793&hairsp;</sup> {@{Another approximation method}@} applies to systems for which {@{quantum mechanics produces only small deviations from classical behavior}@}. {@{These deviations}@} can then be {@{computed based on the classical motion}@}.<sup>[\[7\]](#^ref-7)</sup><sup>:&hairsp;849&hairsp;</sup>

### uncertainty principle

{@{One consequence of the basic quantum formalism}@} is {@{the uncertainty principle}@}. In its {@{most familiar form}@}, this states that {@{no preparation of a quantum particle}@} can imply {@{simultaneously precise predictions both for a measurement of its position and for a measurement of its momentum}@}.<sup>[\[26\]](#^ref-26)</sup><sup>[\[27\]](#^ref-27)</sup> Both {@{position and momentum are observables}@}, meaning that they are {@{represented by [Hermitian operators](Hermitian%20operators.md)}@}. {@{The position operator ${\hat {X} }$ and momentum operator ${\hat {P} }$}@} {@{do not commute}@}, but rather satisfy {@{the [canonical commutation relation](canonical%20commutation%20relation.md)}@}: {@{$$[{\hat {X} },{\hat {P} }]=i\hbar .$$}@} Given {@{a quantum state}@}, {@{the Born rule}@} lets us compute {@{expectation values for both $X$ and $P$, and moreover for powers of them}@}. Defining {@{the uncertainty for an observable by a [standard deviation](standard%20deviation.md)}@}, we have {@{$$\sigma _{X}={\textstyle {\sqrt {\left\langle X^{2}\right\rangle -\left\langle X\right\rangle ^{2} } } },$$}@} and likewise for {@{the momentum: $$\sigma _{P}={\sqrt {\left\langle P^{2}\right\rangle -\left\langle P\right\rangle ^{2} } }.$$}@} {@{The uncertainty principle}@} states that {@{$$\sigma _{X}\sigma _{P}\geq {\frac {\hbar }{2} }.$$}@} Either {@{standard deviation can in principle be made arbitrarily small}@}, but not {@{both simultaneously}@}.<sup>[\[28\]](#^ref-28)</sup> {@{This inequality}@} generalizes to {@{arbitrary pairs of self-adjoint operators $A$ and $B$}@}. {@{The [commutator](commutator.md) of these two operators}@} is {@{$$[A,B]=AB-BA,$$}@} and this provides {@{the lower bound on the product of standard deviations}@}: {@{$$\sigma _{A}\sigma _{B}\geq {\tfrac {1}{2} }\left|{\bigl \langle }[A,B]{\bigr \rangle }\right|.$$}@}

{@{Another consequence of the canonical commutation relation}@} is that {@{the position and momentum operators}@} are {@{[Fourier transforms](Fourier%20transform.md#uncertainty%20principle) of each other}@}, so that {@{a description of an object according to its momentum}@} is {@{the Fourier transform of its description according to its position}@}. The fact that {@{dependence in momentum is the Fourier transform of the dependence in position}@} means that the momentum operator is {@{equivalent \(up to an $i/\hbar$ factor\) to taking the derivative according to the position}@}, since in {@{Fourier analysis [differentiation corresponds to multiplication in the dual space](Fourier%20transform.md#differentiation)}@}. This is why in {@{quantum equations in position space}@}, {@{the momentum $p_{i}$ is replaced by $-i\hbar {\frac {\partial }{\partial x} }$}@}, and in particular in {@{the [non-relativistic Schrödinger equation in position space](Schrödinger%20equation.md#equation)}@} {@{the momentum-squared term}@} is {@{replaced with a Laplacian times $-\hbar ^{2}$}@}.<sup>[\[26\]](#^ref-26)</sup>

### composite systems and entanglement

When two {@{different quantum systems are considered together}@}, {@{the Hilbert space of the combined system}@} is {@{the [tensor product](tensor%20product.md) of the Hilbert spaces of the two components}@}. For example, let _A_ and _B_ be {@{two quantum systems, with Hilbert spaces ${\mathcal {H} }_{A}$ and ${\mathcal {H} }_{B}$, respectively}@}. {@{The Hilbert space of the composite system}@} is then {@{$${\mathcal {H} }_{AB}={\mathcal {H} }_{A}\otimes {\mathcal {H} }_{B}.$$}@} If {@{the state for the first system}@} is {@{the vector $\psi _{A}$ and the state for the second system is $\psi _{B}$}@}, then {@{the state of the composite system}@} is {@{$$\psi _{A}\otimes \psi _{B}.$$}@} Not {@{all states in the joint Hilbert space ${\mathcal {H} }_{AB}$}@} can be {@{written in this form}@}, however, because {@{the superposition principle}@} implies that {@{linear combinations of these "separable" or "product states" are also valid}@}. For example, if {@{$\psi _{A}$ and $\phi _{A}$}@} are {@{both possible states for system $A$, and likewise $\psi _{B}$ and $\phi _{B}$ are both possible states for system $B$}@}, then {@{$${\tfrac {1}{\sqrt {2} } }\left(\psi _{A}\otimes \psi _{B}+\phi _{A}\otimes \phi _{B}\right)$$}@} is {@{a valid joint state that is not separable}@}. {@{States that are not separable}@} are called {@{[entangled](quantum%20entanglement.md)}@}.<sup>[\[29\]](#^ref-29)</sup><sup>[\[30\]](#^ref-30)</sup>

If {@{the state for a composite system is entangled}@}, it is impossible to describe {@{either component system _A_ or system _B_ by a state vector}@}. One can instead define {@{[reduced density matrices](reduced%20density%20matrix.md#reduced%20density%20matrices)}@} that describe {@{the statistics that can be obtained}@} by making {@{measurements on either component system alone}@}. This necessarily {@{causes a loss of information}@}, though: knowing {@{the reduced density matrices of the individual systems}@} is not {@{enough to reconstruct the state of the composite system}@}.<sup>[\[29\]](#^ref-29)</sup><sup>[\[30\]](#^ref-30)</sup> Just as {@{density matrices}@} specify {@{the state of a subsystem of a larger system}@}, analogously, {@{[positive operator-valued measures](POVM.md) \(POVMs\)}@} describe {@{the effect on a subsystem of a measurement performed on a larger system}@}. {@{POVMs are extensively used}@} in {@{quantum information theory}@}.<sup>[\[29\]](#^ref-29)</sup><sup>[\[31\]](#^ref-31)</sup>

As described above, {@{entanglement}@} is {@{a key feature of models of measurement processes}@} in which {@{an apparatus becomes entangled with the system being measured}@}. {@{Systems interacting with the environment}@} in which they reside generally {@{become entangled with that environment}@}, a phenomenon known as {@{[quantum decoherence](quantum%20decoherence.md)}@}. This can explain why, {@{in practice, quantum effects}@} are difficult to {@{observe in systems larger than microscopic}@}.<sup>[\[32\]](#^ref-32)</sup>

### equivalence between formulations

There are many {@{mathematically equivalent formulations of quantum mechanics}@}. {@{One of the oldest and most common}@} is {@{the "[transformation theory](transformation%20theory%20(quantum%20mechanics).md)" proposed by [Paul Dirac](Paul%20Dirac.md)}@}, which {@{unifies and generalizes the two earliest formulations of quantum mechanics}@} – {@{[matrix mechanics](matrix%20mechanics.md) \(invented by [Werner Heisenberg](Werner%20Heisenberg.md)\)}@} and {@{wave mechanics \(invented by [Erwin Schrödinger](Erwin%20Schrödinger.md)\)}@}.<sup>[\[33\]](#^ref-33)</sup> {@{An alternative formulation}@} of quantum mechanics is {@{[Feynman](feynman.md)'s [path integral formulation](path%20integral%20formulation.md)}@}, in which {@{a quantum-mechanical amplitude}@} is considered as {@{a sum over all possible classical and non-classical paths between the initial and final states}@}. This is {@{the quantum-mechanical counterpart}@} of {@{the [action principle](action%20principle.md) in classical mechanics}@}.<sup>[\[34\]](#^ref-34)</sup>

### symmetries and conservation laws

- Main article: ::@:: [Noether's theorem](Noether's%20theorem.md)

{@{The Hamiltonian $H$}@} is known as {@{the _generator_ of time evolution}@}, since it defines {@{a unitary time-evolution operator $U(t)=e^{-iHt/\hbar }$}@} for each {@{value of $t$}@}. From {@{this relation between $U(t)$ and $H$}@}, it follows that {@{any observable $A$ that commutes with $H$}@} will be {@{_conserved_: its expectation value will not change over time}@}.<sup>[\[7\]](#^ref-7)</sup><sup>:&hairsp;471&hairsp;</sup> (annotation: This is due to {@{$\frac{d}{dt}\langle A\rangle(t)=\frac{i}{\hbar}\,\langle[H,A]\rangle(t), \qquad \langle A\rangle(t)=\langle\psi(t)|A|\psi(t)\rangle$}@}, which is {@{the average measurement value of $A$}@} in {@{the evolving state $|\psi(t)\rangle$}@}. To derive the result, we differentiate {@{the expectation value $\langle A \rangle = \langle \psi | A | \psi \rangle$ with respect to time using the product rule}@}. By substituting {@{the Schrödinger equation, $\partial_t | \psi \rangle = \frac{1}{i\hbar} H | \psi \rangle$, and its adjoint, $\partial_t \langle \psi | = -\frac{1}{i\hbar} \langle \psi | H$}@}, we obtain: {@{$$\frac{d}{dt} \langle A \rangle = \frac{1}{i\hbar} \langle \psi | AH | \psi \rangle - \frac{1}{i\hbar} \langle \psi | HA | \psi \rangle + \langle \psi | \frac{\partial A}{\partial t} | \psi \rangle$$}@} Assuming $A$ has {@{no explicit time dependence}@}, factoring out {@{$\frac{1}{i\hbar}$ yields the commutator $[A, H] = AH - HA$}@}. {@{The commutator $[H,A]$}@} measures how {@{the Hamiltonian changes $A$ under time evolution}@}; if {@{$[H,A]=0$}@}, the Hamiltonian leaves {@{$A$ unaffected, the derivative is zero}@}, and the average value of $A$ {@{remains constant in time}@}.) This statement {@{generalizes}@}, as {@{mathematically, any Hermitian operator $A$}@} can generate {@{a family of unitary operators parameterized by a variable $t$}@}. Under {@{the evolution generated by $A$}@}, any {@{observable $B$ that commutes with $A$ will be conserved}@}. Moreover, if $B$ is {@{conserved by evolution under $A$}@}, then $A$ is {@{conserved under the evolution generated by $B$}@}. This implies {@{a quantum version of the result proven by [Emmy Noether](Emmy%20Noether.md) in classical \([Lagrangian](Lagrangian%20mechanics.md)\) mechanics}@}: for every {@{[differentiable](differentiable.md) [symmetry](symmetry%20(physics).md) of a Hamiltonian}@}, there exists {@{a corresponding [conservation law](conservation%20law.md)}@}.

## examples

### free particle

- Main article: ::@:: [Free particle](free%20particle.md)

> {@{![Position space probability density of a Gaussian [wave packet](wave%20packet.md) moving in one dimension in free space](../../archives/Wikimedia%20Commons/Guassian%20Dispersion.gif)}@}
>
> {@{Position space probability density}@} of {@{a Gaussian [wave packet](wave%20packet.md)}@} moving in {@{one dimension in free space}@}

{@{The simplest example of a quantum system}@} with {@{a position degree of freedom}@} is {@{a free particle in a single spatial dimension}@}. {@{A free particle}@} is one which is {@{not subject to external influences}@}, so that its {@{Hamiltonian consists only of its kinetic energy}@}: {@{$$H={\frac {1}{2m} }P^{2}=-{\frac {\hbar ^{2} }{2m} }{\frac {d^{2} }{dx^{2} } }.$$}@} (annotation: {@{A free particle's Hamiltonian}@} contains {@{only kinetic energy because}@}, with no {@{external forces or potentials acting on it}@}, there is no {@{potential energy to include}@}. And recall that $P$ is {@{the momentum operator}@}, which can alternatively be written as {@{$-i\hbar {\frac {d}{dx} }$}@} in {@{position space}@}.) {@{The general solution of the Schrödinger equation}@} is given by {@{$$\psi (x,t)={\frac {1}{\sqrt {2\pi } } }\int _{-\infty }^{\infty }{\hat {\psi } }(k,0)e^{i(kx-{\frac {\hbar k^{2} }{2m} }t)}\mathrm {d} k,$$}@} (annotation: We can start from {@{the initial state $\psi (x,0)$}@} and {@{take its Fourier transform to get ${\hat {\psi } }(k,0)$}@}. Note since we are using {@{the unitary Fourier transform in angular frequency}@} that does not {@{divide by $\hbar$ in the exponent}@}, that means the resulting $k$ is {@{the wave number, which is related to the momentum by $p=\hbar k$}@}. {@{The time evolution of the state in position space}@} is applying {@{the time-evolution operator $U(t)=e^{-iHt/\hbar }$}@} to {@{the initial state $\psi (x,0)$}@}. Now note this operator is {@{a constant with respect to $x$}@}, so after taking the Fourier transform, {@{the time evolution of the state in momentum space}@} is {@{still given by applying $U(t)$ to the initial state ${\hat {\psi } }(k,0)$}@}. As $H$ can be {@{written as $\frac 1 {2m} P^2$}@}, and $P$ is {@{the momentum operator}@}, so {@{the operator $e^{-i P^2 t/{2m \hbar} }$}@} acts {@{as multiplication by $e^{-i \hbar k^2 t/{2m} }$ in momentum space}@} since the momentum operator is just {@{multiplication by $\hbar k$ in momentum space}@}, and by {@{the spectral theorem and Borel functional calculus}@}: if $A$ is {@{self‑adjoint with spectral measure $E$}@}, then for any {@{bounded Borel $f$}@}, {@{$f(A)=\int_{\mathbb{R} } f(\lambda)\,dE(\lambda)$}@}, where $\lambda$ is {@{the spectral parameter}@}, which in {@{the spectral representation}@} acts as {@{multiplication by $f(\lambda)$}@}. Finally, {@{the inverse Fourier transform of this evolved state in momentum space}@} gives us {@{the evolved state in position space}@}.) which is {@{a superposition of all possible [plane waves](plane%20wave.md) $e^{i(kx-{\frac {\hbar k^{2} }{2m} }t)}$}@}, which are {@{eigenstates of the momentum operator with momentum $p=\hbar k$}@}. (annotation: Actually {@{the term $e^{ikx}$}@} is {@{already an eigenstate of the momentum operator}@}, and {@{$e^{-i\hbar k^2 t/{2m} }$}@} is {@{just a time-dependent phase factor}@} that is {@{a constant with respect to $x$}@}, and so does not {@{change the fact that $e^{ikx}$ is an eigenstate of the momentum operator}@}.) {@{The coefficients of the superposition}@} are {@{${\hat {\psi } }(k,0)$}@} (annotation: intuitively interpreted as {@{the amplitude for the particle to have momentum $p=\hbar k$ at time $t=0$}@}), which is {@{the Fourier transform of the initial quantum state $\psi (x,0)$}@} (annotation: intuitively interpreted as {@{the amplitude for the particle to be at position $x$ at time $t=0$}@}).

It is not {@{possible for the solution to be a single momentum eigenstate}@}, or {@{a single position eigenstate}@}, as these are {@{not normalizable quantum states}@}.<sup>[\[note 1\]](#^ref-note%201)</sup> Instead, we can consider {@{a Gaussian [wave packet](wave%20packet.md)}@}: {@{$$\psi (x,0)={\frac {1}{\sqrt[{4}]{\pi a} } }e^{-{\frac {x^{2} }{2a} } }$$}@} (annotation: {@{The weird factor $\frac 1 {\sqrt[4]{\pi} }$}@} when $a = 1$ is {@{just a normalization constant}@} to ensure that {@{the state is normalized under $L^2$ inner product}@}), which has {@{Fourier transform, and therefore momentum distribution}@} {@{$${\hat {\psi } }(k,0)={\sqrt[{4}]{\frac {a}{\pi } } }e^{-{\frac {ak^{2} }{2} } }.$$}@} (annotation: Recall {@{the Fourier transform relation}@} {@{$f(ax) \overset {\mathcal F} \Longleftrightarrow \frac{1}{|a|} f\left(\frac{\xi}{a}\right)$}@}.) We see that as we make {@{$a$ smaller}@} {@{the spread in position gets smaller}@}, but {@{the spread in momentum gets larger}@}. Conversely, by making {@{$a$ larger we make the spread in momentum smaller}@}, but {@{the spread in position gets larger}@}. This illustrates {@{the uncertainty principle}@}.

As we let {@{the Gaussian wave packet evolve in time}@}, we see that its {@{center moves through space at a constant velocity}@} \(like {@{a classical particle with no forces acting on it}@}\). However, {@{the wave packet}@} will also {@{spread out as time progresses}@}, which means that {@{the position becomes more and more uncertain}@}. {@{The uncertainty in momentum}@}, however, {@{stays constant}@}.<sup>[\[35\]](#^ref-35)</sup>

### particle in a box

> {@{![1-dimensional potential energy box \(or infinite potential well\)](../../archives/Wikimedia%20Commons/Infinite%20potential%20well.svg)}@}
>
> 1-dimensional potential energy box \(or infinite potential well\)

- Main article: ::@:: [Particle in a box](particle%20in%20a%20box.md)

{@{The particle in a one-dimensional potential energy box}@} is {@{the most mathematically simple example}@} where restraints {@{lead to the quantization of energy levels}@}. {@{The box}@} is defined as having {@{zero potential energy everywhere _inside_ a certain region}@}, and therefore {@{infinite potential energy everywhere _outside_ that region}@}.<sup>[\[26\]](#^ref-26)</sup><sup>:&hairsp;77–78&hairsp;</sup> For {@{the one-dimensional case in the $x$ direction}@}, {@{the time-independent Schrödinger equation}@} may be written {@{$$-{\frac {\hbar ^{2} }{2m} }{\frac {d^{2}\psi }{dx^{2} } }=E\psi .$$}@} (annotation: {@{The time-independent Schrödinger equation}@} is {@{the eigenvalue equation for the Hamiltonian}@}: {@{$H\psi =E\psi$}@}, where $E$ is {@{the energy eigenvalue}@}. This is used when the Hamiltonian {@{does not depend on time}@}.) With {@{the differential operator}@} defined by {@{$${\hat {p} }_{x}=-i\hbar {\frac {d}{dx} }$$}@} the previous equation is {@{evocative of the [classic kinetic energy analogue](kinetic%20energy.md#kinetic%20energy%20of%20rigid%20bodies)}@}, {@{$${\frac {1}{2m} }{\hat {p} }_{x}^{2}=E,$$}@} with {@{state $\psi$ in this case having energy $E$}@} coincident with {@{the kinetic energy of the particle}@}.

{@{The general solutions of the Schrödinger equation}@} for {@{the particle in a box}@} are {@{$$\psi (x)=Ae^{ikx}+Be^{-ikx}\qquad \qquad E={\frac {\hbar ^{2}k^{2} }{2m} }$$}@} (annotation: The above equation is {@{easy to solve}@} by {@{the method of characteristic equations}@}. The characteristic equation is {@{$-{\frac {\hbar ^{2} }{2m} }r^{2} - E = 0$}@}, which has {@{two roots $r=ik$ and $r=-ik$}@} where {@{$k={\sqrt {\frac {2mE}{\hbar ^{2} } } }$}@}. {@{The general solution}@} is {@{a linear combination of the two independent solutions $e^{ikx}$ and $e^{-ikx}$}@}.) or, from {@{[Euler's formula](Euler's%20formula.md)}@}, {@{$$\psi (x)=C\sin(kx)+D\cos(kx).\!$$}@} (annotation: This is simply {@{a change of basis}@} from {@{the $e^{ikx}$ and $e^{-ikx}$ basis}@} to {@{the $\sin(kx)$ and $\cos(kx)$ basis}@} via the equation {@{$\begin{bmatrix}C\\D\end{bmatrix}=\begin{bmatrix}1/2i & -1/2i\\1/2 & 1/2\end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix}A\\B\end{bmatrix}$}@}, which is {@{a more convenient basis for applying the boundary conditions}@} in this problem.)

{@{The infinite potential walls}@} of the box determine {@{the values of $C,D$, and $k$}@} at {@{$x=0$ and $x=L$ where $\psi$ must be zero}@}. Thus, at {@{$x=0$}@}, {@{$$\psi (0)=0=C\sin(0)+D\cos(0)=D$$}@} and {@{$D=0$}@}. At {@{$x=L$}@}, {@{$$\psi (L)=0=C\sin(kL),$$}@} in which {@{$C$ cannot be zero}@} as this would {@{conflict with the postulate that $\psi$ has norm 1}@}. Therefore, since {@{$\sin(kL)=0$, $kL$ must be an integer multiple of $\pi$}@}, {@{$$k={\frac {n\pi }{L} }\qquad \qquad n=1,2,3,\ldots .$$}@} {@{This constraint on $k$}@} implies {@{a constraint on the energy levels}@}, yielding {@{$$E_{n}={\frac {\hbar ^{2}\pi ^{2}n^{2} }{2mL^{2} } }={\frac {n^{2}h^{2} }{8mL^{2} } }.$$}@} (annotation: Recall {@{$E = \frac {p^2} {2m} = \frac{\hbar^2 k^2}{2m}$ with $p = \hbar k$}@}.)

{@{A [finite potential well](finite%20potential%20well.md)}@} is {@{the generalization of the infinite potential well problem}@} to {@{potential wells having finite depth}@}. {@{The finite potential well problem}@} is mathematically {@{more complicated than the infinite particle-in-a-box problem}@} as {@{the wave function}@} is not {@{pinned to zero at the walls of the well}@}. Instead, {@{the wave function}@} must satisfy {@{more complicated mathematical boundary conditions}@} as it is {@{nonzero in regions outside the well}@}. {@{Another related problem}@} is that of {@{the [rectangular potential barrier](rectangular%20potential%20barrier.md)}@}, which furnishes {@{a model for the [quantum tunneling](quantum%20tunneling.md) effect}@} that plays an important role in {@{the performance of modern technologies}@} such as {@{[flash memory](flash%20memory.md) and [scanning tunneling microscopy](scanning%20tunneling%20microscopy.md)}@}.

### harmonic oscillator

- Main article: ::@:: [Quantum harmonic oscillator](quantum%20harmonic%20oscillator.md)

> {@{![Some trajectories of a [harmonic oscillator](harmonic%20oscillator.md) \(i.e. a ball attached to a [spring](Hooke's%20law.md)\) in [classical mechanics](classical%20mechanics.md) \(A-B\) and quantum mechanics \(C-H\).](../../archives/Wikimedia%20Commons/QuantumHarmonicOscillatorAnimation.gif)}@}
>
> {@{Some trajectories}@} of {@{a [harmonic oscillator](harmonic%20oscillator.md)}@} \(i.e. {@{a ball attached to a [spring](Hooke's%20law.md)}@}\) in {@{[classical mechanics](classical%20mechanics.md) \(A-B\) and quantum mechanics \(C-H\)}@}. In {@{quantum mechanics}@}, {@{the position of the ball}@} is represented by {@{a [wave](wave.md) \(called the wave function\)}@}, with {@{the [real part](real%20part.md) shown in blue and the [imaginary part](imaginary%20part.md) shown in red}@}. {@{Some of the trajectories \(such as C, D, E, and F\)}@} are {@{[standing waves](standing%20wave.md) \(or "[stationary states](stationary%20state.md)"\)}@}. {@{Each standing-wave frequency}@} is proportional to {@{a possible [energy level](energy%20level.md) of the oscillator}@}. {@{This "energy quantization"}@} does not {@{occur in classical physics}@}, where the oscillator can {@{have _any_ energy}@}.

As in {@{the classical case}@}, {@{the potential for the quantum harmonic oscillator}@} is given by<sup>[\[7\]](#^ref-7)</sup><sup>:&hairsp;234&hairsp;</sup> {@{$$V(x)={\frac {1}{2} }m\omega ^{2}x^{2}.$$}@} (annotation: Alternatively, {@{$V(x) = \frac 1 2 k x^2$}@} where {@{$k = m \omega^2$ is the spring constant}@}.)

{@{This problem}@} can either be treated by directly {@{solving the Schrödinger equation, which is not trivial}@}, (annotation: {@{The time-independent Schrödinger equation}@} for the harmonic oscillator is {@{$-\frac {\hbar^2}{2m} \frac{d^2}{dx^2} \psi + \frac{1}{2} m \omega^2 x^2 \psi = E \psi$}@}. This is {@{a second-order linear ordinary differential equation}@}.) or by using {@{the more elegant "ladder method" first proposed by Paul Dirac}@}. {@{The [eigenstates](eigenstate.md#pure%20state)}@} are given by {@{$$\psi _{n}(x)={\sqrt {\frac {1}{2^{n}\,n!} } }\cdot \left({\frac {m\omega }{\pi \hbar } }\right)^{1/4}\cdot e^{-{\frac {m\omega x^{2} }{2\hbar } } }\cdot H_{n}\left({\sqrt {\frac {m\omega }{\hbar } } }x\right),\qquad$$}@} {@{$n=0,1,2,\ldots .$}@} where _H<sub>n</sub>_ are {@{the (annotation: physicists') [Hermite polynomials](Hermite%20polynomials.md)}@} {@{$$H_{n}(x)=(-1)^{n}e^{x^{2} }{\frac {d^{n} }{dx^{n} } }\left(e^{-x^{2} }\right),$$}@} and {@{the corresponding energy levels}@} are {@{$$E_{n}=\hbar \omega \left(n+{1 \over 2}\right).$$}@} This is another example illustrating {@{the discretization of energy for [bound states](bound%20state.md)}@}.

### Mach–Zehnder interferometer

> {@{![Schematic of a Mach–Zehnder interferometer](../../archives/Wikimedia%20Commons/Mach-Zehnder%20interferometer.svg)}@}
>
> {@{Schematic}@} of {@{a Mach–Zehnder interferometer}@}

{@{The [Mach–Zehnder interferometer](Mach–Zehnder%20interferometer.md) \(MZI\)}@} illustrates the concepts of {@{superposition and interference}@} with {@{linear algebra in dimension 2, rather than differential equations}@}. It can be seen as {@{a simplified version of the double-slit experiment}@}, but it is of {@{interest in its own right}@}, for example in {@{the [delayed choice quantum eraser](delayed%20choice%20quantum%20eraser.md), the [Elitzur–Vaidman bomb tester](Elitzur–Vaidman%20bomb%20tester.md), and in studies of quantum entanglement}@}.<sup>[\[36\]](#^ref-36)</sup><sup>[\[37\]](#^ref-37)</sup>

We can model {@{a photon going through the interferometer}@} by considering that at {@{each point it can be in a superposition of only two paths}@}: {@{the "lower" path}@} which starts {@{from the left, goes straight through both beam splitters, and ends at the top}@}, and {@{the "upper" path}@} which starts {@{from the bottom, goes straight through both beam splitters, and ends at the right}@}. (annotation: Note this treats {@{the bottom-left beam splitter}@} as {@{the emitter of the two paths}@}.) {@{The quantum state of the photon}@} is therefore {@{a vector $\psi \in \mathbb {C} ^{2}$ that is a superposition}@} of {@{the "lower" path $\psi _{l}={\begin{pmatrix}1\\0\end{pmatrix} }$ and the "upper" path $\psi _{u}={\begin{pmatrix}0\\1\end{pmatrix} }$}@}, that is, {@{$\psi =\alpha \psi _{l}+\beta \psi _{u}$ for complex $\alpha ,\beta$}@}. In order to respect {@{the postulate that $\langle \psi ,\psi \rangle =1$}@} we require that {@{$|\alpha |^{2}+|\beta |^{2}=1$}@}.

{@{Both [beam splitters](beam%20splitter.md)}@} are modelled as {@{the unitary matrix $B={\frac {1}{\sqrt {2} } }{\begin{pmatrix}1&i\\i&1\end{pmatrix} }$}@}, which means that when {@{a photon meets the beam splitter}@} it will either {@{stay on the same path with a probability amplitude of $1/{\sqrt {2} }$}@}, or be {@{reflected to the other path with a probability amplitude of $i/{\sqrt {2} }$}@}. {@{The phase shifter on the upper arm}@} is modelled as {@{the unitary matrix $P={\begin{pmatrix}1&0\\0&e^{i\Delta \Phi }\end{pmatrix} }$}@}, which means that if {@{the photon is on the "upper" path}@} it will {@{gain a relative phase of $\Delta \Phi$}@}, and it will {@{stay unchanged if it is in the lower path}@}.

A photon that enters {@{the interferometer from the left}@} will then be acted upon with {@{a beam splitter $B$, a phase shifter $P$, and another beam splitter $B$}@}, and so end up in the state {@{$$BPB\psi _{l}=ie^{i\Delta \Phi /2}{\begin{pmatrix}-\sin(\Delta \Phi /2)\\\cos(\Delta \Phi /2)\end{pmatrix} },$$}@} (annotation: derivation with {@{$B = {\frac {1}{\sqrt {2} } }{\begin{pmatrix}1&i\\i&1\end{pmatrix} }$ and $P = {\begin{pmatrix}1&0\\0&e^{i\Delta \Phi }\end{pmatrix} }$}@}: {@{$$\begin{aligned} BPB\begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix} &=BP{\frac {1}{\sqrt {2} } }{\begin{pmatrix}1\\i\end{pmatrix} }\\&=B{\frac {1}{\sqrt {2} } }{\begin{pmatrix}1\\ie^{i\Delta \Phi }\end{pmatrix} }\\&={\frac {1}{2} }{\begin{pmatrix}1-e^{i\Delta \Phi }\\i+ie^{i\Delta \Phi }\end{pmatrix} }\\&=ie^{i\Delta \Phi /2}{\begin{pmatrix}-\sin(\Delta \Phi /2)\\\cos(\Delta \Phi /2)\end{pmatrix} }.\!\end{aligned}$$}@}) and {@{the probabilities that it will be detected at the right or at the top}@} are given respectively by {@{$$p(u)=|\langle \psi _{u},BPB\psi _{l}\rangle |^{2}=\cos ^{2}{\frac {\Delta \Phi }{2} },$$ $$p(l)=|\langle \psi _{l},BPB\psi _{l}\rangle |^{2}=\sin ^{2}{\frac {\Delta \Phi }{2} }.$$}@} One can therefore use {@{the Mach–Zehnder interferometer}@} to estimate {@{the [phase shift](phase%20(waves).md) by estimating these probabilities}@}.

It is interesting to consider {@{what would happen if the photon}@} were definitely in {@{either the "lower" or "upper" paths between the beam splitters}@}. This can be accomplished by {@{blocking one of the paths, or equivalently by removing the first beam splitter}@} \(and feeding {@{the photon from the left or the bottom, as desired}@}\). In {@{both cases}@}, there will be no {@{interference between the paths anymore}@}, and the probabilities are given by {@{$p(u)=p(l)=1/2$, independently of the phase $\Delta \Phi$}@}. From this we can conclude that {@{the photon does not take one path or another}@} after {@{the first beam splitter}@}, but rather that it is in {@{a genuine quantum superposition of the two paths}@}.<sup>[\[38\]](#^ref-38)</sup> (annotation: If the photon really {@{followed just one definite path after the first beam splitter}@}, then blocking {@{the other path would change nothing}@}—but because blocking {@{either path destroys the interference}@}, the photon cannot have {@{taken only one route and must instead have been in a superposition of both paths}@}.)

## applications

- Main article: ::@:: [Applications of quantum mechanics](applications%20of%20quantum%20mechanics.md)

{@{Quantum mechanics}@} has had {@{enormous success in explaining many of the features of our universe}@}, with regard to {@{small-scale and discrete quantities and interactions}@} which cannot be {@{explained by [classical methods](classical%20physics.md)}@}.<sup>[\[note 2\]](#^ref-note%202)</sup> Quantum mechanics is often {@{the only theory that can reveal the individual behaviors}@} of {@{the subatomic particles that make up all forms of matter}@} \({@{electrons, [protons](proton.md), [neutrons](neutron.md), [photons](photon.md)}@}, and others\). {@{[Solid-state physics](solid-state%20physics.md) and [materials science](materials%20science.md)}@} are {@{dependent upon quantum mechanics}@}.<sup>[\[39\]](#^ref-39)</sup>

In {@{many aspects}@}, {@{modern technology}@} operates at {@{a scale where quantum effects are significant}@}. {@{Important applications}@} of quantum theory include {@{[quantum chemistry](quantum%20chemistry.md), [quantum optics](quantum%20optics.md), [quantum computing](quantum%20computing.md)}@}, {@{[superconducting magnets](superconducting%20magnet.md), [light-emitting diodes](light-emitting%20diode.md), the [optical amplifier](optical%20amplifier.md) and the laser}@}, {@{the [transistor](transistor.md) and [semiconductors](semiconductor.md) such as the [microprocessor](microprocessor.md)}@}, {@{[medical and research imaging](medical%20imaging.md) such as [magnetic resonance imaging](magnetic%20resonance%20imaging.md) and [electron microscopy](electron%20microscopy.md)}@}.<sup>[\[40\]](#^ref-40)</sup> Explanations for {@{many biological and physical phenomena}@} are rooted in {@{the nature of the chemical bond}@}, most notably {@{the macro-molecule [DNA](DNA.md)}@}.

### classical mechanics

{@{The rules of quantum mechanics}@} assert that {@{the state space of a system}@} is {@{a Hilbert space}@} and that {@{observables of the system}@} are {@{Hermitian operators acting on vectors in that space}@} – although they do not {@{tell us which Hilbert space or which operators}@}. These can be {@{chosen appropriately}@} in order to obtain {@{a quantitative description of a quantum system}@}, {@{a necessary step}@} in {@{making physical predictions}@}. {@{An important guide for making these choices}@} is {@{the [correspondence principle](correspondence%20principle.md)}@}, {@{a heuristic which states that the predictions of quantum mechanics reduce to those of [classical mechanics](classical%20mechanics.md)}@} in {@{the regime of large [quantum numbers](quantum%20number.md)}@}.<sup>[\[41\]](#^ref-41)</sup> One can also start from {@{an established classical model of a particular system}@}, and then try to guess {@{the underlying quantum model}@} that would give {@{rise to the classical model in the correspondence limit}@}. This approach is known as {@{[quantization](canonical%20quantization.md)}@}.<sup>[\[42\]](#^ref-42)</sup><sup>:&hairsp;299&hairsp;</sup><sup>[\[43\]](#^ref-43)</sup>

When {@{quantum mechanics was originally formulated}@}, it was applied to {@{models whose correspondence limit was [non-relativistic](theory%20of%20relativity.md) classical mechanics}@}. For instance, {@{the well-known model of the [quantum harmonic oscillator](quantum%20harmonic%20oscillator.md)}@} uses {@{an explicitly non-relativistic expression for the [kinetic energy](kinetic%20energy.md)}@} of the oscillator, and is thus {@{a quantum version of the [classical harmonic oscillator](harmonic%20oscillator.md)}@}.<sup>[\[7\]](#^ref-7)</sup><sup>:&hairsp;234&hairsp;</sup>

{@{Complications}@} arise with {@{[chaotic systems](chaotic%20systems.md)}@}, which do not {@{have good quantum numbers}@}, and {@{[quantum chaos](quantum%20chaos.md)}@} studies {@{the relationship between classical and quantum descriptions in these systems}@}.<sup>[\[42\]](#^ref-42)</sup><sup>:&hairsp;353&hairsp;</sup>

{@{[Quantum decoherence](quantum%20decoherence.md)}@} is {@{a mechanism through which quantum systems lose [coherence](quantum%20coherence.md#quantum%20coherence)}@}, and thus become {@{incapable of displaying many typically quantum effects}@}: {@{[quantum superpositions](quantum%20superposition.md) become simply probabilistic mixtures}@}, and {@{quantum entanglement becomes simply classical correlations}@}.<sup>[\[7\]](#^ref-7)</sup><sup>:&hairsp;687–730&hairsp;</sup> {@{Quantum coherence}@} is not {@{typically evident at macroscopic scales}@}, though at {@{temperatures approaching [absolute zero](absolute%20zero.md)}@} {@{quantum behavior may manifest macroscopically}@}.<sup>[\[note 3\]](#^ref-note%203)</sup>

Many {@{macroscopic properties of a classical system}@} are {@{a direct consequence of the quantum behavior}@} of its parts. For example, {@{the stability of bulk matter}@} \(consisting of {@{atoms and [molecules](molecule.md)}@} which would {@{quickly collapse under electric forces alone}@}\), {@{the rigidity}@} of solids, and {@{the mechanical, thermal, chemical, optical and magnetic properties}@} of matter are all results of {@{the interaction of [electric charges](electric%20charge.md) under the rules of quantum mechanics}@}.<sup>[\[44\]](#^ref-44)</sup>

### special relativity and electrodynamics

Early attempts to {@{merge quantum mechanics with [special relativity](special%20relativity.md)}@} involved {@{the replacement of the Schrödinger equation with a covariant equation}@} such as {@{the [Klein–Gordon equation](Klein–Gordon%20equation.md) or the [Dirac equation](Dirac%20equation.md)}@}. While these theories were {@{successful in explaining many experimental results}@}, they had {@{certain unsatisfactory qualities}@} stemming from their {@{neglect of the relativistic creation and annihilation of particles}@}. {@{A fully relativistic quantum theory}@} required {@{the development of quantum field theory}@}, which applies {@{quantization to a field \(rather than a fixed set of particles\)}@}. {@{The first complete quantum field theory, [quantum electrodynamics](quantum%20electrodynamics.md)}@}, provides {@{a fully quantum description of the [electromagnetic interaction](electromagnetic%20interaction.md)}@}. {@{Quantum electrodynamics is, along with [general relativity](general%20relativity.md)}@}, one of {@{the most accurate physical theories ever devised}@}.<sup>[\[45\]](#^ref-45)</sup><sup>[\[46\]](#^ref-46)</sup>

{@{The full apparatus of quantum field theory}@} is often {@{unnecessary for describing electrodynamic systems}@}. {@{A simpler approach, one that has been used since the inception of quantum mechanics}@}, is to treat {@{[charged](electric%20charge.md) particles as quantum mechanical objects}@} being {@{acted on by a classical [electromagnetic field](electromagnetic%20field.md)}@}. For example, {@{the elementary quantum model of the [hydrogen atom](hydrogen%20atom.md)}@} describes {@{the [electric field](electric%20field.md) of the hydrogen atom}@} using {@{a classical $\textstyle -e^{2}/(4\pi \epsilon _{_{0} }r)$ [Coulomb potential](electric%20potential.md)}@}.<sup>[\[7\]](#^ref-7)</sup><sup>:&hairsp;285&hairsp;</sup> Likewise, in {@{a [Stern–Gerlach experiment](Stern–Gerlach%20experiment.md)}@}, {@{a charged particle}@} is {@{modeled as a quantum system}@}, while {@{the background magnetic field is described classically}@}.<sup>[\[42\]](#^ref-42)</sup><sup>:&hairsp;26&hairsp;</sup> This {@{"semi-classical" approach fails}@} if {@{quantum fluctuations in the electromagnetic field}@} play {@{an important role, such as in the emission of photons by [charged particles](charged%20particle.md)}@}.

{@{[Quantum field](field%20(physics).md) theories}@} for {@{the [strong nuclear force](strong%20nuclear%20force.md) and the [weak nuclear force](weak%20nuclear%20force.md)}@} have {@{also been developed}@}. {@{The quantum field theory of the strong nuclear force}@} is called {@{[quantum chromodynamics](quantum%20chromodynamics.md)}@}, and describes {@{the interactions of subnuclear particles such as [quarks](quark.md) and [gluons](gluon.md)}@}. {@{The weak nuclear force and the electromagnetic force}@} were {@{unified, in their quantized forms}@}, into {@{a single quantum field theory \(known as [electroweak theory](electroweak%20theory.md)\)}@}, by the physicists {@{[Abdus Salam](Abdus%20Salam.md), [Sheldon Glashow](Sheldon%20Glashow.md) and [Steven Weinberg](Steven%20Weinberg.md)}@}.<sup>[\[47\]](#^ref-47)</sup>

### relation to general relativity

Even though {@{the predictions of both quantum theory and general relativity}@} have been {@{supported by rigorous and repeated [empirical evidence](empirical%20evidence.md)}@}, their {@{abstract formalisms contradict each other}@} and they have proven {@{extremely difficult to incorporate into one consistent, cohesive model}@}. {@{Gravity}@} is {@{negligible in many areas of particle physics}@}, so that {@{unification between general relativity and quantum mechanics}@} is not {@{an urgent issue in those particular applications}@}. However, the lack of {@{a correct theory of [quantum gravity](quantum%20gravity.md)}@} is {@{an important issue in [physical cosmology](physical%20cosmology.md)}@} and the search by {@{physicists for an elegant "[Theory of Everything](theory%20of%20everything.md)" \(TOE\)}@}. Consequently, resolving {@{the inconsistencies between both theories}@} has been {@{a major goal of 20th- and 21st-century physics}@}. {@{This TOE}@} would combine {@{not only the models of subatomic physics}@} but also derive {@{the four fundamental forces of nature from a single force or phenomenon}@}.<sup>[\[48\]](#^ref-48)</sup>

{@{One proposal}@} for doing so is {@{[string theory](string%20theory.md)}@}, which posits that {@{the [point-like particles](point%20particle.md) of [particle physics](particle%20physics.md)}@} are {@{replaced by [one-dimensional](dimension%20(mathematics%20and%20physics).md) objects called [strings](string%20(physics).md)}@}. {@{String theory}@} describes how {@{these strings propagate through space and interact with each other}@}. On {@{distance scales larger than the string scale}@}, a string looks {@{just like an ordinary particle}@}, with its {@{[mass](mass.md), [charge](charge%20(physics).md), and other properties}@} determined by {@{the [vibrational](vibration.md) state of the string}@}. In {@{string theory}@}, {@{one of the many vibrational states of the string}@} corresponds to {@{the [graviton](graviton.md), a quantum mechanical particle that carries gravitational force}@}.<sup>[\[49\]](#^ref-49)</sup><sup>[\[50\]](#^ref-50)</sup>

{@{Another popular theory}@} is {@{[loop quantum gravity](loop%20quantum%20gravity.md) \(LQG\)}@}, which describes {@{quantum properties of gravity and is thus a theory of [quantum spacetime](quantum%20spacetime.md)}@}. LQG is an attempt to {@{merge and adapt standard quantum mechanics and standard general relativity}@}. This theory describes space as {@{an extremely fine fabric "woven" of finite loops called [spin networks](spin%20network.md)}@}. {@{The evolution of a spin network over time}@} is called {@{a [spin foam](spin%20foam.md)}@}. {@{The characteristic length scale of a spin foam}@} is {@{the [Planck length](Planck%20length.md#Planck%20length), approximately 1.616×10<sup>−35</sup> m}@}, and so {@{lengths shorter than the Planck length}@} are not {@{physically meaningful in LQG}@}.<sup>[\[51\]](#^ref-51)</sup>

## philosophical implications

- Main article: ::@:: [Interpretations of quantum mechanics](interpretations%20of%20quantum%20mechanics.md)

> {@{__Unsolved problem in physics__}@}
>
> _Is there {@{a preferred interpretation of quantum mechanics}@}? How does {@{the quantum description of reality}@}, which includes elements such as {@{the "[superposition](superposition%20principle.md) of states" and "[wave function collapse](wave%20function%20collapse.md)"}@}, give {@{rise to the reality we perceive}@}?_
>
> _[More unsolved problems in physics](list%20of%20unsolved%20problems%20in%20physics.md)_

Since {@{its inception}@}, {@{the many counter-intuitive aspects and results}@} of quantum mechanics have provoked {@{strong [philosophical](philosophical.md) debates and many [interpretations](interpretations%20of%20quantum%20mechanics.md)}@}. {@{The arguments}@} centre on {@{the probabilistic nature}@} of quantum mechanics, the difficulties with {@{[wavefunction collapse](wavefunction%20collapse.md) and the related [measurement problem](measurement%20problem.md)}@}, and {@{[quantum nonlocality](quantum%20nonlocality.md)}@}. Perhaps {@{the only consensus that exists}@} about these issues is that there is {@{no consensus}@}. [Richard Feynman](Richard%20Feynman.md) once said, "I think I can {@{safely say that nobody understands quantum mechanics}@}."<sup>[\[52\]](#^ref-52)</sup> According to {@{[Steven Weinberg](Steven%20Weinberg.md)}@}, "There is now in my opinion {@{no entirely satisfactory interpretation of quantum mechanics}@}."<sup>[\[53\]](#^ref-53)</sup>

{@{The views of [Niels Bohr](Niels%20Bohr.md), Werner Heisenberg and other physicists}@} are often grouped together as {@{the "[Copenhagen interpretation](Copenhagen%20interpretation.md)"}@}.<sup>[\[54\]](#^ref-54)</sup><sup>[\[55\]](#^ref-55)</sup> According to these views, {@{the probabilistic nature}@} of quantum mechanics is not {@{a _temporary_ feature which will eventually be replaced by a deterministic theory}@}, but is instead {@{a _final_ renunciation of the classical idea of "causality"}@}. {@{Bohr in particular emphasized}@} that any {@{well-defined application of the quantum mechanical formalism}@} must always make {@{reference to the experimental arrangement}@}, due to {@{the [complementary](complementarity%20(physics).md) nature of evidence obtained under different experimental situations}@}. {@{Copenhagen-type interpretations}@} were adopted by {@{Nobel laureates in quantum physics}@}, including {@{Bohr,<sup>[\[56\]](#^ref-56)</sup> Heisenberg,<sup>[\[57\]](#^ref-57)</sup> Schrödinger,<sup>[\[58\]](#^ref-58)</sup> Feynman,<sup>[\[2\]](#^ref-2)</sup> and [Zeilinger](Anton%20Zeilinger.md)}@}<sup>[\[59\]](#^ref-59)</sup> as well as {@{21st-century researchers in quantum foundations}@}.<sup>[\[60\]](#^ref-60)</sup>

{@{[Albert Einstein](Albert%20Einstein.md), himself one of the founders of [quantum theory](old%20quantum%20theory.md)}@}, was troubled by its {@{apparent failure to respect some cherished metaphysical principles}@}, such as {@{[determinism](determinism.md) and [locality](principle%20of%20locality.md)}@}. Einstein's {@{long-running exchanges with Bohr about the meaning and status of quantum mechanics}@} are now known as {@{the [Bohr–Einstein debates](Bohr–Einstein%20debates.md)}@}. Einstein believed that {@{underlying quantum mechanics}@} must be {@{a theory that explicitly forbids [action at a distance](action%20at%20a%20distance.md)}@}. He argued that quantum mechanics was {@{incomplete, a theory that was valid but not fundamental}@}, analogous to how {@{[thermodynamics](thermodynamics.md) is valid}@}, but {@{the fundamental theory behind it is [statistical mechanics](statistical%20mechanics.md)}@}. In {@{1935, Einstein and his collaborators [Boris Podolsky](Boris%20Podolsky.md) and [Nathan Rosen](Nathan%20Rosen.md)}@} published an argument that {@{the principle of locality implies the incompleteness of quantum mechanics}@}, {@{a [thought experiment](thought%20experiment.md) later termed the [Einstein–Podolsky–Rosen paradox](Einstein–Podolsky–Rosen%20paradox.md)}@}.<sup>[\[note 4\]](#^ref-note%204)</sup> In {@{1964, [John Bell](John%20Stewart%20Bell.md)}@} showed that EPR's {@{principle of locality, together with determinism}@}, was actually {@{incompatible with quantum mechanics}@}: they implied {@{constraints on the correlations produced by distance systems, now known as [Bell inequalities](Bell%20inequalities.md)}@}, that can be {@{violated by entangled particles}@}.<sup>[\[65\]](#^ref-65)</sup> Since then {@{[several experiments](Bell%20test.md) have been performed to obtain these correlations}@}, with the result that they do in fact {@{violate Bell inequalities, and thus falsify the conjunction of locality with determinism}@}.<sup>[\[16\]](#^ref-16)</sup><sup>[\[17\]](#^ref-17)</sup>

{@{[Bohmian mechanics](Bohmian%20mechanics.md)}@} shows that it is possible to reformulate {@{quantum mechanics to make it deterministic}@}, at the price of {@{making it explicitly nonlocal}@}. It attributes not only {@{a wave function to a physical system, but in addition a real position}@}, that evolves {@{deterministically under a nonlocal guiding equation}@}. {@{The evolution of a physical system}@} is given at {@{all times by the Schrödinger equation together with the guiding equation}@}; there is never {@{a collapse of the wave function}@}. This solves {@{the measurement problem}@}.<sup>[\[66\]](#^ref-66)</sup>

> {@{![The [Schrödinger's cat](Schrödinger's%20cat.md) thought experiment can be used to visualize the many-worlds interpretation of quantum mechanics, where a branching of the universe occurs through a superposition of two quantum mechanical states.](../../archives/Wikimedia%20Commons/Schroedingers%20cat%20film.svg)}@}
>
> {@{The [Schrödinger's cat](Schrödinger's%20cat.md) thought experiment}@} can be used to visualize {@{the many-worlds interpretation of quantum mechanics}@}, where {@{a branching of the universe}@} occurs through {@{a superposition of two quantum mechanical states}@}.

{@{Everett's [many-worlds interpretation](many-worlds%20interpretation.md), formulated in 1956}@}, holds that {@{_all_ the possibilities described by quantum theory _simultaneously_ occur}@} in {@{a multiverse composed of mostly independent parallel universes}@}.<sup>[\[67\]](#^ref-67)</sup> This is a consequence of {@{removing the axiom of the collapse of the wave packet}@}. {@{All possible states}@} of {@{the measured system and the measuring apparatus, together with the observer}@}, are {@{present in a real physical quantum superposition}@}. While {@{the multiverse is deterministic}@}, we perceive {@{non-deterministic behavior governed by probabilities}@}, because we do not {@{observe the multiverse as a whole, but only one parallel universe at a time}@}. {@{Exactly how this is supposed to work}@} has been {@{the subject of much debate}@}. {@{Several attempts}@} have been made to make {@{sense of this and derive the Born rule}@},<sup>[\[68\]](#^ref-68)</sup><sup>[\[69\]](#^ref-69)</sup> with {@{no consensus on whether they have been successful}@}.<sup>[\[70\]](#^ref-70)</sup><sup>[\[71\]](#^ref-71)</sup><sup>[\[72\]](#^ref-72)</sup>

{@{[Relational quantum mechanics](relational%20quantum%20mechanics.md) appeared in the late 1990s}@} as {@{a modern derivative of Copenhagen-type ideas}@},<sup>[\[73\]](#^ref-73)</sup><sup>[\[74\]](#^ref-74)</sup> and {@{[QBism](QBism.md) was developed some years}@} later.<sup>[\[75\]](#^ref-75)</sup><sup>[\[76\]](#^ref-76)</sup>

## history

- Main articles: ::@:: [History of quantum mechanics](history%20of%20quantum%20mechanics.md) and [Atomic theory](atomic%20theory.md)

Quantum mechanics was developed in {@{the early decades of the 20th century}@}, driven by {@{the need to explain phenomena}@} that, in {@{some cases, had been observed in earlier times}@}. {@{Scientific inquiry into the wave nature of light}@} began in {@{the 17th and 18th centuries}@}, when scientists such as {@{[Robert Hooke](Robert%20Hooke.md), [Christiaan Huygens](Christiaan%20Huygens.md) and [Leonhard Euler](Leonhard%20Euler.md)}@} proposed {@{a wave theory of light based on experimental observations}@}.<sup>[\[77\]](#^ref-77)</sup> In {@{1803 English [polymath](polymath.md) [Thomas Young](Thomas%20Young%20(scientist).md)}@} described {@{the famous [double-slit experiment](young's%20interference%20experiment.md)}@}.<sup>[\[78\]](#^ref-78)</sup> This experiment played {@{a major role in the general acceptance of the [wave theory of light](wave%20theory%20of%20light.md)}@}.

During {@{the early 19th century}@}, {@{[chemical](chemistry.md) research by [John Dalton](John%20Dalton.md) and [Amedeo Avogadro](Amedeo%20Avogadro.md)}@} lent {@{weight to the [atomic theory](atomic%20theory.md) of matter}@}, an idea that {@{[James Clerk Maxwell](James%20Clerk%20Maxwell.md), [Ludwig Boltzmann](Ludwig%20Boltzmann.md) and others built upon}@} to establish {@{the [kinetic theory of gases](kinetic%20theory%20of%20gases.md)}@}. {@{The successes of kinetic theory}@} gave {@{further credence to the idea that matter is composed of atoms}@}, yet the theory also had {@{shortcomings that would only be resolved by the development of quantum mechanics}@}.<sup>[\[79\]](#^ref-79)</sup> While {@{the early conception of atoms from [Greek philosophy](Greek%20philosophy.md)}@} had been that they were {@{indivisible units – the word "atom" deriving from the [Greek](Greek%20language.md) for 'uncuttable'}@} – {@{the 19th century saw the formulation}@} of {@{hypotheses about subatomic structure}@}. {@{One important discovery}@} in that regard was {@{[Michael Faraday](Michael%20Faraday.md)'s 1838 observation of a glow}@} caused by {@{an electrical discharge inside a glass tube containing gas at low pressure}@}. {@{[Julius Plücker](Julius%20Plücker.md), [Johann Wilhelm Hittorf](Johann%20Wilhelm%20Hittorf.md) and [Eugen Goldstein](Eugen%20Goldstein.md)}@} {@{carried on and improved upon Faraday's work}@}, leading to {@{the identification of [cathode rays](cathode%20rays.md), which [J. J. Thomson](J.%20J.%20Thomson.md)}@} found to {@{consist of subatomic particles that would be called electrons}@}.<sup>[\[80\]](#^ref-80)</sup><sup>[\[81\]](#^ref-81)</sup>

> {@{![[Max Planck](Max%20Planck.md) is considered the father of the quantum theory.](../../archives/Wikimedia%20Commons/Max%20Planck%20%281858-1947%29.jpg)}@}
>
> {@{[Max Planck](Max%20Planck.md)}@} is considered {@{the father of the quantum theory}@}.

{@{The [black-body radiation](black-body%20radiation.md) problem}@} was discovered by {@{[Gustav Kirchhoff](Gustav%20Kirchhoff.md) in 1859}@}. In {@{1900, Max Planck}@} proposed the hypothesis that {@{energy is radiated and absorbed in discrete "quanta" \(or energy packets\)}@}, yielding {@{a calculation that precisely matched the observed patterns of black-body radiation}@}.<sup>[\[82\]](#^ref-82)</sup> {@{The word _quantum_}@} derives from {@{the [Latin](Latin.md), meaning "how great" or "how much"}@}.<sup>[\[83\]](#^ref-83)</sup> According to {@{Planck, quantities of energy}@} could be {@{thought of as divided into "elements"}@} whose {@{size \(_E_\) would be proportional to their [frequency](frequency.md) \(_ν_\)}@}: {@{$$E=h\nu \ {},$$ where _h_ is the [Planck constant](Planck%20constant.md)}@}. {@{Planck cautiously insisted}@} that this was {@{only an aspect of the processes of absorption and emission of radiation}@} and was not {@{the _physical reality_ of the radiation}@}.<sup>[\[84\]](#^ref-84)</sup> In fact, he considered his {@{quantum hypothesis a mathematical trick}@} to get {@{the right answer rather than a sizable discovery}@}.<sup>[\[85\]](#^ref-85)</sup> However, in {@{1905 Albert Einstein}@} interpreted {@{Planck's quantum hypothesis [realistically](local%20realism.md#local%20realism)}@} and used it to {@{explain the [photoelectric effect](photoelectric%20effect.md)}@}, in which {@{shining light on certain materials can eject electrons from the material}@}. {@{Niels Bohr then developed Planck's ideas}@} about radiation into {@{a [model of the hydrogen atom](Bohr%20model.md) that successfully predicted the [spectral lines](spectral%20line.md) of hydrogen}@}.<sup>[\[86\]](#^ref-86)</sup> {@{Einstein further developed this idea}@} to show that {@{an [electromagnetic wave](electromagnetic%20wave.md) such as light}@} could also be described as {@{a particle \(later called the photon\), with a discrete amount of energy that depends on its frequency}@}.<sup>[\[87\]](#^ref-87)</sup> In his {@{paper "On the Quantum Theory of Radiation", Einstein}@} expanded on {@{the interaction between energy and matter}@} to explain {@{the absorption and emission of energy by atoms}@}. Although {@{overshadowed at the time by his general theory of relativity}@}, this paper articulated {@{the mechanism underlying the stimulated emission of radiation}@},<sup>[\[88\]](#^ref-88)</sup> which became {@{the basis of the laser}@}.<sup>[\[89\]](#^ref-89)</sup>

> {@{![The 1927 [Solvay Conference](Solvay%20Conference.md) in [Brussels](Brussels.md) was the fifth world physics conference.](../../archives/Wikimedia%20Commons/Solvay%20conference%201927.jpg)}@}
>
> {@{The 1927 [Solvay Conference](Solvay%20Conference.md)}@} in {@{[Brussels](Brussels.md) was the fifth world physics conference}@}.

{@{This phase}@} is known as {@{the [old quantum theory](old%20quantum%20theory.md)}@}. Never {@{complete or self-consistent}@}, the old quantum theory was rather {@{a set of [heuristic](heuristic.md) corrections to classical mechanics}@}.<sup>[\[90\]](#^ref-90)</sup><sup>[\[91\]](#^ref-91)</sup> The theory is now understood as {@{a [semi-classical approximation](WKB%20approximation.md#application%20to%20the%20Schr%C3%B6dinger%20equation) to modern quantum mechanics}@}.<sup>[\[92\]](#^ref-92)</sup><sup>[\[93\]](#^ref-93)</sup> {@{Notable results from this period}@} include, in addition to {@{the work of Planck, Einstein and Bohr}@} mentioned above, {@{Einstein and [Peter Debye](Peter%20Debye.md)'s work}@} on {@{the [specific heat](specific%20heat.md) of solids}@}, {@{Bohr and [Hendrika Johanna van Leeuwen](Hendrika%20Johanna%20van%20Leeuwen.md)'s [proof](Bohr–Van%20Leeuwen%20theorem.md)}@} that {@{classical physics cannot account for [diamagnetism](diamagnetism.md)}@}, and {@{[Arnold Sommerfeld](Arnold%20Sommerfeld.md)'s extension}@} of {@{the Bohr model to include special-relativistic effects}@}.<sup>[\[90\]](#^ref-90)</sup><sup>[\[94\]](#^ref-94)</sup>

In {@{the mid-1920s quantum mechanics}@} was developed to become {@{the standard formulation for atomic physics}@}. In {@{1923, the French physicist [Louis de Broglie](Louis%20de%20Broglie.md)}@} put forward his {@{theory of matter waves}@} by stating that {@{particles can exhibit wave characteristics and vice versa}@}. Building on {@{de Broglie's approach, modern quantum mechanics}@} was {@{born in 1925, when the German physicists Werner Heisenberg, Max Born, and [Pascual Jordan](Pascual%20Jordan.md)}@}<sup>[\[95\]](#^ref-95)</sup><sup>[\[96\]](#^ref-96)</sup> developed {@{[matrix mechanics](matrix%20mechanics.md)}@} and {@{the Austrian physicist Erwin Schrödinger}@} invented {@{[wave mechanics](Schrödinger%20equation.md)}@}. Born introduced {@{the probabilistic interpretation of Schrödinger's wave function in July 1926}@}.<sup>[\[97\]](#^ref-97)</sup> Thus, {@{the entire field of quantum physics}@} emerged, leading to its {@{wider acceptance at the Fifth [Solvay Conference](Solvay%20Conference.md) in 1927}@}.<sup>[\[98\]](#^ref-98)</sup>

By {@{1930, quantum mechanics}@} had been further {@{unified and formalized by [David Hilbert](David%20Hilbert.md), Paul Dirac and [John von Neumann](John%20von%20Neumann.md)}@}<sup>[\[99\]](#^ref-99)</sup> with greater {@{emphasis on [measurement](measurement%20in%20quantum%20mechanics.md), the statistical nature of our knowledge of reality}@}, and {@{[philosophical speculation about the 'observer'](interpretations%20of%20quantum%20mechanics.md)}@}. It has since {@{permeated many disciplines}@}, including {@{quantum chemistry, [quantum electronics](quantum%20electronics.md#quantum%20electronics), [quantum optics](quantum%20optics.md), and [quantum information science](quantum%20information%20science.md)}@}. It also provides {@{a useful framework for many features of the modern [periodic table of elements](periodic%20table%20of%20elements.md)}@}, and describes the behaviors of {@{[atoms](atoms.md) during [chemical bonding](chemical%20bond.md)}@} and the flow of {@{electrons in computer [semiconductors](semiconductor.md)}@}, and therefore plays {@{a crucial role in many modern technologies}@}. While quantum mechanics was constructed to {@{describe the world of the very small}@}, it is also needed to explain {@{some [macroscopic](macroscopic.md) phenomena such as [superconductors](superconductors.md)<sup>[\[100\]](#^ref-100)</sup> and [superfluids](superfluid.md)}@}.<sup>[\[101\]](#^ref-101)</sup>

## further reading

The following titles, all by working physicists, attempt to communicate quantum theory to lay people, using a minimum of technical apparatus:

- [Chester, Marvin](Marvin%20Chester.md) \(1987\). _Primer of Quantum Mechanics_. John Wiley. [ISBN](ISBN%20(identifier).md) [0-486-42878-8](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:BookSources/0-486-42878-8)

[ISBN](ISBN%20(identifier).md) [978-1-84614-432-5](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:BookSources/978-1-84614-432-5).
