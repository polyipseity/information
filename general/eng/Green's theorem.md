In {@{[vector calculus](vector%20calculus.md)}@}, {@{__Green's theorem__}@} relates {@{a [line integral](line%20integral.md) around a [simple closed curve](curve.md#definition) _C_ to a [double integral](double%20integral.md) over the [plane](plane%20(geometry).md) region _D_ \(surface in $\mathbb {R} ^{2}$\) bounded by _C_}@}. It is {@{the two-dimensional special case}@} of {@{[Stokes' theorem](Kelvin–Stokes%20theorem.md) \(surface in $\mathbb {R} ^{3}$\)}@}. In {@{one dimension}@}, it is equivalent to {@{the [fundamental theorem of calculus](fundamental%20theorem%20of%20calculus.md)}@}. In {@{three dimensions}@}, it is {@{[equivalent to the divergence theorem](divergence%20theorem.md#multiple%20dimensions)}@}. <!--SR:!2025-09-27,62,310!2025-10-05,66,310!2025-10-06,67,310!2025-09-21,57,310!2025-09-22,58,310!2025-10-05,66,310!2025-09-20,56,310!2025-09-22,58,310!2025-10-06,67,310-->

## theorem

Let _C_ be {@{a positively [oriented](curve%20orientation.md), [piecewise](piecewise.md) [smooth](smoothness.md), [simple closed curve](simple%20closed%20curve.md#jordan) in a [plane](plane%20(mathematics).md)}@}, and let _D_ be {@{the region bounded by _C_}@}. If _L_ and _M_ are {@{functions of \(_x_, _y_\) defined on an [open region](open%20set.md) containing _D_ and have [continuous](continuous%20function.md) [partial derivatives](partial%20derivatives.md) there}@}, then {@{$$\oint _{C}(L\,dx+M\,dy)=\iint _{D}\left({\frac {\partial M}{\partial x} }-{\frac {\partial L}{\partial y} }\right)dA$$}@} where {@{the path of integration along _C_ is [counterclockwise](counterclockwise.md)}@}.<sup>[\[1\]](#^ref-1)</sup><sup>[\[2\]](#^ref-2)</sup> <!--SR:!2025-09-27,62,310!2025-10-05,66,310!2025-10-02,63,310!2025-09-22,58,310!2025-09-19,55,310-->

## application

In {@{physics}@}, Green's theorem {@{finds many applications}@}. One is {@{solving two-dimensional flow integrals}@}, stating that {@{the sum of fluid outflowing from a volume is equal to the total outflow summed about an enclosing area}@}. In {@{[plane geometry](Euclidean%20plane%20geometry.md), and in particular, area [surveying](surveying.md)}@}, Green's theorem can be used to {@{determine the area and centroid of plane figures}@} {@{solely by integrating over the perimeter}@}. <!--SR:!2025-09-27,62,310!2025-09-28,63,310!2025-09-19,55,310!2025-10-06,67,310!2025-09-22,58,310!2025-09-22,58,310!2025-10-06,67,310-->

## proof when _D_ is a simple region

> {@{![If _D_ is a simple type of region with its boundary consisting of the curves _C_<sub>1</sub>, _C_<sub>2</sub>, _C_<sub>3</sub>, _C_<sub>4</sub>, half of Green's theorem can be demonstrated.](../../archives/Wikimedia%20Commons/Green%27s-theorem-simple-region.svg)}@}
>
> If {@{_D_ is a simple type of region}@} with its boundary {@{consisting of the curves _C_<sub>1</sub>, _C_<sub>2</sub>, _C_<sub>3</sub>, _C_<sub>4</sub>}@}, {@{half of Green's theorem}@} can be demonstrated. <!--SR:!2025-10-04,65,310!2025-09-27,62,310!2025-09-27,62,310!2025-09-22,58,310-->

The following is {@{a proof of half of the theorem for the simplified area _D_}@}, {@{a type I region where _C_<sub>1</sub> and _C_<sub>3</sub> are curves connected by vertical lines \(possibly of zero length\)}@}. A similar proof exists for {@{the other half of the theorem}@} when {@{_D_ is a type II region}@} where {@{_C_<sub>2</sub> and _C_<sub>4</sub> are curves connected by horizontal lines \(again, possibly of zero length\)}@}. Putting {@{these two parts together}@}, the theorem is {@{thus proven for regions of type III}@} \(defined as {@{regions which are both type I and type II}@}\). The general case can then be {@{deduced from this special case}@} by {@{decomposing _D_ into a set of type III regions}@}. <!--SR:!2025-09-28,63,310!2025-10-04,65,310!2025-10-05,66,310!2025-10-05,66,310!2025-09-30,61,310!2025-10-05,66,310!2025-10-06,67,310!2025-09-27,62,310!2025-09-18,54,310!2025-10-05,66,310-->

If it can be shown that {@{$$\oint _{C}L\,dx=\iint _{D}\left(-{\frac {\partial L}{\partial y} }\right)dA$$}@} __<a id="math 1">\(1\)</a>__ <p> and {@{$$\oint _{C}\ M\,dy=\iint _{D}\left({\frac {\partial M}{\partial x} }\right)dA$$}@} __<a id="math 2">\(2\)</a>__ <p> are true, then {@{Green's theorem follows immediately for the region D}@}. We can prove {@{\(__[1](#math%201)__\) easily for regions of type I, and \(__[2](#math%202)__\) for regions of type II}@}. Green's theorem then {@{follows for regions of type III}@}. <!--SR:!2025-09-21,57,310!2025-10-05,66,310!2025-10-06,67,310!2025-09-30,61,310!2025-10-05,66,310-->

Assume {@{region _D_ is a type I region and can thus be characterized}@}, as pictured on the right, by {@{$$D=\{(x,y)\mid a\leq x\leq b,g_{1}(x)\leq y\leq g_{2}(x)\}$$}@} where {@{_g_<sub>1</sub> and _g_<sub>2</sub> are [continuous functions](continuous%20function.md) on \[_a_, _b_\]}@}. Compute {@{the double integral in \(__[1](#math%201)__\)}@}: {@{$${\begin{aligned}\iint _{D}{\frac {\partial L}{\partial y} }\,dA&=\int _{a}^{b}\,\int _{g_{1}(x)}^{g_{2}(x)}{\frac {\partial L}{\partial y} }(x,y)\,dy\,dx\\&=\int _{a}^{b}\left[L(x,g_{2}(x))-L(x,g_{1}(x))\right]\,dx.\end{aligned} }$$}@} __<a id="math 3">\(3\)</a>__ <p> <!--SR:!2025-09-22,58,310!2025-09-28,63,310!2025-09-28,63,310!2025-09-27,62,310!2025-10-01,62,310-->

Now compute {@{the line integral in \(__[1](#math%201)__\)}@}. _C_ can be {@{rewritten as the union of four curves: _C_<sub>1</sub>, _C_<sub>2</sub>, _C_<sub>3</sub>, _C_<sub>4</sub>}@}. <!--SR:!2025-09-28,63,310!2025-09-22,58,310-->

With {@{_C_<sub>1</sub>}@}, use {@{the [parametric equations](parametric%20equation.md): _x_ = _x_, _y_ = _g_<sub>1</sub>\(_x_\), _a_ ≤ _x_ ≤ _b_}@}. Then {@{$$\int _{C_{1} }L(x,y)\,dx=\int _{a}^{b}L(x,g_{1}(x))\,dx.$$}@} <!--SR:!2025-10-06,67,310!2025-09-19,55,310!2025-10-03,67,310-->

With {@{_C_<sub>3</sub>}@}, use {@{the parametric equations: _x_ = _x_, _y_ = _g_<sub>2</sub>\(_x_\), _a_ ≤ _x_ ≤ _b_}@}. Then {@{$$\int _{C_{3} }L(x,y)\,dx=\int _{b}^{a}L(x,y)\,dx=-\int _{a}^{b}L(x,g_{2}(x))\,dx.$$}@}The integral {@{over _C_<sub>3</sub> is negated}@} because {@{it goes in the negative direction from _b_ to _a_}@}, as {@{_C_ is oriented positively \(anticlockwise\)}@}. On {@{_C_<sub>2</sub> and _C_<sub>4</sub>}@}, {@{_x_ remains constant}@}, meaning {@{$$\int _{C_{4} }L(x,y)\,dx=\int _{C_{2} }L(x,y)\,dx=0.$$}@} <!--SR:!2025-10-05,66,310!2025-10-06,67,310!2025-10-05,66,310!2025-10-06,67,310!2025-10-06,67,310!2025-10-05,66,310!2025-09-28,63,310!2025-10-06,67,310!2025-09-27,62,310-->

Therefore, {@{$${\begin{aligned}\oint _{C}L\,dx&=\int _{C_{1} }L(x,y)\,dx+\int _{C_{2} }L(x,y)\,dx+\int _{C_{3} }L(x,y)\,dx+\int _{C_{4} }L(x,y)\,dx\\&=\int _{a}^{b}L(x,g_{1}(x))\,dx-\int _{a}^{b}L(x,g_{2}(x))\,dx.\end{aligned} }$$}@} __<a id="math 4">\(4\)</a>__ <p> <!--SR:!2025-09-27,62,310-->

Combining {@{\(__[3](#math%203)__\) with \(__[4](#math%204)__\)}@}, we {@{get \(__[1](#math%201)__\) for regions of type I}@}. {@{A similar treatment using the same endpoints}@} yields {@{\(__[2](#math%202)__\) for regions of type II}@}. Putting {@{the two together}@}, we get {@{the result for regions of type III}@}. <!--SR:!2025-09-21,57,310!2025-09-20,56,310!2025-10-04,65,310!2025-10-04,65,310!2025-10-04,65,310!2025-10-02,63,310-->

## Proof for rectifiable Jordan curves

We are going to prove the following

> {@{__Theorem__}@}—Let $\Gamma$ be {@{a rectifiable, positively oriented [Jordan curve](Jordan%20curve%20theorem.md) in $\mathbb {R} ^{2}$}@} and let $R$ denote {@{its inner region \(annotation: with an overscore denoting borders are included\)}@}. Suppose that {@{$A,B:{\overline {R} }\to \mathbb {R}$ are continuous functions}@} with the property that {@{$A$ has second partial derivative \(annotation: not second order\) at every point of $R$}@}, {@{$B$ has first partial derivative at every point of $R$}@} and that {@{the functions $D_{1}B,D_{2}A:R\to \mathbb {R}$ are Riemann-integrable over $R$}@}. Then {@{$$\int _{\Gamma }(A\,dx+B\,dy)=\int _{R}\left(D_{1}B(x,y)-D_{2}A(x,y)\right)\,d(x,y).$$}@} <!--SR:!2025-09-22,58,310!2025-09-28,63,310!2025-10-05,66,310!2025-09-28,63,310!2025-10-04,65,310!2025-09-28,63,310!2025-09-21,57,310!2025-09-28,63,310-->

We need {@{the following lemmas}@} whose proofs can be found in:<sup>[\[3\]](#^ref-3)</sup> <!--SR:!2025-09-22,58,310-->

> {@{__Lemma 1 \(Decomposition Lemma\)__}@}—Assume $\Gamma$ is {@{a rectifiable, positively oriented Jordan curve in the plane}@} and let $R$ be {@{its inner region}@}. For {@{every positive real $\delta$}@}, let ${\mathcal {F} }(\delta )$ denote {@{the collection of squares in the plane bounded by the lines $x=m\delta ,y=m\delta$ \(i.e. a grid\)}@}, where {@{$m$ runs through the set of integers}@}. Then, for {@{this $\delta$}@}, there {@{exists a decomposition of ${\overline {R} }$ into a finite number of non-overlapping subregions}@} in such a manner that
>
> 1. i. Each one of {@{the subregions contained in $R$, say $R_{1},R_{2},\ldots ,R_{k}$ \(annotation: inner subregions\)}@}, is {@{a square from ${\mathcal {F} }(\delta )$}@}.
> 2. ii. Each one of {@{the remaining subregions, say $R_{k+1},\ldots ,R_{s}$ \(annotation: border subregions\)}@}, has {@{as boundary a rectifiable Jordan curve formed by a finite number of arcs of $\Gamma$ and parts of the sides of some square from ${\mathcal {F} }(\delta )$}@}.
> 3. iii. Each one of {@{the border regions $R_{k+1},\ldots ,R_{s}$}@} can be {@{enclosed in a square of edge-length $2\delta$}@}. \(annotation: Not sure if {@{$\delta$ in place of $2\delta$ is okay}@}...\)
> 4. iv. If {@{$\Gamma _{i}$ is the positively oriented boundary curve of $R_{i}$}@}, then {@{$\Gamma =\Gamma _{1}+\Gamma _{2}+\cdots +\Gamma _{s}$}@}.
> 5. v. {@{The number $s-k$ of border regions}@} is {@{no greater than $4\!\left({\frac {\Lambda }{\delta } }+1\right)$ \(annotation: the plus one is more for rounding up the value\)}@}, where {@{$\Lambda$ is the length of $\Gamma$ \(annotation: remember this notation\)}@}. \(annotation: To prove this, proceed via {@{a pigeonhole-like argument}@}. Start with {@{a point touching 4 squares}@}. This {@{explains the term $4(+1) = 4$}@}. Then you need to {@{extend the point to a line by at least $\delta$ to touch 2 new squares or $\sqrt 2 \delta$ to touch 3 new squares}@}. Thus, {@{each line segment of $\delta$ length}@} can {@{at most touch 4 \(3 is more strict, but 4 is for convenience\) new squares}@}. This explains {@{the term $4 \Lambda / \delta$}@}.\) <!--SR:!2025-09-20,56,310!2025-09-22,58,310!2025-10-01,62,310!2025-10-06,67,310!2025-10-05,66,310!2025-10-06,67,310!2025-10-06,67,310!2025-10-03,67,310!2025-09-28,63,310!2025-10-06,67,310!2025-10-05,66,310!2025-09-22,58,310!2025-09-18,54,310!2025-09-22,58,310!2025-09-28,63,310!2025-09-22,58,310!2025-10-06,67,310!2025-09-18,54,310!2025-10-06,67,310!2025-09-27,62,310!2025-09-22,58,310!2025-09-27,62,310!2025-09-19,55,310!2025-10-05,66,310!2025-09-20,56,310!2025-10-04,65,310!2025-09-28,63,310-->

<!-- markdownlint MD028 -->

> {@{__Lemma 2__}@}—Let $\Gamma$ be {@{a rectifiable curve \(annotation: not necessarily closed\) in the plane}@} and let {@{$\Delta _{\Gamma }(h)$ \(annotation: remember this notation\)}@} be {@{the set of points in the plane whose distance from \(the range of\) $\Gamma$ is at most $h$}@}. {@{The outer Jordan content of this set}@} satisfies {@{${\overline {c} }\,\,\Delta _{\Gamma }(h)\leq 2h\Lambda +\pi h^{2}$}@}.
>
> \(annotation: To prove this, {@{rectify the curve with line segments approximating it}@}. Then, consider {@{the set of points in the plane whose distance from the line segment of length $l$ is at most $h$}@}. Obviously, its outer Jordan content is {@{$2hl + \pi h^2$}@}. Then consider {@{joining two line segments}@}. The outer Jordan content is {@{added together, but then $\pi h^2$ is subtracted to avoid double counting}@}. By induction, we have the above formula. Note that $2h \Lambda + \pi h^2$ is {@{a upper bound}@} because {@{an area more than $\pi h^2$ may need to be subtracted to avoid double counting, e.g. a closed loop}@}.\) <!--SR:!2025-10-05,66,310!2025-09-22,58,310!2025-09-28,63,310!2025-10-06,67,310!2025-10-06,67,310!2025-09-27,62,310!2025-09-27,62,310!2025-09-27,62,310!2025-09-22,58,310!2025-09-28,63,310!2025-09-28,63,310!2025-10-06,67,310!2025-10-03,67,310-->

> {@{__Lemma 3__}@}—Let $\Gamma$ be {@{a rectifiable closed curve in $\mathbb {R} ^{2}$}@} and let {@{$f:{\text{range of } }\Gamma \to \mathbb {R}$ be a continuous function \(annotation: for integrability\)}@}. Then {@{$$\left\vert \int _{\Gamma }f(x,y)\,dy\right\vert \leq {\frac {1}{2} }\Lambda \Omega _{f},$$}@} and {@{$$\left\vert \int _{\Gamma }f(x,y)\,dx\right\vert \leq {\frac {1}{2} }\Lambda \Omega _{f},$$}@} where {@{$\Omega _{f}$ is the oscillation \(annotation: difference between max and min\) of $f$ on the range of $\Gamma$}@}.
>
> \(annotation: To prove this, consider {@{only one coordinate as we move along the closed curve and ignore other coordinates}@}. Since {@{the curve is closed}@}, {@{any movement in the coordinate in one direction must eventually be matched by movement in the opposite direction}@}. Then we consider {@{maximizing/minimizing the integral value}@}. Then, the curve {@{should move in the specified coordinate by $\Lambda / 2$ and $f$ equals the max/min value}@}, and then {@{infinitesimally small in any other coordinate}@}, and finally {@{move in the specified coordinate in the opposite direction by $\Lambda / 2$ and $f$ equals the min/max value}@}. Then clearly, {@{the maximum/minimum integral value is the expression in the previous paragraph}@}. \) <!--SR:!2025-09-27,62,310!2025-09-19,55,310!2025-09-21,57,310!2025-09-28,63,310!2025-09-27,62,310!2025-09-19,55,310!2025-10-06,67,310!2025-10-05,66,310!2025-09-22,58,310!2025-10-05,66,310!2025-10-06,67,310!2025-09-19,55,310!2025-10-02,63,310!2025-09-28,63,310-->

Now we are in position to prove the theorem:

{@{__Proof of Theorem.__}@} Let $\varepsilon$ be {@{an arbitrary positive [real number](real%20number.md)}@}. By {@{continuity of $A$, $B$ and compactness of ${\overline {R} }$}@}, given {@{$\varepsilon >0$}@}, there {@{exists $0<\delta <1$}@} such that whenever {@{two points of ${\overline {R} }$ are less than $2{\sqrt {2} }\,\delta$ apart}@}, {@{their images under $A,B$ are less than $\varepsilon$ apart}@}. For {@{this $\delta$}@}, consider {@{the decomposition given by the previous Lemma}@}. We have {@{$$\int _{\Gamma }A\,dx+B\,dy=\sum _{i=1}^{k}\int _{\Gamma _{i} }A\,dx+B\,dy\quad +\sum _{i=k+1}^{s}\int _{\Gamma _{i} }A\,dx+B\,dy.$$}@} \(annotation: This is {@{simply separating the inner regions and the border regions}@}.\) <!--SR:!2025-10-05,66,310!2025-09-22,58,310!2025-09-22,58,310!2025-09-27,62,310!2025-09-22,58,310!2025-10-05,66,310!2025-09-22,58,310!2025-10-05,66,310!2025-10-02,63,310!2025-09-21,57,310-->

Put {@{$\varphi :=D_{1}B-D_{2}A$}@}. \(annotation: Simply the integrand in the surface integral.\) <!--SR:!2025-09-22,58,310-->

For {@{each $i\in \{1,\ldots ,k\}$ \(annotation: inner regions\)}@}, the curve $\Gamma _{i}$ is {@{a positively oriented square}@}, for which {@{Green's formula holds}@}. Hence {@{$$\sum _{i=1}^{k}\int _{\Gamma _{i} }A\,dx+B\,dy=\sum _{i=1}^{k}\int _{R_{i} }\varphi =\int _{\bigcup _{i=1}^{k}R_{i} }\,\varphi .$$}@} <!--SR:!2025-10-06,67,310!2025-10-05,66,310!2025-09-27,62,310!2025-09-22,58,310--> \(annotation: This {@{handles the equality for inner regions}@}. Below we will show {@{the expression on both sides of Green's theorem can be made arbitrarily small on the border regions}@}.\)

{@{Every point of a border region}@} is {@{at a distance no greater than $2{\sqrt {2} }\,\delta$ from $\Gamma$}@}. Thus, if {@{$K$ is the union of all border regions}@}, then {@{$K\subset \Delta _{\Gamma }(2{\sqrt {2} }\,\delta )$}@}; hence {@{$c(K)\leq {\overline {c} }\,\Delta _{\Gamma }(2{\sqrt {2} }\,\delta )\leq 4{\sqrt {2} }\,\delta +8\pi \delta ^{2}$}@}, by {@{Lemma 2}@}. Notice that {@{$$\int _{R}\varphi \,\,-\int _{\bigcup _{i=1}^{k}R_{i} }\varphi =\int _{K}\varphi .$$}@} This yields {@{$$\left\vert \sum _{i=1}^{k}\int _{\Gamma _{i} }A\,dx+B\,dy\quad -\int _{R}\varphi \right\vert \leq M\delta (1+\pi {\sqrt {2} }\,\delta ){\text{ for some } }M>0.$$}@} \(annotation: {@{The left hand side of inequality}@} is {@{simply the area integral on the border regions \(with absolute taken\)}@}. On {@{the right hand side}@}, $M$ is due to {@{the function $\varphi$ being bounded}@}. It has also {@{absorbed $4\sqrt 2$}@} in the above expression.\) <!--SR:!2025-10-05,66,310!2025-09-19,55,310!2025-09-21,57,310!2025-09-22,58,310!2025-09-21,57,310!2025-09-28,63,310!2025-10-01,62,310!2025-09-28,63,310!2025-09-27,62,310!2025-09-05,41,290-->

We may as well {@{choose $\delta$}@} so that {@{the RHS of the last inequality is $<\varepsilon$}@}. <!--SR:!2025-09-30,61,310!2025-09-18,54,310--> \(annotation: This proves {@{the area integral on the border regions can be made arbitrarily small}@}. What remains is {@{to prove the same for the line integral on the border regions}@}.\)

{@{The remark in the beginning of this proof}@} implies that {@{the oscillations \(annotation: difference between max and min\) of $A$ and $B$ on every border region is at most $\varepsilon$}@}. We have {@{$$\left\vert \sum _{i=k+1}^{s}\int _{\Gamma _{i} }A\,dx+B\,dy\right\vert \leq \varepsilon \sum _{i=k+1}^{s}\Lambda _{i}.$$}@} By {@{Lemma 1\(iii\)}@}, {@{$$\sum _{i=k+1}^{s}\Lambda _{i}\leq \Lambda +(4\delta )\,4\!\left({\frac {\Lambda }{\delta } }+1\right)\leq 17\Lambda +16.$$}@} \(annotation: {@{The added term in the middle}@} is due to {@{portions of the square enclosing the border region}@}.\) {@{Combining these \(annotation: referring to that both the area and line integrals on the border regions can be made arbitrarily small\)}@}, we finally get {@{$$\left\vert \int _{\Gamma }A\,dx+B\,dy\quad -\int _{R}\varphi \right\vert <C\varepsilon ,$$}@} for {@{some $C>0$}@}. Since {@{this is true for every $\varepsilon >0$}@}, {@{we are done}@}. <!--SR:!2025-09-21,57,310!2025-10-04,65,310!2025-09-04,40,290!2025-09-20,56,310!2025-09-04,40,290!2025-10-06,67,310!2025-09-30,61,310!2025-10-06,67,310!2025-10-06,67,310!2025-10-02,63,310-->

## validity under different hypotheses

{@{The hypothesis of the last theorem}@} are {@{not the only ones under which Green's formula is true}@}. {@{Another common set of conditions}@} is the following: <!--SR:!2025-10-06,67,310!2025-09-20,56,310!2025-09-30,61,310-->

{@{The functions $A,B:{\overline {R} }\to \mathbb {R}$}@} are {@{still assumed to be continuous}@}. However, we now {@{require them to be Fréchet-differentiable at every point of $R$ \(annotation: instead of requiring certain first-order partial derivatives to exist\)}@}. This implies {@{the existence of all directional derivatives}@}, in particular {@{$D_{e_{i} }A=:D_{i}A,D_{e_{i} }B=:D_{i}B,\,i=1,2$}@}, where, {@{as usual, $(e_{1},e_{2})$ is the canonical ordered basis of $\mathbb {R} ^{2}$}@}. In addition, we require {@{the function $D_{1}B-D_{2}A$ to be Riemann-integrable over $R$ \(annotation: same as before\)}@}. <!--SR:!2025-09-19,55,310!2025-10-06,67,310!2025-10-01,62,310!2025-10-05,66,310!2025-10-01,62,310!2025-09-22,58,310!2025-09-19,55,310-->

As {@{a corollary of this}@}, we get {@{the Cauchy Integral Theorem for rectifiable Jordan curves}@}: <!--SR:!2025-09-30,61,310!2025-09-22,58,310-->

> {@{__Theorem \(Cauchy\)__}@}—If $\Gamma$ is {@{a rectifiable Jordan curve in $\mathbb {C}$}@} and if {@{$f:{\text{closure of inner region of } }\Gamma \to \mathbb {C}$ is a continuous mapping holomorphic throughout the inner region of $\Gamma$}@}, then {@{$$\int _{\Gamma }f=0,$$}@} the integral being {@{a complex contour integral}@}. <!--SR:!2025-09-22,58,310!2025-10-05,66,310!2025-10-06,67,310!2025-10-05,66,310!2025-09-27,62,310-->

<!-- markdownlint MD028 -->

> __Proof__
>
> We regard {@{the complex plane as $\mathbb {R} ^{2}$}@}. Now, define {@{$u,v:{\overline {R} }\to \mathbb {R}$}@} to be such that {@{$f(x+iy)=u(x,y)+iv(x,y)$}@}. These functions are {@{clearly continuous}@}. It is well known that $u$ and $v$ are {@{Fréchet-differentiable and that they satisfy the Cauchy-Riemann equations}@}: {@{$D_{1}v+D_{2}u=D_{1}u-D_{2}v={\text{zero function} }$ \(annotation: which is obviously Riemann-integrable\)}@}. <!--SR:!2025-10-03,67,310!2025-10-02,63,310!2025-09-18,54,310!2025-10-05,66,310!2025-10-02,63,310!2025-09-22,58,310-->

Now, analyzing {@{the sums used to define the complex contour integral in question}@}, it is easy to realize that {@{$$\int _{\Gamma }f=\int _{\Gamma }u\,dx-v\,dy\quad +i\int _{\Gamma }v\,dx+u\,dy,$$}@} {@{the integrals on the RHS}@} being {@{usual line integrals}@}. These remarks allow us to {@{apply Green's Theorem to each one of these line integrals}@}, {@{finishing the proof}@}. <!--SR:!2025-09-30,61,310!2025-09-27,62,310!2025-09-22,58,310!2025-09-28,63,310!2025-10-01,62,310!2025-09-22,58,310-->

## multiply-connected regions

{@{__Theorem.__}@} Let {@{$\Gamma _{0},\Gamma _{1},\ldots ,\Gamma _{n}$ be positively oriented rectifiable Jordan curves in $\mathbb {R} ^{2}$}@} satisfying {@{$${\begin{aligned}\Gamma _{i}\subset R_{0},&&{\text{if } }1\leq i\leq n\\\Gamma _{i}\subset \mathbb {R} ^{2}\setminus {\overline {R} }_{j},&&{\text{if } }1\leq i,j\leq n{\text{ and } }i\neq j,\end{aligned} }$$}@} \(annotation: The overline denotes {@{including the region border}@}.\) where {@{$R_{i}$ is the inner region of $\Gamma _{i}$}@}. Let {@{$$D=R_{0}\setminus ({\overline {R} }_{1}\cup {\overline {R} }_{2}\cup \cdots \cup {\overline {R} }_{n}).$$}@} <!--SR:!2025-09-22,58,310!2025-09-21,57,310!2025-10-06,67,310!2025-09-19,55,310!2025-09-22,58,310!2025-10-05,66,310-->

Suppose {@{$p:{\overline {D} }\to \mathbb {R}$ and $q:{\overline {D} }\to \mathbb {R}$}@} are {@{continuous functions whose restriction to $D$ is Fréchet-differentiable}@}. If {@{the function $$(x,y)\longmapsto {\frac {\partial q}{\partial e_{1} } }(x,y)-{\frac {\partial p}{\partial e_{2} } }(x,y)$$}@} is {@{Riemann-integrable over $D$}@}, then {@{$${\begin{aligned}&\int _{\Gamma _{0} }p(x,y)\,dx+q(x,y)\,dy-\sum _{i=1}^{n}\int _{\Gamma _{i} }p(x,y)\,dx+q(x,y)\,dy\\[5pt]={}&\int _{D}\left\{ {\frac {\partial q}{\partial e_{1} } }(x,y)-{\frac {\partial p}{\partial e_{2} } }(x,y)\right\}\,d(x,y).\end{aligned} }$$}@} \(annotation: Effectively, this is saying {@{Green's theorem can be applied to surface with holes}@}.\) <!--SR:!2025-10-06,67,310!2025-10-02,63,310!2025-09-18,54,310!2025-09-27,62,310!2025-09-27,62,310!2025-09-28,63,310-->

## relationship to Stokes' theorem

Green's theorem is {@{a special case of the [Kelvin–Stokes theorem](Stokes'%20theorem.md#theorem)}@}, when {@{applied to a region in the $xy$-plane}@}. <!--SR:!2025-10-04,65,310!2025-10-02,63,310-->

We can {@{augment the two-dimensional field into a three-dimensional field}@} with {@{a _z_ component that is always 0}@}. Write __F__ for {@{the [vector](Euclidean%20vector.md)-valued function $\mathbf {F} =(L,M,0)$}@}. Start with {@{the left side of Green's theorem}@}: {@{$$\oint _{C}(L\,dx+M\,dy)=\oint _{C}(L,M,0)\cdot (dx,dy,dz)=\oint _{C}\mathbf {F} \cdot d\mathbf {r} .$$}@} <!--SR:!2025-09-20,56,310!2025-09-27,62,310!2025-09-28,63,310!2025-09-27,62,310!2025-09-20,56,310-->

{@{The Kelvin–Stokes theorem}@}: {@{$$\oint _{C}\mathbf {F} \cdot d\mathbf {r} =\iint _{S}\nabla \times \mathbf {F} \cdot \mathbf {\hat {n} } \,dS.$$}@} <!--SR:!2025-09-21,57,310!2025-09-05,41,290-->

{@{The surface $S$}@} is {@{just the region in the plane $D$}@}, with {@{the unit normal $\mathbf {\hat {n} }$}@} defined {@{\(by convention\) to have a positive z component in order to match the "positive orientation" definitions for both theorems}@}. <!--SR:!2025-09-28,63,310!2025-09-29,63,310!2025-09-30,61,310!2025-10-06,67,310-->

{@{The expression inside the integral}@} becomes {@{$$\nabla \times \mathbf {F} \cdot \mathbf {\hat {n} } =\left[\left({\frac {\partial 0}{\partial y} }-{\frac {\partial M}{\partial z} }\right)\mathbf {i} +\left({\frac {\partial L}{\partial z} }-{\frac {\partial 0}{\partial x} }\right)\mathbf {j} +\left({\frac {\partial M}{\partial x} }-{\frac {\partial L}{\partial y} }\right)\mathbf {k} \right]\cdot \mathbf {k} =\left({\frac {\partial M}{\partial x} }-{\frac {\partial L}{\partial y} }\right).$$}@} <!--SR:!2025-09-20,56,310!2025-10-06,67,310-->

Thus we get {@{the right side of Green's theorem}@} {@{$$\iint _{S}\nabla \times \mathbf {F} \cdot \mathbf {\hat {n} } \,dS=\iint _{D}\left({\frac {\partial M}{\partial x} }-{\frac {\partial L}{\partial y} }\right)\,dA.$$}@} <!--SR:!2025-09-18,54,310!2025-10-01,62,310-->

Green's theorem is also {@{a straightforward result of the general Stokes' theorem}@} using {@{[differential forms](differential%20form.md) and [exterior derivatives](exterior%20derivative.md)}@}: {@{$$\oint _{C}L\,dx+M\,dy=\oint _{\partial D}\!\omega =\int _{D}d\omega =\int _{D}{\frac {\partial L}{\partial y} }\,dy\wedge \,dx+{\frac {\partial M}{\partial x} }\,dx\wedge \,dy=\iint _{D}\left({\frac {\partial M}{\partial x} }-{\frac {\partial L}{\partial y} }\right)\,dx\,dy.$$}@} <!--SR:!2025-09-22,58,310!2025-09-22,58,310!2025-10-01,62,310-->

## relationship to the divergence theorem

Considering {@{only two-dimensional vector fields}@}, Green's theorem is {@{equivalent to the two-dimensional version of the [divergence theorem](divergence%20theorem.md)}@}: {@{$$\iint _{D}\left(\nabla \cdot \mathbf {F} \right)dA=\oint _{C}\mathbf {F} \cdot \mathbf {\hat {n} } \,ds,$$}@} where $\nabla \cdot \mathbf {F}$ is {@{the divergence on the two-dimensional vector field $\mathbf {F}$}@}, and $\mathbf {\hat {n} }$ is {@{the outward-pointing unit normal vector on the boundary}@}. <!--SR:!2025-09-28,63,310!2025-09-19,55,310!2025-09-28,63,310!2025-09-28,63,310!2025-09-27,62,310-->

To see this, consider {@{the unit normal $\mathbf {\hat {n} }$}@} in the right side of the equation. Since in {@{Green's theorem $d\mathbf {r} =(dx,dy)$}@} is {@{a vector pointing tangential along the curve}@}, and the curve _C_ is {@{the positively oriented \(i.e. anticlockwise\) curve along the boundary}@}, {@{an outward normal}@} would be {@{a vector which points 90° to the right \(annotation: clockwise\) of this}@}; one choice would be {@{$(dy,-dx)$}@}. {@{The length of this vector}@} is {@{${\sqrt {dx^{2}+dy^{2} } }=ds$}@}. So {@{$(dy,-dx)=\mathbf {\hat {n} } \,ds$}@}. <!--SR:!2025-10-05,66,310!2025-10-06,67,310!2025-09-22,58,310!2025-10-04,65,310!2025-09-28,63,310!2025-09-27,62,310!2025-09-27,62,310!2025-10-05,66,310!2025-10-02,63,310!2025-09-20,56,310-->

Start with {@{the left side of Green's theorem}@}: {@{$$\oint _{C}(L\,dx+M\,dy)=\oint _{C}(M,-L)\cdot (dy,-dx)=\oint _{C}(M,-L)\cdot \mathbf {\hat {n} } \,ds.$$}@} Applying {@{the two-dimensional divergence theorem with $\mathbf {F} =(M,-L)$}@}, we get {@{the right side of Green's theorem}@}: {@{$$\oint _{C}(M,-L)\cdot \mathbf {\hat {n} } \,ds=\iint _{D}\left(\nabla \cdot (M,-L)\right)\,dA=\iint _{D}\left({\frac {\partial M}{\partial x} }-{\frac {\partial L}{\partial y} }\right)\,dA.$$}@} <!--SR:!2025-09-18,54,310!2025-09-18,54,310!2025-09-27,62,310!2025-10-04,65,310!2025-09-18,54,310-->

## area calculation

Green's theorem can be used to {@{compute area by line integral}@}.<sup>[\[4\]](#^ref-4)</sup> {@{The area of a planar region $D$}@} is given by {@{$$A=\iint _{D}dA.$$}@} <!--SR:!2025-09-27,62,310!2025-10-02,63,310!2025-10-05,66,310-->

Choose {@{$L$ and $M$ such that ${\frac {\partial M}{\partial x} }-{\frac {\partial L}{\partial y} }=1$}@}, the area is given by {@{$$A=\oint _{C}(L\,dx+M\,dy).$$}@} <!--SR:!2025-10-05,66,310!2025-10-05,66,310-->

{@{Possible formulas for the area of $D$}@} include<sup>[\[4\]](#^ref-4)</sup> {@{$$A=\oint _{C}x\,dy=-\oint _{C}y\,dx={\tfrac {1}{2} }\oint _{C}(-y\,dx+x\,dy).$$}@} <!--SR:!2025-09-29,63,310!2025-09-18,54,310-->

## history

It is named after {@{[George Green](George%20Green%20(mathematician).md)}@}, who stated a similar result in {@{an 1828 paper titled _[An Essay on the Application of Mathematical Analysis to the Theories of Electricity and Magnetism](An%20Essay%20on%20the%20Application%20of%20Mathematical%20Analysis%20to%20the%20Theories%20of%20Electricity%20and%20Magnetism.md)_}@}. In {@{1846, [Augustin-Louis Cauchy](Augustin-Louis%20Cauchy.md)}@} published {@{a paper stating Green's theorem as the penultimate sentence}@}. This is in fact {@{the first printed version of Green's theorem in the form appearing in modern textbooks}@}. George Green, _An Essay on the Application of Mathematical Analysis to the Theories of Electricity and Magnetism_ \(Nottingham, England: T. Wheelhouse, 1828\). Green did not actually derive the form of "Green's theorem" which appears in this article; rather, he derived a form of the "divergence theorem", which appears on [pages 10–12](https://books.google.com/books?id=GwYXAAAAYAAJ&pg=PA10) of his _Essay_.In 1846, the form of "Green's theorem" which appears in this article was first published, without proof, in an article by [Augustin Cauchy](Augustin-Louis%20Cauchy.md): A. Cauchy \(1846\) ["Sur les intégrales qui s'étendent à tous les points d'une courbe fermée"](https://archive.org/stream/ComptesRendusAcademieDesSciences0023/ComptesRendusAcadmieDesSciences-Tome023-Juillet-dcembre1846#page/n254/mode/1up) \(On integrals that extend over all of the points of a closed curve\), _Comptes rendus_, __23__: 251–255. \(The equation appears at the bottom of page 254, where \(_S_\) denotes the line integral of a function _k_ along the curve _s_ that encloses the area _S_.\) {@{A proof of the theorem}@} was finally provided in {@{1851 by [Bernhard Riemann](Bernhard%20Riemann.md)}@} in his inaugural dissertation: Bernhard Riemann \(1851\) [_Grundlagen für eine allgemeine Theorie der Functionen einer veränderlichen complexen Grösse_](https://books.google.com/books?id=PpALAAAAYAAJ&pg=PP5) \(Basis for a general theory of functions of a variable complex quantity\), \(Göttingen, \(Germany\): Adalbert Rente, 1867\); see pages 8–9.<sup>[\[5\]](#^ref-5)</sup> <!--SR:!2025-09-27,62,310!2025-10-06,67,310!2025-10-04,65,310!2025-09-22,58,310!2025-10-06,67,310!2025-09-28,63,310!2025-09-22,58,310-->
