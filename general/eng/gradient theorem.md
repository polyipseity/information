{@{The __gradient theorem__, also known as the __fundamental theorem of calculus for line integrals__}@}, says that {@{a [line integral](line%20integral.md) through a [gradient field](conservative%20vector%20field.md) can be evaluated by evaluating the original scalar field at the endpoints of the curve}@}. The theorem is {@{a generalization of the [second fundamental theorem of calculus](fundamental%20theorem%20of%20calculus.md)}@} to {@{any curve in a plane or space \(generally _n_-dimensional\) rather than just the real line}@}. <!--SR:!2025-09-24,65,310!2025-09-24,65,310!2026-05-20,246,330!2025-09-20,61,310-->

If {@{_φ_ : _U_ ⊆ __R__<sup>_n_</sup> → __R__ is a [differentiable function](differentiable%20function.md)}@} and {@{_γ_ a [differentiable](differentiable%20function.md) curve in _U_}@} which {@{starts at a point __p__ and ends at a point __q__}@}, then {@{$$\int _{\gamma }\nabla \varphi (\mathbf {r} )\cdot \mathrm {d} \mathbf {r} =\varphi \left(\mathbf {q} \right)-\varphi \left(\mathbf {p} \right)$$}@} where {@{∇<!-- markdown separator -->_φ_ denotes the gradient vector field of _φ_}@}. <!--SR:!2025-09-19,60,310!2025-09-18,59,310!2026-05-27,252,330!2025-09-20,61,310!2026-05-28,253,330-->

{@{The gradient theorem}@} implies that {@{line integrals through gradient fields are [path-independent](conservative%20vector%20field.md#path%20independence)}@}. In {@{physics}@} this theorem is {@{one of the ways of defining a [_conservative_ force](conservative%20force.md)}@}. By {@{placing _φ_ as potential}@}, {@{∇<!-- markdown separator -->_φ_ is a [conservative field](conservative%20field.md)}@}. {@{[Work](work%20(physics).md) done by conservative forces}@} {@{does not depend on the path followed by the object}@}, but {@{only the end points, as the above equation shows}@}. <!--SR:!2025-09-23,64,310!2025-09-26,67,310!2025-09-18,59,310!2025-09-23,64,310!2025-09-25,66,310!2025-09-21,62,310!2026-05-22,248,330!2025-09-22,63,310!2025-09-20,61,310-->

{@{The gradient theorem}@} also has {@{an interesting converse}@}: {@{any path-independent vector field}@} can be {@{expressed as the gradient of a [scalar field](scalar%20field.md)}@}. Just like {@{the gradient theorem itself}@}, this converse has {@{many striking consequences and applications in both pure and applied mathematics}@}. <!--SR:!2026-05-29,254,330!2025-09-20,61,310!2025-09-23,64,310!2025-09-19,60,310!2025-09-18,59,310!2025-09-26,67,310-->

## proof

If {@{_φ_ is a [differentiable function](differentiable%20function.md) from some [open subset](open%20set.md) _U_ ⊆ __R__<sup>_n_</sup> to __R__}@} and {@{__r__ is a differentiable function from some closed [interval](interval%20(mathematics).md) \[_a_, _b_\] to _U_}@} \(Note that __r__ is {@{differentiable at the interval endpoints _a_ and _b_}@}. To do this, __r__ is {@{defined on an interval that is larger than and includes \[_a_, _b_\]}@}.\), then by {@{the [multivariate chain rule](chain%20rule.md#multivariable%20case)}@}, {@{the [composite function](function%20composition.md) _φ_ ∘ __r__}@} is {@{differentiable on \[_a_, _b_\]}@}: {@{$${\frac {\mathrm {d} }{\mathrm {d} t} }(\varphi \circ \mathbf {r} )(t)=\nabla \varphi (\mathbf {r} (t))\cdot \mathbf {r} '(t)$$}@} for {@{all _t_ in \[_a_, _b_\]}@}. Here the ⋅ denotes {@{the [dot product](dot%20product.md)}@}. <!--SR:!2025-09-21,62,310!2025-09-20,61,310!2025-09-26,67,310!2025-09-22,63,310!2026-05-22,248,330!2025-09-21,62,310!2025-09-23,64,310!2025-09-24,65,310!2025-09-24,65,310!2025-09-23,64,310-->

Now suppose {@{the domain _U_ of _φ_ contains the differentiable curve _γ_ with endpoints __p__ and __q__}@}. \(This is {@{[oriented](orientation%20(vector%20space).md) in the direction from __p__ to __q__}@}\). If __r__ {@{[parametrizes](parametrization%20(geometry).md) _γ_ for _t_ in \[_a_, _b_\]}@} \(i.e., __r__ represents {@{_γ_ as a function of _t_}@}\), then {@{$${\begin{aligned}\int _{\gamma }\nabla \varphi (\mathbf {r} )\cdot \mathrm {d} \mathbf {r} &=\int _{a}^{b}\nabla \varphi (\mathbf {r} (t))\cdot \mathbf {r} '(t)\mathrm {d} t\\&=\int _{a}^{b}{\frac {d}{dt} }\varphi (\mathbf {r} (t))\mathrm {d} t=\varphi (\mathbf {r} (b))-\varphi (\mathbf {r} (a))=\varphi \left(\mathbf {q} \right)-\varphi \left(\mathbf {p} \right),\end{aligned} }$$}@} where {@{the [definition of a line integral](line%20integral.md#line%20integral%20of%20a%20vector%20field) is used}@} in the first equality, {@{the above equation is used}@} in the second equality, and {@{the [second fundamental theorem of calculus](fundamental%20theorem%20of%20calculus.md#second%20part) is used}@} in the third equality.<sup>[\[1\]](#^ref-1)</sup> <!--SR:!2025-09-24,65,310!2025-09-23,64,310!2025-09-21,62,310!2026-05-27,252,330!2025-09-25,66,310!2025-09-23,64,310!2025-09-24,65,310!2026-01-20,152,310-->

Even if {@{the gradient theorem \(also called _fundamental theorem of calculus for line integrals_\)}@} has been {@{proved for a differentiable \(so looked as smooth\) curve}@} so far, the theorem is also {@{proved for a piecewise-smooth curve}@} since this curve is {@{made by joining multiple differentiable curves}@} so the proof for this curve is {@{made by the proof per differentiable curve component}@}.<sup>[\[2\]](#^ref-2)</sup> <!--SR:!2025-09-25,66,310!2026-05-20,246,330!2026-05-23,249,330!2025-09-18,59,310!2025-09-20,61,310-->

## examples

### example 1

Suppose _γ_ ⊂ __R__<sup>2</sup> is {@{the circular arc oriented counterclockwise from \(5, 0\) to \(−4, 3\)}@}. Using {@{the [definition of a line integral](line%20integral.md#line%20integral%20of%20a%20vector%20field)}@}, {@{$${\begin{aligned}\int _{\gamma }y\,\mathrm {d} x+x\,\mathrm {d} y&=\int _{0}^{\pi -\tan ^{-1}\!\left({\frac {3}{4} }\right)}((5\sin t)(-5\sin t)+(5\cos t)(5\cos t))\,\mathrm {d} t\\&=\int _{0}^{\pi -\tan ^{-1}\!\left({\frac {3}{4} }\right)}25\left(-\sin ^{2}t+\cos ^{2}t\right)\mathrm {d} t\\&=\int _{0}^{\pi -\tan ^{-1}\!\left({\frac {3}{4} }\right)}25\cos(2t)\mathrm {d} t\ =\ \left.{\tfrac {25}{2} }\sin(2t)\right|_{0}^{\pi -\tan ^{-1}\!\left({\tfrac {3}{4} }\right)}\\[.5em]&={\tfrac {25}{2} }\sin \left(2\pi -2\tan ^{-1}\!\!\left({\tfrac {3}{4} }\right)\right)\\[.5em]&=-{\tfrac {25}{2} }\sin \left(2\tan ^{-1}\!\!\left({\tfrac {3}{4} }\right)\right)\ =\ -{\frac {25(3/4)}{(3/4)^{2}+1} }=-12.\end{aligned} }$$}@} <!--SR:!2025-09-25,66,310!2026-05-25,251,330!2026-01-14,132,290-->

This result can be {@{obtained much more simply}@} by {@{noticing that the function $f(x,y)=xy$ has gradient $\nabla f(x,y)=(y,x)$}@}, so by {@{the Gradient Theorem}@}: {@{$$\int _{\gamma }y\,\mathrm {d} x+x\,\mathrm {d} y=\int _{\gamma }\nabla (xy)\cdot (\mathrm {d} x,\mathrm {d} y)\ =\ xy\,|_{(5,0)}^{(-4,3)}=-4\cdot 3-5\cdot 0=-12.$$}@} <!--SR:!2025-09-25,66,310!2025-09-21,62,310!2025-09-19,60,310!2026-05-28,253,330-->

### example 2

For {@{a more abstract example}@}, suppose _γ_ ⊂ __R__<sup>_n_</sup> has {@{endpoints __p__, __q__, with orientation from __p__ to __q__}@}. For {@{__u__ in __R__<sup>_n_</sup>}@}, let \|__u__\| denote {@{the [Euclidean norm](Euclidean%20norm.md#Euclidean%20norm) of __u__}@}. If {@{_α_ ≥ 1 \(annotation: if smaller than 1, then the integrand of the left hand side is undefined at the origin\) is a real number}@}, then {@{$${\begin{aligned}\int _{\gamma }|\mathbf {x} |^{\alpha -1}\mathbf {x} \cdot \mathrm {d} \mathbf {x} &={\frac {1}{\alpha +1} }\int _{\gamma }(\alpha +1)|\mathbf {x} |^{(\alpha +1)-2}\mathbf {x} \cdot \mathrm {d} \mathbf {x} \\&={\frac {1}{\alpha +1} }\int _{\gamma }\nabla |\mathbf {x} |^{\alpha +1}\cdot \mathrm {d} \mathbf {x} ={\frac {|\mathbf {q} |^{\alpha +1}-|\mathbf {p} |^{\alpha +1} }{\alpha +1} }\end{aligned} }$$}@} Here the final equality {@{follows by the gradient theorem}@}, since {@{the function _f_\(__x__\) = \|__x__\|<sup>_α_+1</sup> is differentiable on __R__<sup>_n_</sup> if _α_ ≥ 1}@}. <!--SR:!2025-09-21,62,310!2025-09-25,66,310!2025-09-20,61,310!2025-09-20,61,310!2026-05-27,252,330!2026-01-16,134,290!2025-09-23,64,310!2025-12-07,107,290-->

If {@{_α_ \< 1}@} then this equality {@{will still hold in most cases}@}, but {@{caution must be taken if _γ_ passes through or encloses the origin}@}, because {@{the integrand vector field \|__x__\|<sup>_α_ − 1</sup>__x__ will fail to be defined there}@}. However, {@{the case _α_ = −1 is somewhat different}@}; in this case, {@{the integrand becomes \|__x__\|<sup>−2</sup>__x__ = ∇\(log \|__x__\|\)}@}, so that {@{the final equality becomes log \|__q__\| − log \|__p__\|}@}. <!--SR:!2025-09-24,65,310!2025-09-26,67,310!2025-09-22,63,310!2026-05-27,252,330!2026-05-24,250,330!2026-03-18,193,310!2026-05-21,247,330-->

Note that if {@{_n_ = 1}@}, then this example is {@{simply a slight variant of the familiar [power rule](power%20rule.md) from single-variable calculus}@}. <!--SR:!2025-09-18,59,310!2025-09-21,62,310-->

### example 3

Suppose there are {@{_n_ [point charges](point%20particle.md#point%20charge) arranged in three-dimensional space}@}, and the _i_-th point charge has {@{[charge](electric%20charge.md) _Q_<sub>_i_</sub> and is located at position __p__<sub>_i_</sub> in __R__<sup>3</sup>}@}. We would like to {@{calculate the [work](work%20(physics).md) done on a particle of charge _q_}@} as {@{it travels from a point __a__ to a point __b__ in __R__<sup>3</sup>}@}. Using {@{[Coulomb's law](Coulomb's%20law.md)}@}, we can {@{easily determine that the [force](force.md) on the particle at position __r__}@} will be {@{$$\mathbf {F} (\mathbf {r} )=kq\sum _{i=1}^{n}{\frac {Q_{i}(\mathbf {r} -\mathbf {p} _{i})}{\left|\mathbf {r} -\mathbf {p} _{i}\right|^{3} } }$$}@} Here \|__u__\| denotes {@{the [Euclidean norm](Euclidean%20norm.md#Euclidean%20norm) of the vector __u__ in __R__<sup>3</sup>}@}, and {@{_k_ = 1/\(4<!-- markdown separator -->_πε_<sub>0</sub>\), where _ε_<sub>0</sub> is the [vacuum permittivity](vacuum%20permittivity.md)}@}. <!--SR:!2026-05-24,250,330!2025-09-21,62,310!2025-09-26,67,310!2025-09-19,60,310!2025-09-22,63,310!2025-09-18,59,310!2026-03-13,189,310!2025-09-25,66,310!2025-09-26,67,310-->

Let _γ_ {@{⊂ __R__<sup>3</sup> − {__p__<sub>1</sub>, ..., __p__<sub>_n_</sub>} be an arbitrary differentiable curve from __a__ to __b__}@}. Then {@{the work done on the particle}@} is {@{$$W=\int _{\gamma }\mathbf {F} (\mathbf {r} )\cdot \mathrm {d} \mathbf {r} =\int _{\gamma }\left(kq\sum _{i=1}^{n}{\frac {Q_{i}(\mathbf {r} -\mathbf {p} _{i})}{\left|\mathbf {r} -\mathbf {p} _{i}\right|^{3} } }\right)\cdot \mathrm {d} \mathbf {r} =kq\sum _{i=1}^{n}\left(Q_{i}\int _{\gamma }{\frac {\mathbf {r} -\mathbf {p} _{i} }{\left|\mathbf {r} -\mathbf {p} _{i}\right|^{3} } }\cdot \mathrm {d} \mathbf {r} \right)$$}@} <!--SR:!2026-05-29,254,330!2025-09-23,64,310!2026-01-29,159,310-->

Now for {@{each _i_}@}, direct computation shows that {@{$${\frac {\mathbf {r} -\mathbf {p} _{i} }{\left|\mathbf {r} -\mathbf {p} _{i}\right|^{3} } }=-\nabla {\frac {1}{\left|\mathbf {r} -\mathbf {p} _{i}\right|} }.$$}@} <!--SR:!2025-09-18,59,310!2026-05-30,255,330-->

Thus, continuing {@{from above and using the gradient theorem}@}, {@{$$W=-kq\sum _{i=1}^{n}\left(Q_{i}\int _{\gamma }\nabla {\frac {1}{\left|\mathbf {r} -\mathbf {p} _{i}\right|} }\cdot \mathrm {d} \mathbf {r} \right)=kq\sum _{i=1}^{n}Q_{i}\left({\frac {1}{\left|\mathbf {a} -\mathbf {p} _{i}\right|} }-{\frac {1}{\left|\mathbf {b} -\mathbf {p} _{i}\right|} }\right)$$}@} <!--SR:!2025-09-21,62,310!2026-05-22,248,330-->

We are finished. Of course, we could have {@{easily completed this calculation}@} using {@{the powerful language of [electrostatic potential](electric%20potential.md) or [electrostatic potential energy](electric%20potential%20energy.md)}@} \(with the familiar formulas {@{_W_ = −Δ<!-- markdown separator -->_U_ = −<!-- markdown separator -->_q_<!-- markdown separator -->Δ<!-- markdown separator -->_V_}@}\). However, we have {@{not yet _defined_ potential or potential energy}@}, because {@{the _converse_ of the gradient theorem is required}@} to {@{prove that these are well-defined, differentiable functions and that these formulas hold}@} \([see below](#example%20of%20the%20converse%20principle)\). Thus, we have {@{solved this problem}@} using {@{only Coulomb's Law, the definition of work, and the gradient theorem}@}. <!--SR:!2025-09-24,65,310!2026-05-23,249,330!2025-09-24,65,310!2025-09-22,63,310!2025-09-19,60,310!2025-09-25,66,310!2026-05-26,251,330!2025-09-18,59,310-->

## converse of the gradient theorem

{@{The gradient theorem}@} states that if {@{the vector field __F__ is the gradient of some scalar-valued function}@} \(i.e., if {@{__F__ is [conservative](conservative%20vector%20field.md)}@}\), then {@{__F__ is a path-independent vector field}@} \(i.e., {@{the integral of __F__ over some piecewise-differentiable curve is dependent only on end points}@}\). This theorem has {@{a powerful converse}@}: <!--SR:!2025-09-24,65,310!2025-09-23,64,310!2025-09-18,59,310!2025-09-24,65,310!2025-09-23,64,310!2025-09-26,67,310-->

> __Theorem__— If __F__ is a path-independent vector field, ::@:: then __F__ is the gradient of some scalar-valued function.<sup>[\[3\]](#^ref-3)</sup> <!--SR:!2025-09-19,60,310!2025-09-22,63,310-->

It is straightforward to show that {@{a vector field is path-independent}@} if and only if {@{the integral of the vector field over every closed loop in its domain is zero}@}. Thus {@{the converse can alternatively be stated as follows}@}: If {@{the integral of __F__ over every closed loop in the domain of __F__ is zero}@}, then {@{__F__ is the gradient of some scalar-valued function}@}. <!--SR:!2025-09-25,66,310!2025-09-21,62,310!2025-09-22,63,310!2025-09-26,67,310!2025-09-25,66,310-->

### proof of the converse

Suppose _U_ is {@{an [open](open%20set.md), [path-connected](connected%20space.md#path%20connectedness) subset of __R__<sup>_n_</sup>}@}, and __F__ : {@{_U_ → __R__<sup>_n_</sup> is a [continuous](continuous%20function.md) and path-independent vector field}@}. Fix {@{some element __a__ of _U_}@}, and define _f_ : {@{_U_ → __R__ by $$f(\mathbf {x} ):=\int _{\gamma [\mathbf {a} ,\mathbf {x} ]}\mathbf {F} (\mathbf {u} )\cdot \mathrm {d} \mathbf {u}$$}@} Here _γ_\[__a__, __x__\] is {@{any \(differentiable\) curve in _U_ originating at __a__ and terminating at __x__}@}. We know that {@{_f_ is [well-defined](well-defined.md) because __F__ is path-independent}@}. <!--SR:!2025-09-21,62,310!2025-09-25,66,310!2025-09-22,63,310!2025-09-19,60,310!2025-09-19,60,310!2025-09-20,61,310-->

Let __v__ be {@{any nonzero vector in __R__<sup>_n_</sup>}@}. By {@{the definition of the [directional derivative](directional%20derivative.md)}@}, {@{$${\begin{aligned}{\frac {\partial f(\mathbf {x} )}{\partial \mathbf {v} } }&=\lim _{t\to 0}{\frac {f(\mathbf {x} +t\mathbf {v} )-f(\mathbf {x} )}{t} }\\&=\lim _{t\to 0}{\frac {\int _{\gamma [\mathbf {a} ,\mathbf {x} +t\mathbf {v} ]}\mathbf {F} (\mathbf {u} )\cdot \mathrm {d} \mathbf {u} -\int _{\gamma [\mathbf {a} ,\mathbf {x} ]}\mathbf {F} (\mathbf {u} )\cdot d\mathbf {u} }{t} }\\&=\lim _{t\to 0}{\frac {1}{t} }\int _{\gamma [\mathbf {x} ,\mathbf {x} +t\mathbf {v} ]}\mathbf {F} (\mathbf {u} )\cdot \mathrm {d} \mathbf {u} \end{aligned} }$$}@} To {@{calculate the integral within the final limit}@}, we must {@{[parametrize](parametrization%20(geometry).md) _γ_\[__x__, __x__ + _t_<!-- markdown separator -->__v__\]}@}. Since {@{__F__ is path-independent, _U_ is open, and _t_ is approaching zero}@}, we may assume that {@{this path is a straight line}@}, and {@{parametrize it as __u__\(_s_\) = __x__ + _s_<!-- markdown separator -->__v__ for 0 \< _s_ \< _t_}@}. Now, since {@{__u'__\(_s_\) = __v__}@}, the limit becomes {@{$$\lim _{t\to 0}{\frac {1}{t} }\int _{0}^{t}\mathbf {F} (\mathbf {u} (s))\cdot \mathbf {u} '(s)\,\mathrm {d} s={\frac {\mathrm {d} }{\mathrm {d} t} }\int _{0}^{t}\mathbf {F} (\mathbf {x} +s\mathbf {v} )\cdot \mathbf {v} \,\mathrm {d} s{\bigg |}_{t=0}=\mathbf {F} (\mathbf {x} )\cdot \mathbf {v}$$}@} where the first equality is from {@{[the definition of the derivative](derivative.md#definition) with a fact that the integral is equal to 0 at _t_ = 0}@}, and the second equality is from {@{the [first fundamental theorem of calculus](fundamental%20theorem%20of%20calculus.md#first%20part)}@}. Thus we have {@{a formula for ∂<sub>__v__</sub>_f_}@}, \(one of ways to {@{represent the [directional derivative](directional%20derivative.md)}@}\) where {@{__v__ is arbitrary}@}; for {@{$f(\mathbf {x} ):=\int _{\gamma [\mathbf {a} ,\mathbf {x} ]}\mathbf {F} (\mathbf {u} )\cdot \mathrm {d} \mathbf {u}$}@} \(see its full definition above\), its {@{directional derivative with respect to __v__}@} is {@{$${\frac {\partial f(\mathbf {x} )}{\partial \mathbf {v} } }=\partial _{\mathbf {v} }f(\mathbf {x} )=D_{\mathbf {v} }f(\mathbf {x} )=\mathbf {F} (\mathbf {x} )\cdot \mathbf {v}$$}@} where {@{the first two equalities just show different representations of the directional derivative}@}. According to {@{[the definition of the gradient](gradient.md#definition) of a scalar function _f_}@}, {@{$\nabla f(\mathbf {x} )=\mathbf {F} (\mathbf {x} )$}@}, thus we have {@{found a scalar-valued function _f_ whose gradient is the path-independent vector field __F__}@} \(i.e., __F__ is {@{a conservative vector field}@}.\), as desired.<sup>[\[3\]](#^ref-3)</sup> <!--SR:!2025-09-19,60,310!2025-09-22,63,310!2025-09-18,59,310!2025-09-20,61,310!2025-09-26,67,310!2025-09-20,61,310!2025-09-20,61,310!2025-09-22,63,310!2026-05-29,254,330!2025-09-18,59,310!2025-09-26,67,310!2025-09-18,59,310!2025-09-21,62,310!2025-09-21,62,310!2025-09-20,61,310!2025-09-22,63,310!2026-05-28,253,330!2025-09-25,66,310!2025-09-19,60,310!2025-09-24,65,310!2026-05-30,255,330!2025-09-25,66,310!2025-09-23,64,310-->

### example of the converse principle

- Main article: ::@:: [Electric potential energy](electric%20potential%20energy.md) <!--SR:!2025-09-18,59,310!2025-09-21,62,310-->

To {@{illustrate the power of this converse principle}@}, we cite an example that {@{has significant [physical](physics.md) consequences}@}. In {@{[classical electromagnetism](classical%20electromagnetism.md)}@}, {@{the [electric force](electric%20force.md)}@} is {@{a path-independent force}@}; i.e. {@{the [work](work%20(physics).md) done on a particle}@} that {@{has returned to its original position within an [electric field](electric%20field.md) is zero}@} \(assuming that {@{no changing [magnetic fields](magnetic%20field.md) are present}@}\). <!--SR:!2026-05-30,255,330!2025-09-19,60,310!2025-09-18,59,310!2026-05-21,247,330!2025-09-22,63,310!2025-09-24,65,310!2026-05-25,251,330!2025-09-24,65,310-->

Therefore, the above theorem implies that {@{the electric [force field](force%20field%20(physics).md) __F__<sub>_e_</sub> : _S_ → __R__<sup>3</sup> is conservative}@} \(here _S_ is {@{some [open](open%20set.md), [path-connected](connected%20space.md#path%20connectedness) subset of __R__<sup>3</sup>}@} that {@{contains a [charge](electric%20charge.md) distribution}@}\). Following {@{the ideas of the above proof}@}, we can {@{set some reference point __a__ in _S_}@}, and define {@{a function _U<sub>e</sub>_: _S_ → __R__}@} by {@{$$U_{e}(\mathbf {r} ):=-\int _{\gamma [\mathbf {a} ,\mathbf {r} ]}\mathbf {F} _{e}(\mathbf {u} )\cdot \mathrm {d} \mathbf {u}$$}@} <!--SR:!2025-09-19,60,310!2025-09-19,60,310!2025-09-19,60,310!2025-09-25,66,310!2025-09-19,60,310!2025-09-18,59,310!2025-09-20,61,310-->

Using the above proof, we know _U_<sub>_e_</sub> is {@{well-defined and differentiable}@}, and {@{__F__<sub>_e_</sub> = −∇<!-- markdown separator -->_U<sub>e</sub>_}@} \(from this formula we can {@{use the gradient theorem to easily derive the well-known formula for calculating work done by conservative forces}@}: {@{_W_ = −Δ<!-- markdown separator -->_U_}@}\). This function _U_<sub>_e_</sub> is often referred to as {@{the [electrostatic potential energy](electric%20potential%20energy.md) of the system of charges in _S_}@} \(with reference to {@{the zero-of-potential __a__}@}\). In many cases, {@{the domain _S_}@} is assumed {@{to be [unbounded](bounded%20set.md)}@} and the reference point __a__ is {@{taken to be "infinity"}@}, which can be {@{made [rigorous](rigour.md#mathematical%20rigour) using limiting techniques}@}. This function _U_<sub>_e_</sub> is {@{an indispensable tool used in the analysis of many physical systems}@}. <!--SR:!2025-09-22,63,310!2025-09-23,64,310!2025-09-20,61,310!2025-09-21,62,310!2025-09-20,61,310!2025-09-23,64,310!2026-05-21,247,330!2025-09-24,65,310!2025-09-21,62,310!2025-09-25,66,310!2025-09-22,63,310-->

## generalizations

- Main articles: ::@:: [Stokes' theorem](Stokes'%20theorem.md) and [Closed and exact differential forms](closed%20and%20exact%20differential%20forms.md) <!--SR:!2025-09-26,67,310!2025-09-22,63,310-->

{@{Many of the critical theorems of vector calculus}@} {@{generalize elegantly to statements about the [integration of differential forms](differential%20form.md#integration) on [manifolds](differentiable%20manifold.md)}@}. In the language of {@{[differential forms](differential%20form.md) and [exterior derivatives](exterior%20derivative.md)}@}, the gradient theorem states that {@{$$\int _{\partial \gamma }\phi =\int _{\gamma }\mathrm {d} \phi$$}@} for {@{any [0-form](differential%20form.md), _ϕ_, defined on some differentiable curve _γ_ ⊂ __R__<sup>_n_</sup>}@} \(here {@{the integral of _ϕ_ over the boundary of the _γ_}@} is understood to be {@{the evaluation of _ϕ_ at the endpoints of _γ_}@}\). <!--SR:!2025-09-22,63,310!2025-09-19,60,310!2025-09-23,64,310!2025-09-20,61,310!2025-09-23,64,310!2025-09-18,59,310!2026-05-26,251,330-->

Notice {@{the striking similarity between this statement and the [generalized Stokes’ theorem](generalized%20Stokes%20theorem.md)}@}, which says that {@{the integral of any [compactly supported](compact%20support.md#compact%20support) differential form _ω_}@} over {@{the [boundary](boundary%20(topology).md) of some [orientable](orientation%20(vector%20space).md) manifold Ω}@} is equal to {@{the integral of its [exterior derivative](exterior%20derivative.md) d<!-- markdown separator -->_ω_ over the whole of Ω}@}, i.e., {@{$$\int _{\partial \Omega }\omega =\int _{\Omega }\mathrm {d} \omega$$}@} This powerful statement is {@{a generalization of the gradient theorem from 1-forms defined on one-dimensional manifolds}@} to {@{differential forms defined on manifolds of arbitrary dimension}@}. <!--SR:!2025-09-24,65,310!2025-09-24,65,310!2026-05-20,246,330!2025-09-26,67,310!2026-05-26,251,330!2025-09-22,63,310!2025-09-25,66,310-->

{@{The converse statement of the gradient theorem}@} also has {@{a powerful generalization in terms of differential forms on manifolds}@}. In particular, suppose {@{_ω_ is a form defined on a [contractible domain](contractible%20space.md)}@}, and {@{the integral of _ω_ over any closed manifold is zero}@}. \(annotation: This statement corresponds to that {@{any closed loop integrates to zero in the converse of the gradient theorem}@}; this statement implies {@{$\mathrm d\omega = 0$ by the generalized Stokes theorem}@}.\) Then there {@{exists a form _ψ_ such that _ω_ = d<!-- markdown separator -->_ψ_}@}. Thus, on {@{a contractible domain}@}, {@{every [closed](closed%20and%20exact%20differential%20forms.md) form is [exact](closed%20and%20exact%20differential%20forms.md)}@}. This result is summarized by {@{the [Poincaré lemma](closed%20and%20exact%20differential%20forms.md#Poincar%C3%A9%20lemma)}@}. <!--SR:!2025-09-26,67,310!2025-09-23,64,310!2025-09-26,67,310!2025-09-25,66,310!2025-09-26,67,310!2025-09-26,67,310!2025-09-21,62,310!2025-09-24,65,310!2026-05-23,249,330!2025-09-23,64,310-->
