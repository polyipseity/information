# σ-algebra

- For {@{an algebraic structure admitting a given signature Σ of operations}@}, see {@{[Universal algebra](universal%20algebra.md)}@}. <!--SR:!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,277-->

In {@{[mathematical analysis](mathematical%20analysis.md) and in [probability theory](probability%20theory.md)}@}, {@{a __σ-algebra__ \("sigma algebra"\)}@} is {@{part of the formalism for defining [sets that can be measured](measurable%20set.md)}@}. In {@{[calculus](calculus.md) and [analysis](mathematical%20analysis.md)}@}, for example, σ-algebras are used to {@{define the concept of sets with [area](area.md) or [volume](volume.md)}@}. In {@{probability theory}@}, they are used to {@{define events for which a probability can be defined}@}. In this way, σ-algebras help to {@{formalize the notion of _size_}@}. <!--SR:!2026-04-12,4,277!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,300!2026-04-12,4,277!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310-->

In {@{formal terms}@}, {@{a σ-algebra}@} \(also {@{__σ-field__, where the σ comes from the [German](German%20language.md) "Summe",<sup>[\[1\]](#^ref-1)</sup> meaning "sum"}@}\) on {@{a [set](set%20(mathematics).md) $X$ is a nonempty collection $\Sigma$ of [subsets](subsets.md) of  $X$}@} {@{[closed](closure%20(mathematics).md) under [complement](complement%20(set%20theory).md), countable [unions](union%20(set%20theory).md), and countable [intersections](intersection%20(set%20theory).md)}@}. {@{The ordered pair $(X,\Sigma )$}@} is called {@{a [measurable space](measurable%20space.md)}@}. <!--SR:!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,300!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310-->

{@{The set $X$}@} is understood to be {@{an [ambient space](ambient%20space.md)}@} \(such as {@{the 2D plane or the set of outcomes}@} when rolling {@{a six-sided die {1,2,3,4,5,6}<!-- flashcard separator -->}@}\), and {@{the collection $\Sigma$}@} is {@{a choice of subsets declared to have a well-defined size}@}. {@{The closure requirements}@} for σ-algebras are designed to {@{capture our intuitive ideas about how sizes combine}@}: if there is {@{a well-defined probability that an event occurs}@}, there should be {@{a well-defined probability that it does not occur}@} \({@{closure under complements}@}\); if {@{several sets have a well-defined size}@}, so should their {@{combination \(countable unions\)}@}; if {@{several events have a well-defined probability of occurring}@}, so should {@{the event where they all occur simultaneously \(countable intersections\)}@}. <!--SR:!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,300!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,277!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,300!2026-04-12,4,277!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,300!2026-04-12,4,310-->

{@{The definition of σ-algebra}@} resembles {@{other mathematical structures}@} such as {@{a [topology](topological%20space.md)}@} \(which is required to be {@{closed under all unions but only finite intersections}@}, and which doesn't {@{necessarily contain all complements of its sets}@}\) or {@{a [set algebra](set%20algebra.md)}@} \(which is {@{closed only under _finite_ unions and intersections}@}\). <!--SR:!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,277!2026-04-12,4,277!2026-04-12,4,277!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,300-->

## examples of σ-algebras

If {@{$X=\{a,b,c,d\}$ one possible σ-algebra on $X$}@} is {@{$\Sigma =\{\varnothing ,\{a,b\},\{c,d\},\{a,b,c,d\}\}$}@}, where {@{$\varnothing$ is the [empty set](empty%20set.md)}@}. In {@{general}@}, {@{a finite algebra}@} is {@{always a σ-algebra}@}. <!--SR:!2026-04-12,4,277!2026-04-12,4,297!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310-->

If {@{$\{A_{1},A_{2},A_{3},\ldots \}$, is a countable [partition](partition%20of%20a%20set.md) of $X$}@} then {@{the collection of all unions of sets in the partition \(including the empty set\)}@} is {@{a σ-algebra}@}. <!--SR:!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,300-->

{@{A more useful example}@} is {@{the set of subsets of the [real line](real%20line.md)}@} formed by starting {@{with all [open intervals](open%20interval.md#open%20interval)}@} and adding in {@{all countable unions, countable intersections, and relative complements}@} and continuing {@{this process \(by [transfinite iteration](transfinite%20iteration.md#transfinite%20recursion) through all [countable ordinals](countable%20ordinal.md)\)}@} until {@{the relevant closure properties are achieved}@} \(a construction known as {@{the [Borel hierarchy](Borel%20hierarchy.md)}@}\). <!--SR:!2026-04-12,4,300!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,277!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,300-->

## motivation

There are at least {@{three key motivators for σ-algebras}@}: defining {@{measures, manipulating limits of sets}@}, and managing {@{partial information characterized by sets}@}. <!--SR:!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310-->

### measure

{@{A [measure](measure%20(mathematics).md) on $X$}@} is {@{a [function](function%20(mathematics).md) that assigns a non-negative [real number](real%20number.md) to subsets of $X;$}@} this can be thought of as making {@{precise a notion of "size" or "volume" for sets}@}. We want {@{the size of the union of disjoint sets}@} to be {@{the sum of their individual sizes}@}, even for {@{an infinite sequence of [disjoint sets](disjoint%20sets.md)}@}. <!--SR:!2026-04-12,4,300!2026-04-12,4,300!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,300!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310-->

One would like to assign {@{a size to _every_ subset of $X$}@}, but in {@{many natural settings, this is not possible}@}. For example, {@{the [axiom of choice](axiom%20of%20choice.md)}@} implies that when {@{the size under consideration}@} is {@{the ordinary notion of length for subsets of the real line}@}, then there exist {@{sets for which no size exists}@}, for example, {@{the [Vitali sets](Vitali%20set.md)}@}. For this reason, one considers instead {@{a smaller collection of privileged subsets of $X$}@}. These subsets will be called {@{the measurable sets}@}. They are {@{closed under operations that one would expect for measurable sets}@}, that is, the complement {@{of a measurable set is a measurable set}@} and the countable union {@{of measurable sets is a measurable set}@}. {@{Non-empty collections of sets with these properties}@} are called {@{σ-algebras}@}. <!--SR:!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,277!2026-04-12,4,300!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,277!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,277-->

### limits of sets

{@{Many uses of measure}@}, such as the probability concept of {@{[almost sure convergence](convergence%20of%20random%20variables.md)}@}, involve {@{[limits of sequences of sets](set-theoretic%20limit.md)}@}. For this, {@{closure under countable unions and intersections}@} is {@{paramount}@}. {@{Set limits}@} are defined as {@{follows on σ-algebras}@}. (annotation: 2 items: {@{limit supremum, limit infimum}@}) <!--SR:!2026-04-12,4,300!2026-04-12,4,299!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,300!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310-->

- {@{The _[limit supremum](limit%20supremum.md#limit%20superior)_ or _outer limit_}@} of {@{a sequence $A_{1},A_{2},A_{3},\ldots$ of subsets of $X$}@} is {@{$$\limsup _{n\to \infty }A_{n}=\bigcap _{n=1}^{\infty }\bigcup _{m=n}^{\infty }A_{m}=\bigcap _{n=1}^{\infty }A_{n}\cup A_{n+1}\cup \cdots .$$}@} (annotation: Intuitively, it can be interpreted as {@{the "least upper bound"}@}, where {@{"least" corresponds to the intersection}@} and {@{"upper bound" corresponds to the union}@}.) It consists of {@{all points $x$ that are in infinitely many of these sets}@} \(or equivalently, that are in {@{[_cofinally_ many](cofinal%20(mathematics).md) of them}@}\). That is, {@{$x\in \limsup _{n\to \infty }A_{n}$}@} {@{if and only if there exists an infinite [subsequence](subsequence.md) $A_{n_{1} },A_{n_{2} },\ldots$}@} {@{\(where $n_{1}<n_{2}<\cdots$\) of sets that all contain $x;$}@} that is, such that {@{$x\in A_{n_{1} }\cap A_{n_{2} }\cap \cdots$}@}.
- {@{The _[limit infimum](limit%20infimum.md#limit%20inferior)_ or _inner limit_}@} of {@{a sequence $A_{1},A_{2},A_{3},\ldots$ of subsets of $X$}@} is {@{$$\liminf _{n\to \infty }A_{n}=\bigcup _{n=1}^{\infty }\bigcap _{m=n}^{\infty }A_{m}=\bigcup _{n=1}^{\infty }A_{n}\cap A_{n+1}\cap \cdots .$$}@} (annotation: Intuitively, it can be interpreted as {@{the "greatest lower bound"}@}, where {@{"greatest" corresponds to the union}@} and {@{"lower bound" corresponds to the intersection}@}.) It consists of {@{all points that are in all but finitely many of these sets}@} \(or equivalently, that are {@{_eventually_ in all of them}@}\). That is, {@{$x\in \liminf _{n\to \infty }A_{n}$}@} {@{if and only if there exists an index $N\in \mathbb {N}$ such that $A_{N},A_{N+1},\ldots$ all contain $x;$}@} that is, such that {@{$x\in A_{N}\cap A_{N+1}\cap \cdots$}@}. <!--SR:!2026-04-12,4,299!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,297!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,277!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,277!2026-04-12,4,300!2026-04-12,4,277!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,277!2026-04-12,4,300!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,277!2026-04-12,4,300!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,300!2026-04-12,4,310-->

{@{The inner limit}@} is {@{always a subset of the outer limit}@}: {@{$$\liminf _{n\to \infty }A_{n}~\subseteq ~\limsup _{n\to \infty }A_{n}.$$}@} If these {@{two sets are equal}@} then their {@{limit $\lim _{n\to \infty }A_{n}$ exists and is equal to this common set}@}: {@{$$\lim _{n\to \infty }A_{n}:=\liminf _{n\to \infty }A_{n}=\limsup _{n\to \infty }A_{n}.$$}@} <!--SR:!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,277!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310-->

### sub σ-algebras

In {@{much of probability}@}, especially when {@{[conditional expectation](conditional%20expectation.md)}@} is involved, one is concerned with sets that {@{represent only part of all the possible information that can be observed}@}. {@{This partial information}@} can be characterized with {@{a smaller σ-algebra which is a subset of the principal σ-algebra}@}; it consists of {@{the collection of subsets relevant only to and determined only by the partial information}@}. Formally, if {@{$\Sigma ,\Sigma '$ are σ-algebras on $X$}@}, then {@{$\Sigma '$ is a sub σ-algebra of $\Sigma$}@} if {@{$\Sigma '\subseteq \Sigma$}@}. <!--SR:!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,277!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,300!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310-->

{@{The [Bernoulli process](Bernoulli%20process.md)}@} provides {@{a simple example}@}. This consists of {@{a sequence of random coin flips, coming up Heads \($H$\) or Tails \($T$\), of unbounded length}@}. {@{The [sample space](sample%20space.md) Ω}@} consists of {@{all possible infinite sequences of $H$ or $T:$}@} {@{$$\Omega =\{H,T\}^{\infty }=\{(x_{1},x_{2},x_{3},\dots ):x_{i}\in \{H,T\},i\geq 1\}.$$}@} <!--SR:!2026-04-12,4,297!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,270!2026-04-12,4,310-->

{@{The full sigma algebra}@} can be generated from {@{an ascending sequence of subalgebras}@}, by considering {@{the information that might be obtained}@} after observing {@{some or all of the first $n$ coin flips}@}. {@{This sequence of subalgebras}@} is given by {@{$${\mathcal {G} }_{n}=\{A\times \Omega :A\subseteq \{H,T\}^{n}\}$$}@} (annotation: $\mathcal{G}_n$ is {@{the sigma-algebra}@} made of {@{all sets of the form $A \times \Omega$, where $A \subseteq \{H,T\}^n$}@}, meaning that you pick {@{exactly which length-_n_ prefixes are allowed}@} and then attach {@{_all possible infinite continuations_}@}, so {@{each event in $\mathcal{G}_n$}@} is completely {@{determined by the first _n_ coordinates and ignores the rest of the product space}@}.) {@{Each of these}@} is {@{finer than the last}@}, and so can be {@{ordered as a [filtration](filtration%20(probability%20theory).md)}@} {@{$${\mathcal {G} }_{0}\subseteq {\mathcal {G} }_{1}\subseteq {\mathcal {G} }_{2}\subseteq \cdots \subseteq {\mathcal {G} }_{\infty }$$}@} {@{The first subalgebra ${\mathcal {G} }_{0}=\{\varnothing ,\Omega \}$}@} is {@{the trivial algebra}@}: it has {@{only two elements in it, the empty set and the total space}@}. {@{The second subalgebra ${\mathcal {G} }_{1}$}@} has {@{four elements}@}: the two in {@{${\mathcal {G} }_{0}$ plus two more}@}: sequences that {@{start with $H$ and sequences that start with $T$}@}. {@{Each subalgebra}@} is {@{finer than the last}@}. {@{The $n$'th subalgebra}@} contains {@{$2^{n+1}$ elements}@}: it divides {@{the total space $\Omega$ into all of the possible sequences}@} that might {@{have been observed after $n$ flips}@}, including {@{the possible non-observation of some of the flips}@}. (annotation: Related to extending {@{a σ-algebra from a subspace}@}, as mentioned below.) <!--SR:!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,300!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,300!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,277!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,300!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,277!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,300!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,300-->

{@{The limiting algebra ${\mathcal {G} }_{\infty }$}@} is {@{the smallest σ-algebra containing all the others}@}. It is {@{the algebra generated}@} by {@{the [product topology](product%20topology.md) or [weak topology](weak%20topology.md) on the product space $\{H,T\}^{\infty }$}@}. (annotation: {@{The product topology}@} on $\Omega=\{H,T\}^\infty$ is defined as {@{the _coarsest_ topology making each coordinate projection $\pi_k$ continuous}@}: since {@{$\{H,T\}$ is discrete}@}, {@{every singleton is open}@}, so {@{the preimages $\pi_k^{-1}(\{a\})=\{\omega:\omega_k=a\}$ must be open}@}; {@{finite intersections of such preimages}@} form {@{the familiar finite-coordinate _cylinders_}@}, and {@{closing under unions}@} yields {@{all opens, with no additional sets added precisely}@} because the topology is {@{required to be the smallest one satisfying continuity of all $\pi_k$}@}. Similarly, {@{the weak topology}@} is {@{the _coarsest_ topology making every finite-block projection $\pi_{1..n}$ continuous}@}: any {@{$A\subseteq\{H,T\}^n$ is open in the discrete codomain}@}, so {@{$\pi_{1..n}^{-1}(A)$ must be open}@}, and these preimages are {@{again exactly finite-coordinate cylinders}@}; taking {@{the smallest topology containing them}@} gives {@{the weak topology}@}. <p> It is always {@{valid to take the Borel σ-algebra $\sigma(\mathcal{T})$ of any topology}@}, and doing so simply means using {@{the open sets as the _generators_}@} and then applying {@{the _countable σ-operations_ (complements and countable unions/intersections) needed}@} to produce {@{all measurable sets built from those opens}@}.) <!--SR:!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,277!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,300!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,277!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,297!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,277!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,300!2026-04-12,4,300-->

## definition and properties

### definition

Let {@{$X$ be some set (annotation: which can be empty)}@}, and let $P(X)$ represent {@{its [power set](power%20set.md), the set of all subsets of $X$}@}. Then {@{a subset $\Sigma \subseteq P(X)$ is called a __σ-algebra__}@} if it {@{satisfies the following three properties}@}:<sup>[\[2\]](#^ref-2)</sup> (annotation: 3 items: {@{contains the whole set}@}, {@{closed under complements}@}, {@{closed under countable unions}@}) <!--SR:!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,300!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310-->

1. \(annotation: contains the whole set\) ::@:: $X$ is in $\Sigma$. <!--SR:!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,300-->
2. $\Sigma$ is _closed under complementation_: ::@:: If some set $A$ is in $\Sigma$, then so is its [complement](complement%20(set%20theory).md), $X\setminus A$. <!--SR:!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310-->
3. $\Sigma$ is _closed under countable unions_: ::@:: If $A_{1},A_{2},A_{3},\ldots$ are in $\Sigma$, then so is $A=A_{1}\cup A_{2}\cup A_{3}\cup \cdots$. <!--SR:!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310-->

From these {@{properties (annotation: contains the whole set, closed under complements, closed under countable unions)}@}, it follows that the σ-algebra is also {@{closed under countable [intersections](intersection%20(set%20theory).md) \(by applying [De Morgan's laws](De%20Morgan's%20laws.md)\)}@}. <!--SR:!2026-04-12,4,277!2026-04-12,4,310-->

It also follows that {@{the [empty set](empty%20set.md) $\varnothing$ is in $\Sigma$}@}, since by {@{__\(1\)__ $X$ is in $\Sigma$}@} and {@{__\(2\)__ asserts that its complement, the empty set, is also in $\Sigma$}@}. Moreover, since {@{$\{X,\varnothing \}$ satisfies all 3 conditions}@}, it follows that {@{$\{X,\varnothing \}$}@} is {@{the smallest possible σ-algebra on $X$}@}. {@{The largest possible σ-algebra on $X$}@} is {@{$P(X)$}@}. (annotation: A measure can {@{always be defined on any σ-algebra}@}, but {@{it may _not_ be possible to define a "nice" measure on it}@}. For example, {@{the power set of an uncountable set}@} admits {@{only trivial measures that assign either 0 or $\infty$ to all nonempty sets}@}.) <!--SR:!2026-04-12,4,277!2026-04-12,4,277!2026-04-12,4,277!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,277!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,277-->

{@{Elements of the σ-algebra}@} are called {@{[measurable sets](measurable%20set.md)}@}. (annotation: {@{The term "_measurable set_"}@} is {@{a bit of a misnomer for the reasons below}@}.) {@{An ordered pair $(X,\Sigma )$}@}, where {@{$X$ is a set and $\Sigma$ is a σ-algebra over $X$}@}, is called {@{a __measurable space__}@}. (annotation: Note {@{the term "_measurable space_" is a bit of a misnomer}@} because it {@{only requires a σ-algebra}@}. A measure can {@{always be defined on any σ-algebra}@}, but {@{it may _not_ be possible to define a "nice" measure on it}@}. For example, {@{the power set of an uncountable set}@} admits {@{only trivial measures that assign either 0 or $\infty$ to all nonempty sets}@}.) {@{A function between two measurable spaces}@} is called {@{a [measurable function](measurable%20function.md)}@} if {@{the [preimage](preimage.md#inverse%20image) of every measurable set is measurable}@}. {@{The collection of measurable spaces}@} forms {@{a [category](category%20(mathematics).md), with the [measurable functions](measurable%20function.md) as [morphisms](morphism.md)}@}. {@{[Measures](measure%20(mathematics).md)}@} are defined as {@{certain types of functions from a σ-algebra to $[0,\infty ]$}@}. <!--SR:!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,300!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,300!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,300!2026-04-12,4,270!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,300!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,300!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,300-->

{@{A σ-algebra}@} is {@{both a [π-system](pi-system.md) and a [Dynkin system](Dynkin%20system.md) \(λ-system\)}@}. {@{The converse is true}@} as well, by {@{Dynkin's theorem}@} \(see below\). (annotation: If {@{an algebra is both a π-system and a Dynkin system}@}, then {@{the π-system is trivially contained in the Dynkin system}@}, so {@{Dynkin's π-λ theorem}@} implies that {@{the σ-algebra generated by the π-system is contained in the Dynkin system}@}. But we already know {@{the π-system is the Dynkin system}@}, so {@{the σ-algebra generated by the π-system is the Dynkin system}@}, and thus {@{the algebra is a σ-algebra}@}.) <!--SR:!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,300!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,277!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,300!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310-->

- A [π-system](pi-system.md) $P$ ::@:: is a collection of subsets of $X$ that is closed under finitely many intersections, and <!--SR:!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310-->
- A [Dynkin system](Dynkin%20system.md) \(or λ-system\) $D$ ::@:: is a collection of subsets of $X$ that contains $X$ and is closed under complement and under countable unions of _disjoint_ subsets. <!--SR:!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310-->

{@{Dynkin's π-λ theorem}@} says, if {@{$P$ is a π-system and $D$ is a Dynkin system that contains $P$}@}, then {@{the σ-algebra $\sigma (P)$ [generated](sigma-algebra.md#%CF%83-algebra%20generated%20by%20an%20arbitrary%20family) by $P$ is contained in $D$}@}. Since certain {@{π-systems are relatively simple classes}@}, it may {@{not be hard to verify that all sets in $P$ enjoy the property under consideration}@} while, on the other hand, showing that {@{the collection $D$ of all subsets with the property is a Dynkin system}@} can also be {@{straightforward}@}. {@{Dynkin's π-λ Theorem}@} then implies that {@{all sets in $\sigma (P)$ enjoy the property}@}, avoiding the task of {@{checking it for an arbitrary set in $\sigma (P)$}@}. (annotation: Let {@{$\Omega=\mathbb{N}$}@}. Fix two {@{probability measures $\mu,\nu$ on $(\mathbb{N},\mathcal{P}(\mathbb{N}))$}@}. Let {@{$P=\{[n,\infty): n\in\mathbb{N}\}$}@}, which is {@{a π-system since $[m,\infty)\cap[n,\infty)=[\max\{m,n\},\infty)$}@}. Define {@{$D=\{A\subseteq\mathbb{N}: \mu(A)=\nu(A)\}$}@}. From {@{this equality of measures}@} one recovers {@{$\mu(\{n\})=\nu(\{n\})$ in the discrete case on $\mathbb{N}$}@} (or equally on {@{$\mathbb{Z}$ with minor notational changes}@}). In {@{the absolutely continuous case}@}, {@{the sample space and generating π-system}@} are replaced by {@{$\mathbb{R}$ and the half-lines $(-\infty,x]$}@}, and {@{the same argument}@} yields {@{equality of PDFs almost everywhere}@}.) <!--SR:!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,300!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,277!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,300!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,300!2026-04-12,4,277!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,300!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,300!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,297!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,277!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310-->

{@{One of the most fundamental uses}@} of the π-λ theorem is to show {@{equivalence of separately defined measures or integrals}@}. For example, it is used to equate {@{a probability for a random variable $X$ with the [Lebesgue-Stieltjes integral](Lebesgue-Stieltjes%20integral.md)}@} typically associated with {@{computing the probability}@}: {@{$$\mathbb {P} (X\in A)=\int _{A}\,F(dx)$$}@} for {@{all $A$ in the Borel σ-algebra on $\mathbb {R}$}@}, where $F(x)$ is {@{the [cumulative distribution function](cumulative%20distribution%20function.md) for $X$, defined on $\mathbb {R}$}@}, while $\mathbb {P}$ is {@{a [probability measure](probability%20measure.md), defined on a σ-algebra $\Sigma$ of subsets of some [sample space](sample%20space.md) $\Omega$}@}. <!--SR:!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310-->

### combining σ-algebras

Suppose {@{$\textstyle \left\{\Sigma _{\alpha }:\alpha \in {\mathcal {A} }\right\}$}@} is {@{a collection of σ-algebras on a space $X$}@}. <!--SR:!2026-04-12,4,300!2026-04-12,4,310-->

__Meet__

{@{The intersection of a collection of σ-algebras}@} is {@{a σ-algebra}@}. To emphasize {@{its character as a σ-algebra}@}, it often is denoted by: {@{$$\bigwedge _{\alpha \in {\mathcal {A} } }\Sigma _{\alpha }.$$}@} <!--SR:!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,300!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310-->

__Sketch of Proof:__ Let $\Sigma ^{*}$ denote {@{the intersection}@}. Since {@{$X$ is in every $\Sigma _{\alpha }$}@}, {@{$\Sigma ^{*}$ is not empty}@}. {@{Closure under complement and countable unions for every $\Sigma _{\alpha }$}@} implies {@{the same must be true for $\Sigma ^{*}$}@}. (annotation: Simply consider {@{an arbitrary set $A$ in $\Sigma^*$}@}.) Therefore, {@{$\Sigma ^{*}$ is a σ-algebra}@}. <!--SR:!2026-04-12,4,300!2026-04-12,4,277!2026-04-12,4,300!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,300!2026-04-12,4,310-->

__Join__

{@{The union of a collection of σ-algebras}@} is {@{not generally a σ-algebra, or even an algebra}@}, but it {@{[generates](sigma-algebra.md#%CF%83-algebra%20generated%20by%20an%20arbitrary%20family) a σ-algebra known as the join}@} which typically is denoted {@{$$\bigvee _{\alpha \in {\mathcal {A} } }\Sigma _{\alpha }=\sigma \left(\bigcup _{\alpha \in {\mathcal {A} } }\Sigma _{\alpha }\right).$$}@} {@{A π-system that generates the join}@} is {@{$${\mathcal {P} }=\left\{\bigcap _{i=1}^{n}A_{i}:A_{i}\in \Sigma _{\alpha _{i} },\alpha _{i}\in {\mathcal {A} },\ n\geq 1\right\}.$$}@} (annotation: In words, this is {@{the collection of all finite intersections of sets}@}, where each set is {@{taken from some σ-algebra in the family}@}.) <!--SR:!2026-04-12,4,300!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,277!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,300-->

__Sketch of Proof:__ (annotation: We want to show {@{$\sigma({\mathcal {P} })=\sigma \left(\bigcup _{\alpha \in {\mathcal {A} } }\Sigma _{\alpha }\right)$}@}.) By {@{the case $n=1$}@}, it is seen that {@{each $\Sigma _{\alpha }\subset {\mathcal {P} }$}@}, so {@{$$\bigcup _{\alpha \in {\mathcal {A} } }\Sigma _{\alpha }\subseteq {\mathcal {P} }.$$}@} This implies {@{$$\sigma \left(\bigcup _{\alpha \in {\mathcal {A} } }\Sigma _{\alpha }\right)\subseteq \sigma ({\mathcal {P} })$$}@} by {@{the definition of a σ-algebra [generated](sigma-algebra.md#%CF%83-algebra%20generated%20by%20an%20arbitrary%20family) by a collection of subsets}@}. (annotation: Intuitively, {@{$\sigma$ is monotonic}@}, so {@{the σ-algebra generated by a larger collection of sets is larger}@}.) On the other hand, {@{$${\mathcal {P} }\subseteq \sigma \left(\bigcup _{\alpha \in {\mathcal {A} } }\Sigma _{\alpha }\right)$$}@} (annotation: Consider that {@{a set in $\mathcal{P}$ is a finite intersection of sets}@}, each of which is {@{in some $\Sigma_\alpha$ and thus in the union}@}, so their {@{intersection is in the σ-algebra generated by the union}@}.) which, by {@{Dynkin's π-λ theorem}@}, implies {@{$$\sigma ({\mathcal {P} })\subseteq \sigma \left(\bigcup _{\alpha \in {\mathcal {A} } }\Sigma _{\alpha }\right).$$}@} (annotation: Recall that a σ-algebra is {@{both a π-system and a Dynkin system}@}.) <!--SR:!2026-04-12,4,299!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,300!2026-04-12,4,297!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,300!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310-->

### σ-algebras for subspaces

Suppose $Y$ is {@{a subset of $X$}@} and let {@{$(X,\Sigma )$ be a measurable space}@}. (annotation: 2 items: {@{restriction, extension}@}) <!--SR:!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,277-->

- {@{The collection $\{Y\cap B:B\in \Sigma \}$}@} is {@{a σ-algebra of subsets of $Y$ (annotation: so $Y$ may _not_ be in $\Sigma$ and thus the resulting σ-algebra)}@}. (annotation: Intuitively, this is {@{the σ-algebra obtained by restricting $\Sigma$ to $Y$}@}. In {@{probability theory}@}, this may be used to justify treating {@{$Y$ as a measurable space in its own right, even if it is not a measurable subset of $X$}@}, e.g. defining {@{conditional probabilities on given that event $Y$ has occurred}@}.)
- Suppose {@{$(Y,\Lambda )$ is a measurable space}@}. {@{The collection $\{A\subseteq X:A\cap Y\in \Lambda \}$}@} is {@{a σ-algebra of subsets of $X$ (annotation: unlike restriction, $X$ must be in the resulting σ-algebra)}@}. (annotation: Intuitively, this is {@{the σ-algebra obtained by extending $\Lambda$ to $X$}@}. In {@{probability theory}@}, this may be used to justify constructing {@{a probability space for an infinite collection of random variables}@} by first defining {@{a probability space for a finite collection of them}@} and then {@{extending it to the infinite collection}@}. See {@{the Bernoulli process example}@} above.) <!--SR:!2026-04-12,4,300!2026-04-12,4,300!2026-04-12,4,299!2026-04-12,4,300!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,300!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,300!2026-04-12,4,277!2026-04-12,4,300!2026-04-12,4,310-->

### restriction–extension adjunction for σ-algebras

\(annotation: applies to this section\)

When {@{a subset $Y \subseteq X$ is fixed}@}, {@{the trace operation $R$ and the extension operation $E$}@} together form {@{a natural pair of constructions that relate σ-algebras on $X$ to σ-algebras on $Y$}@}. {@{The trace of a σ-algebra $\Sigma$ on $X$}@} is obtained by {@{intersecting each of its sets with $Y$}@}, producing {@{$R(\Sigma)=\{Y\cap B : B\in\Sigma\}$, which is a σ-algebra on $Y$}@}. This process simply {@{"looks at" each measurable set inside the smaller space}@}, forgetting {@{any structure on $X\setminus Y$}@}. Conversely, given {@{a σ-algebra $\Lambda$ on $Y$}@}, {@{the extension $E(\Lambda)=\{A\subseteq X : A\cap Y\in\Lambda\}$}@} produces {@{a σ-algebra on $X$ that agrees with $\Lambda$ on $Y$ and imposes no constraints elsewhere}@}; every {@{subset of $X\setminus Y$ is allowed}@}. <!--SR:!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,300!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,300!2026-04-12,4,300!2026-04-12,4,277!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,300!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,277!2026-04-12,4,310-->

{@{These operations (annotation: trace and extension)}@} satisfy {@{a key equivalence that characterizes a Galois connection}@}: {@{$$R(\Sigma)\subseteq \Lambda \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad \Sigma\subseteq E(\Lambda) \,.$$}@} Here, $R$ is {@{the lower or left adjoint, and $E$ is the upper or right adjoint}@}. {@{This equivalence}@} follows {@{directly from the definitions}@}. If {@{$R(\Sigma)\subseteq\Lambda$}@}, then for any {@{$B\in\Sigma$, its intersection $Y\cap B$ is in $\Lambda$}@}, and therefore {@{$B\in E(\Lambda)$; hence $\Sigma\subseteq E(\Lambda)$}@}. Conversely, if {@{$\Sigma\subseteq E(\Lambda)$}@}, then each {@{$B\in\Sigma$ satisfies $Y\cap B \in \Lambda$}@}, so every {@{element of the trace $R(\Sigma)$ lies in $\Lambda$}@}. {@{This simple bidirectional implication}@} is precisely the condition for $R$ to be {@{the left adjoint of $E$ in the posets of σ-algebras ordered by inclusion}@}. <!--SR:!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,300!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,300!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310-->

{@{One immediate consequence of adjointness}@} is that {@{$R(E(\Lambda))=\Lambda$}@}: when {@{a σ-algebra on $Y$ is extended to $X$ and then restricted back}@}, {@{nothing changes}@}, because {@{the extension does not introduce any new structure inside $Y$}@}. In contrast, {@{$\Sigma\subseteq E(R(\Sigma))$ always holds, but equality can fail}@}. {@{The composite $E(R(\Sigma))$}@} {@{freely adds all subsets of $X\setminus Y$}@}, meaning it is {@{the smallest σ-algebra on $X$}@} that captures {@{exactly the same information on $Y$ as $\Sigma$}@}. This makes {@{$E\circ R$ a closure operator on $\mathrm{Sig}(X)$}@}, inflating {@{a σ-algebra such that it stabilizes with respect to $Y$}@}. <!--SR:!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,277!2026-04-12,4,300!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,300!2026-04-12,4,277!2026-04-12,4,277!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310-->

From {@{a universal property viewpoint}@}, {@{restriction}@} produces {@{the coarsest σ-algebra on $Y$}@} making {@{the inclusion $Y\hookrightarrow X$ measurable with respect to a given σ-algebra on $X$}@}. {@{Extension, dually}@}, produces {@{the finest σ-algebra on $X$}@} whose {@{restriction to $Y$ is included in a prescribed σ-algebra on $Y$}@}. {@{This duality}@} mirrors {@{the usual left-adjoint/right-adjoint behavior}@}: {@{the left adjoint $R$ forgets structure}@}, while {@{the right adjoint $E$ freely adds structure without imposing additional constraints on the given part}@}. <!--SR:!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,277!2026-04-12,4,300!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,300!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,277-->

{@{A few insights}@} highlight why this {@{adjunction is conceptually natural}@}: (annotation: 3 items: {@{explicit description of extension, maximal σ-algebra from local data, generalization to arbitrary measurable maps}@}) <!--SR:!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310-->

- {@{The extension $E(\Lambda)$}@} can be described {@{explicitly as $\sigma(\Lambda\cup\mathcal{P}(X\setminus Y))$}@}; {@{the right adjoint}@} {@{"fills out" the complement of $Y$ as freely as possible}@}.
- When {@{probability laws}@} are {@{known only on "local pieces" of a system}@}—such as {@{finite collections of coordinates, boundary conditions, or restricted subsystems}@}—{@{the extension construction}@} provides {@{the _maximal σ-algebra on the full space_ that remains compatible with that local data}@}. {@{A canonical example}@} is {@{the Kolmogorov Extension Theorem}@}: {@{finite-dimensional distributions}@} of a process determine {@{only local behavior}@}, but using {@{extension one builds the full path-space σ-algebra}@} on {@{$X = S^{\mathbb{N} }$ or $C([0,\infty),\mathbb{R})$}@}, ensuring no {@{extra constraints are imposed beyond those finite laws}@}. {@{This mechanism}@} allows {@{Brownian motion, Markov processes, and Gaussian processes}@} to be {@{constructed from partial local information}@}.
- {@{The adjunction for inclusions}@} generalizes to {@{any measurable map $f:Y\to X$}@}, where {@{$f^{-1}$}@} produces {@{the _coarsest σ-algebra_ making $f$ measurable (initial σ-algebra)}@} and {@{the corresponding final σ-algebra}@} produces {@{the _finest σ-algebra_ on the target for which $f$ remains measurable}@}. {@{This structure}@} underlies the definition of {@{random variables and their distributions}@}: {@{a random variable $X:(\Omega,\mathcal{F})\to (S,\mathcal{S})$ is measurable}@} precisely because {@{$\mathcal{S}$ is chosen as the final σ-algebra that makes $X$ measurable}@}, ensuring {@{the pushforward law $X_*P(A) = P(X^{-1}(A))$ exists}@}. It exists because for every {@{$A\in\mathcal{S}$, the preimage $X^{-1}(A)$ is in $\mathcal{F}$}@}. On the other hand, {@{the final-ness of $\mathcal{S}$}@} guarantees that the pushforward law is {@{the most general possible distribution for $X$}@} consistent with {@{the measurable structure of the domain of $X$}@}. The final-ness is important because, for example, consider {@{always setting $\mathcal{S} = \set{\varnothing, S}$}@}. Then we cannot {@{define any nontrivial random variable}@}, since {@{the only measurable sets in the codomain}@} are {@{the empty set and the whole space}@}, so {@{any random variable}@} must follow {@{the degenerate distribution that assigns probability 0 to the empty set and 1 to the whole space}@}. Thus, {@{the adjunction}@} is built into {@{the foundational mechanism}@} of how {@{random variables and probability distributions are defined}@}. <!--SR:!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,277!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,277!2026-04-12,4,299!2026-04-12,4,300!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,277!2026-04-12,4,300!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,277!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,300!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,300!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,297!2026-04-12,4,270!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,277!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,277!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,300-->

{@{This pairing}@} captures {@{the precise sense in which measurable structure}@} can be either {@{localized to a subspace or freely extended to a larger space}@}, with {@{adjointness}@} guaranteeing that each operation is {@{the most economical possible companion of the other}@}. <!--SR:!2026-04-12,4,277!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310-->

### relation to σ-ring

{@{A _σ_-algebra $\Sigma$}@} is just {@{a [_σ_-ring](sigma-ring.md) that contains the universal set $X$}@}.<sup>[\[3\]](#^ref-3)</sup> {@{A _σ_-ring need not be a _σ_-algebra}@}, as for example {@{measurable subsets of zero Lebesgue measure}@} in the real line are {@{a _σ_-ring, but not a _σ_-algebra}@} since the real line has {@{infinite measure and thus cannot be obtained by their countable union}@}. If, instead of {@{zero measure}@}, one takes {@{measurable subsets of finite Lebesgue measure}@}, those are {@{a [ring](ring%20of%20sets.md) but not a _σ_-ring}@}, since the real line can {@{be obtained by their countable union yet its measure is not finite}@}. <!--SR:!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,277!2026-04-12,4,300!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,277!2026-04-12,4,310-->

## particular cases and examples

### separable σ-algebras

{@{A __separable $\sigma$-algebra__ \(or __separable $\sigma$-field__\)}@} is {@{a $\sigma$-algebra ${\mathcal {F} }$ that is a [separable space](separable%20space.md)}@} when considered as {@{a [metric space](metric%20space.md) with [metric](metric%20(mathematics).md) $\rho (A,B)=\mu (A{\mathbin {\triangle } }B)$ for $A,B\in {\mathcal {F} }$}@} and {@{a given finite [measure](measure%20(mathematics).md) $\mu$}@} \(and with $\triangle$ being {@{the [symmetric difference](symmetric%20difference.md) operator}@}\).<sup>[\[4\]](#^ref-4)</sup> {@{Any $\sigma$-algebra generated by a [countable](countable.md) collection of [sets](set%20(mathematics).md)}@} is {@{separable, but the converse need not hold}@}. For example, {@{the Lebesgue $\sigma$-algebra is separable}@} \(since every {@{Lebesgue measurable set is equivalent (annotation: up to a null set) to some Borel set}@}\) but not {@{countably generated \(since its cardinality is higher than continuum\)}@}. <!--SR:!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,300!2026-04-12,4,300!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310-->

{@{A separable measure space (annotation: a _measurable_ space with an explicit measure)}@} has {@{a natural [pseudometric](pseudometric%20space.md)}@} that renders it {@{[separable](separable%20space.md) as a [pseudometric space](pseudometric%20space.md)}@}. {@{The distance between two sets}@} is defined as {@{the measure of the [symmetric difference](symmetric%20difference.md) of the two sets}@}. {@{The symmetric difference of two distinct sets}@} can have {@{measure zero}@}; hence {@{the pseudometric as defined above need not be a true metric}@}. However, if {@{sets whose symmetric difference has measure zero}@} are {@{identified into a single [equivalence class](equivalence%20class.md)}@}, {@{the resulting [quotient set](equivalence%20class.md)}@} can be properly {@{metrized by the induced metric}@}. If {@{the measure space is separable}@}, it can be shown that {@{the corresponding metric space is, too}@}. <!--SR:!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,300!2026-04-12,4,277!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,300!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,277!2026-04-12,4,300!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,300-->

### simple set-based examples

Let {@{$X$}@} be {@{any set}@}. <!--SR:!2026-04-12,4,277!2026-04-12,4,310-->

- {@{The family consisting}@} only of {@{the empty set and the set $X$}@}, called {@{the minimal or __trivial σ-algebra__ over $X$}@}.
- {@{The [power set](power%20set.md) of $X$}@}, called {@{the __discrete σ-algebra__}@}. (annotation: Note when {@{$X$ is uncountable}@}, {@{the discrete σ-algebra cannot be assigned a measure that has "nice" properties}@}.)
- {@{The collection $\{\varnothing ,A,X\setminus A,X\}$}@} is {@{a simple σ-algebra generated by the subset $A$}@}.
- {@{The collection of subsets of $X$ which are countable or whose complements are countable}@} is {@{a σ-algebra}@} \(which is distinct from {@{the power set of $X$ if and only if $X$ is uncountable}@}\). This is {@{the σ-algebra generated by the [singletons](singleton%20(mathematics).md) of $X$}@}. Note: "countable" {@{includes finite or empty}@}.
- {@{The collection of all unions of sets in a countable [partition](partition%20of%20a%20set.md)}@} of $X$ is {@{a σ-algebra}@}. <!--SR:!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,300!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,297!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,277!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,297!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,277-->

### stopping time sigma-algebras

{@{A [stopping time](stopping%20time.md) $\tau$}@} can define {@{a $\sigma$-algebra ${\mathcal {F} }_{\tau }$, the so-called [stopping time sigma-algebra](filtration%20(mathematics).md#relation%20to%20stopping%20times%20stopping%20time%20sigma-algebras)}@}, which in {@{a [filtered probability space](filtration%20(mathematics).md#measure%20theory)}@} describes {@{the information up to the random time $\tau$}@} in the sense that, if {@{the filtered probability space}@} is {@{interpreted as a random experiment}@}, {@{the maximum information}@} that can be {@{found out about the experiment from arbitrarily often repeating it until the time $\tau$}@} is {@{${\mathcal {F} }_{\tau }$}@}.<sup>[\[5\]](#^ref-5)</sup> <!--SR:!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,297!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,300-->

## σ-algebras generated by families of sets

### σ-algebra generated by an arbitrary family

Let $F$ be {@{an arbitrary family of subsets of $X$}@}. Then there exists {@{a unique smallest σ-algebra which contains every set in $F$}@} \(even though {@{$F$ may or may not itself be a σ-algebra}@}\). It is, in fact, {@{the intersection of all σ-algebras containing $F$}@}. \(See {@{intersections of σ-algebras}@} above.\) {@{This σ-algebra}@} is denoted {@{$\sigma (F)$ and is called __the σ-algebra generated by $F$<!-- LaTeX separator -->__}@}. <!--SR:!2026-04-12,4,300!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,277!2026-04-12,4,300!2026-04-12,4,310-->

If {@{$F$ is empty}@}, then {@{$\sigma (\varnothing )=\{\varnothing ,X\}$}@}. Otherwise {@{$\sigma (F)$}@} consists of all {@{the subsets of $X$ that can be made from elements of $F$}@} by {@{a countable number of complement, union and intersection operations}@}. <!--SR:!2026-04-12,4,300!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,300!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310-->

For {@{a simple example}@}, consider {@{the set $X=\{1,2,3\}$}@}. Then {@{the σ-algebra generated by the single subset $\{1\}$}@} is {@{$\sigma (\{1\})=\{\varnothing ,\{1\},\{2,3\},\{1,2,3\}\}$}@}. By {@{an [abuse of notation](abuse%20of%20notation.md)}@}, when {@{a collection of subsets contains only one element, $A$}@}, {@{$\sigma (A)$ may be written}@} instead of {@{$\sigma (\{A\})$}@}; in the prior example {@{$\sigma (\{1\})$ instead of $\sigma (\{\{1\}\})$}@}. Indeed, using {@{$\sigma \left(A_{1},A_{2},\ldots \right)$ to mean $\sigma \left(\left\{A_{1},A_{2},\ldots \right\}\right)$}@} is {@{also quite common}@}. <!--SR:!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,277!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,300!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,277!2026-04-12,4,277!2026-04-12,4,310-->

There are many {@{families of subsets that generate useful σ-algebras}@}. {@{Some of these}@} are presented here. <!--SR:!2026-04-12,4,300!2026-04-12,4,310-->

### σ-algebra generated by a function

If $f$ is {@{a function from a set $X$ to a set $Y$}@} and $B$ is {@{a $\sigma$-algebra of subsets of $Y$}@}, then {@{the __<!-- LaTeX separator -->$\sigma$-algebra generated by the function $f$<!-- LaTeX separator -->__, denoted by $\sigma (f)$}@}, is {@{the collection of all inverse images $f^{-1}(S)$ of the sets $S$ in $B$}@}. That is, {@{$$\sigma (f)=\left\{f^{-1}(S)\,:\,S\in B\right\}.$$}@} (annotation: This is already {@{a σ-algebra by the properties of inverse images}@}, so there is no {@{need to take the σ-algebra generated by it}@}.) <!--SR:!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,300!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310-->

{@{A function $f$ from a set $X$ to a set $Y$}@} is {@{[measurable](measurable%20function.md) with respect to a σ-algebra $\Sigma$ of subsets of $X$}@} {@{if and only if $\sigma (f)$ is a subset of $\Sigma$}@}. <!--SR:!2026-04-12,4,277!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310-->

{@{One common situation}@}, and understood {@{by default if $B$ is not specified explicitly}@}, is when $Y$ is {@{a [metric](metric%20space.md) or [topological space](topological%20space.md)}@} and $B$ is {@{the collection of [Borel sets](Borel%20set.md) on $Y$}@}. <!--SR:!2026-04-12,4,277!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310-->

If $f$ is {@{a function from $X$ to $\mathbb {R} ^{n}$}@} then {@{$\sigma (f)$}@} is generated by {@{the family of subsets which are inverse images}@} of {@{intervals/rectangles in $\mathbb {R} ^{n}$}@}: {@{$$\sigma (f)=\sigma \left(\left\{f^{-1}(\left[a_{1},b_{1}\right]\times \cdots \times \left[a_{n},b_{n}\right]):a_{i},b_{i}\in \mathbb {R} \right\}\right).$$}@} (annotation: {@{These rectangles}@} form {@{a π-system that generates the Borel σ-algebra on $\mathbb{R}^n$}@}.) <!--SR:!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,277!2026-04-12,4,277!2026-04-12,4,300!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,299!2026-04-12,4,310-->

{@{A useful property}@} is {@{the following}@}. Assume $f$ is {@{a measurable map from $\left(X,\Sigma _{X}\right)$ to $\left(S,\Sigma _{S}\right)$}@} and $g$ is {@{a measurable map from $\left(X,\Sigma _{X}\right)$ to $\left(T,\Sigma _{T}\right)$}@}. If there exists {@{a measurable map $h$ from $\left(T,\Sigma _{T}\right)$ to $\left(S,\Sigma _{S}\right)$}@} such that {@{$f(x)=h(g(x))$ for all $x$}@}, then {@{$\sigma (f)\subseteq \sigma (g)$}@}. (annotation: Categorically, in {@{the category __Meas__}@}, if $f$ {@{factors through $g$ (i.e. $f=h\circ g$ for some measurable $h$)}@}, then {@{$f$ is no more informative than $g$}@}, equivalently {@{$\sigma(f)\subseteq\sigma(g)$}@}; intuitively, for every {@{measurable set $A$ in the codomain of $f$}@}, its {@{preimage under $f$ is $g^{-1}(h^{-1}(A))$}@}, so every {@{set measured by $f$ is already measured by $g$}@}.) If $S$ is {@{finite or countably infinite}@} or, more generally, {@{$\left(S,\Sigma _{S}\right)$ is a [standard Borel space](standard%20Borel%20space.md)}@} \(for example, {@{a separable complete metric space}@} with its {@{associated Borel sets}@}\), then {@{the converse is also true}@}.<sup>[\[6\]](#^ref-6)</sup> (annotation: {@{The converse}@} states that, under {@{the above additional condition}@}, if {@{$\sigma (f)\subseteq \sigma (g)$}@}, then there {@{exists a measurable $h$ such that $f=h\circ g$}@}. This is {@{a nontrivial result}@} that relies on {@{the structure of standard Borel spaces}@}; it fails for {@{arbitrary measurable spaces}@}.) {@{Examples of standard Borel spaces}@} include {@{$\mathbb {R} ^{n}$ with its Borel sets and $\mathbb {R} ^{\infty }$ with the cylinder σ-algebra}@} described below. <!--SR:!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,300!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,300!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,300!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,300!2026-04-12,4,300!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,277!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,277-->

### Borel and Lebesgue σ-algebras

{@{An important example}@} is {@{the [Borel algebra](Borel%20algebra.md) over any [topological space](topological%20space.md)}@}: {@{the σ-algebra generated by the [open sets](open%20set.md)}@} \(or, equivalently, by {@{the [closed sets](closed%20set.md)}@}\). {@{This σ-algebra}@} is not, {@{in general, the whole power set}@}. For {@{a non-trivial example that is not a Borel set}@}, see {@{the [Vitali set](Vitali%20set.md) or [Non-Borel sets](Borel%20set.md#non-Borel%20sets)}@}. <!--SR:!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,277!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,297!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310-->

On {@{the [Euclidean space](Euclidean%20space.md) $\mathbb {R} ^{n}$}@}, {@{another σ-algebra}@} is of importance: that of {@{all [Lebesgue measurable](Lebesgue%20measure.md) sets}@}. {@{This σ-algebra}@} contains {@{more sets than the Borel σ-algebra on $\mathbb {R} ^{n}$}@} (annotation: Note {@{a weak form of the axiom of choice is needed}@}. {@{ZF alone}@} is {@{consistent with the statement that all subsets of the real numbers are Borel sets}@}, and thus that {@{the Borel σ-algebra on the real numbers}@} is equal to {@{the Lebesgue-measure σ-algebra on the real numbers}@}.) and is {@{preferred in [integration](integral.md) theory}@}, as it {@{gives a [complete measure space](complete%20measure.md)}@}. (annotation: In such a space, every {@{subset of a null set is measurable}@}, so there are {@{no "holes" in the measure space}@}.) <!--SR:!2026-04-12,4,300!2026-04-12,4,300!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,300!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,300!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310-->

### product σ-algebra

Let {@{$\left(X_{1},\Sigma _{1}\right)$ and $\left(X_{2},\Sigma _{2}\right)$}@} be {@{two measurable spaces}@}. {@{The σ-algebra for the corresponding [product space](product%20space.md) $X_{1}\times X_{2}$}@} is called {@{the __product σ-algebra__}@} and is defined by {@{$$\Sigma _{1}\times \Sigma _{2}=\sigma \left(\left\{B_{1}\times B_{2}:B_{1}\in \Sigma _{1},B_{2}\in \Sigma _{2}\right\}\right).$$}@} Observe that {@{$\{B_{1}\times B_{2}:B_{1}\in \Sigma _{1},B_{2}\in \Sigma _{2}\}$}@} is {@{a π-system}@}. (annotation: Intersecting {@{two sets of the form $B_1 \times B_2$}@} produces {@{another set of the same form}@}, since {@{$\left(B_{1}\times B_{2}\right)\cap \left(B'_{1}\times B'_{2}\right)=\left(B_{1}\cap B'_{1}\right)\times \left(B_{2}\cap B'_{2}\right)$}@}.) <!--SR:!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,300!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,300!2026-04-12,4,310-->

{@{The Borel σ-algebra for $\mathbb {R} ^{n}$}@} is generated by {@{half-infinite rectangles and by finite rectangles (annotation: any one of two suffices)}@}. For example, {@{$${\mathcal {B} }(\mathbb {R} ^{n})=\sigma \left(\left\{(-\infty ,b_{1}]\times \cdots \times (-\infty ,b_{n}]:b_{i}\in \mathbb {R} \right\}\right)=\sigma \left(\left\{\left(a_{1},b_{1}\right]\times \cdots \times \left(a_{n},b_{n}\right]:a_{i},b_{i}\in \mathbb {R} \right\}\right).$$}@} For {@{each of these two examples}@}, {@{the generating family is a π-system}@}. <!--SR:!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,277!2026-04-12,4,277!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,277-->

### σ-algebra generated by cylinder sets

- Main article: ::@:: [Cylinder σ-algebra](cylinder%20σ-algebra.md) <!--SR:!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310-->

Suppose {@{$$X\subseteq \mathbb {R} ^{\mathbb {T} }=\{f:f(t)\in \mathbb {R} ,\ t\in \mathbb {T} \}$$}@} is {@{a set of real-valued functions}@}. Let ${\mathcal {B} }(\mathbb {R} )$ denote {@{the Borel subsets of $\mathbb {R}$}@}. {@{A [cylinder subset](cylinder%20set.md) of $X$}@} is {@{a finitely restricted set}@} defined as {@{$$C_{t_{1},\dots ,t_{n} }(B_{1},\dots ,B_{n})=\left\{f\in X:f(t_{i})\in B_{i},1\leq i\leq n\right\}.$$}@} (annotation: In words, {@{the set of functions in $X$}@} that is {@{restricted to lie in $B_i$ at each of the finite number of points $t_i$}@}, but {@{is unrestricted elsewhere}@}.) <!--SR:!2026-04-12,4,277!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,277!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,277!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310-->

Each {@{$$\left\{C_{t_{1},\dots ,t_{n} }\left(B_{1},\dots ,B_{n}\right):B_{i}\in {\mathcal {B} }(\mathbb {R} ),1\leq i\leq n\right\}$$}@} (annotation: In words, {@{a collection}@} of {@{the cylinder sets}@} defined by {@{the same finite set of points $t_1,\dots,t_n$}@}.) is {@{a π-system that generates a σ-algebra $\textstyle \Sigma _{t_{1},\dots ,t_{n} }$}@}. Then {@{the family of subsets}@} {@{$${\mathcal {F} }_{X}=\bigcup _{n=1}^{\infty }\bigcup _{t_{i}\in \mathbb {T} ,i\leq n}\Sigma _{t_{1},\dots ,t_{n} }$$}@} (annotation: In words, the family is obtained by taking {@{the union of all the σ-algebras}@} generated by {@{cylinder sets defined by any finite set of points}@}.) is {@{an algebra that generates the __[cylinder σ-algebra](cylinder%20σ-algebra.md)__ for $X$}@}. {@{This σ-algebra}@} is {@{a subalgebra}@} of {@{the Borel σ-algebra determined by the [product topology](product%20topology.md) of $\mathbb {R} ^{\mathbb {T} }$}@} {@{restricted to $X$}@}. (annotation: The wording here is {@{slightly unclear}@}. To clarify, the product topology {@{induces a Borel σ-algebra}@}, and restricting {@{that Borel σ-algebra to $X$ is the cylinder σ-algebra for $X$}@}.) <!--SR:!2026-04-12,4,300!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,277!2026-04-12,4,297!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,300!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,270!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,277!2026-04-12,4,277!2026-04-12,4,310-->

{@{An important special case}@} is when $\mathbb {T}$ is {@{the set of natural numbers}@} and $X$ is {@{a set of real-valued sequences}@}. In this case, it suffices to consider {@{the cylinder sets}@} {@{$$C_{n}\left(B_{1},\dots ,B_{n}\right)=\left(B_{1}\times \cdots \times B_{n}\times \mathbb {R} ^{\infty }\right)\cap X=\left\{\left(x_{1},x_{2},\ldots ,x_{n},x_{n+1},\ldots \right)\in X:x_{i}\in B_{i},1\leq i\leq n\right\},$$}@} (annotation: In words, {@{the set of sequences in $X$}@} that is {@{restricted to lie in $B_i$ at each of the first $n$ coordinates}@}, but is {@{unrestricted elsewhere}@}.) for which {@{$$\Sigma _{n}=\sigma \left(\{C_{n}\left(B_{1},\dots ,B_{n}\right):B_{i}\in {\mathcal {B} }(\mathbb {R} ),1\leq i\leq n\}\right)$$}@} is {@{a non-decreasing sequence of σ-algebras}@}. <!--SR:!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,300!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,277!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,300-->

### ball σ-algebra

{@{The ball σ-algebra}@} is {@{the smallest σ-algebra containing all the open \(and/or closed\) balls}@}. This is never {@{larger than the [Borel σ-algebra](Borel%20set.md)}@} (annotation: since every {@{open ball is a open set}@}). Note that {@{the two σ-algebra}@} are {@{equal for separable spaces}@}. For {@{some nonseparable spaces}@}, some maps are {@{ball measurable even though they are not Borel measurable}@}, making use of {@{the ball σ-algebra useful in the analysis}@} of such maps.<sup>[\[7\]](#^ref-7)</sup> <!--SR:!2026-04-12,4,277!2026-04-12,4,299!2026-04-12,4,277!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,277!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310-->

### σ-algebra generated by random variable or vector

Suppose {@{$(\Omega ,\Sigma ,\mathbb {P} )$}@} is {@{a [probability space](probability%20space.md)}@}. If {@{$\textstyle Y:\Omega \to \mathbb {R} ^{n}$}@} is {@{measurable with respect to the Borel σ-algebra on $\mathbb {R} ^{n}$}@} then $Y$ is called {@{a __[random variable](random%20variable.md)__ \($n=1$\) or __random vector__ \($n>1$\)}@}. {@{The σ-algebra generated by $Y$}@} is {@{$$\sigma (Y)=\left\{Y^{-1}(A):A\in {\mathcal {B} }\left(\mathbb {R} ^{n}\right)\right\}.$$}@} (annotation: This is already {@{a σ-algebra by the properties of inverse images}@}, so there is no {@{need to take the σ-algebra generated by it}@}.) <!--SR:!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,277!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,277!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,300-->

### σ-algebra generated by a stochastic process

Suppose {@{$(\Omega ,\Sigma ,\mathbb {P} )$}@} is {@{a [probability space](probability%20space.md)}@} and $\mathbb {R} ^{\mathbb {T} }$ is {@{the set of real-valued functions on $\mathbb {T}$}@}. If {@{$\textstyle Y:\Omega \to X\subseteq \mathbb {R} ^{\mathbb {T} }$}@} is {@{measurable with respect to the cylinder σ-algebra $\sigma \left({\mathcal {F} }_{X}\right)$ \(see above\) for $X$}@} then $Y$ is called {@{a __[stochastic process](stochastic%20process.md)__ or __random process__}@}. {@{The σ-algebra generated by $Y$}@} is {@{$$\sigma (Y)=\left\{Y^{-1}(A):A\in \sigma \left({\mathcal {F} }_{X}\right)\right\}=\sigma \left(\left\{Y^{-1}(A):A\in {\mathcal {F} }_{X}\right\}\right),$$}@} {@{the σ-algebra generated by the inverse images of cylinder sets}@}. (annotation: For {@{the first equality}@}, it is already {@{a σ-algebra by the properties of inverse images}@}, so there is no {@{need to take the σ-algebra generated by it}@}.) <!--SR:!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,300!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,277!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,277!2026-04-12,4,277!2026-04-12,4,310!2026-04-12,4,277!2026-04-12,4,310-->

> Additionally, a ___[semiring](ring%20of%20sets.md#semiring)___ is a [_π_-system](pi-system.md) where every complement $B\setminus A$ is equal to a finite [disjoint union](disjoint%20union.md) of sets in ${\mathcal {F} }$. <br/> A ___[semialgebra](ring%20of%20sets.md#semialgebra)___ is a semiring where every complement $\Omega \setminus A$ is equal to a finite [disjoint union](disjoint%20union.md) of sets in ${\mathcal {F} }$. <br/> $A,B,A_{1},A_{2},\ldots$ are arbitrary elements of ${\mathcal {F} }$ and it is assumed that ${\mathcal {F} }\neq \varnothing$.
