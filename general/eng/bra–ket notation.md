{@{__Bra–ket notation__}@}, also called {@{__Dirac notation__}@}, is {@{a notation for [linear algebra](linear%20algebra.md) and [linear operators](linear%20operator.md)}@} on {@{[complex vector spaces](complex%20vector%20space.md) together with their [dual space](dual%20space.md) both in the finite-dimensional and infinite-dimensional case}@}. It is specifically designed to {@{ease the types of calculations}@} that frequently {@{come up in [quantum mechanics](quantum%20mechanics.md)}@}. Its {@{use in quantum mechanics}@} is {@{quite widespread}@}. <!--SR:!2027-06-30,509,401!2027-03-17,407,381!2027-04-30,451,381!2027-07-06,515,401!2027-04-27,448,381!2027-04-12,433,381!2027-03-12,402,381!2027-07-12,519,401-->

Bra–ket notation was created by {@{[Paul Dirac](Paul%20Dirac.md) in his 1939 publication _A New Notation for Quantum Mechanics_}@}. The notation was introduced as {@{an easier way to write quantum mechanical expressions}@}.<sup>[\[1\]](#^ref-1)</sup> {@{The name}@} comes from {@{the English word "bracket"}@}. <!--SR:!2028-10-27,1213,350!2027-07-11,815,330!2026-03-14,129,389!2027-07-05,514,401-->

## quantum mechanics

In {@{[quantum mechanics](quantum%20mechanics.md) and [quantum computing](quantum%20computing.md)}@}, bra–ket notation is used {@{ubiquitously to denote [quantum states](quantum%20state.md)}@}. The notation uses {@{[angle brackets](angle%20bracket.md#angle%20brackets),  $\langle$ and  $\rangle$, and a [vertical bar](vertical%20bar.md)  $|$}@}, to {@{construct "bras" and "kets"}@}. <!--SR:!2027-12-20,972,350!2026-07-07,549,330!2028-02-10,1009,350!2027-06-26,507,401-->

{@{A __ket__}@} is of {@{the form $|v\rangle$}@}. Mathematically it denotes {@{a [vector](vector%20space.md), ${\boldsymbol {v} }$}@}, in {@{an abstract \(complex\) [vector space](vector%20space.md) $V$}@}, and physically it {@{represents a state of some quantum system}@}. \(annotation: A simple case is in {@{the finite-dimensional complex vector space $\mathbb C^n$}@}, {@{a ket}@} can be identified with {@{a [column vector](row%20and%20column%20vectors.md)}@}: {@{$$|v\rangle = \mathbf v = \begin{bmatrix} v_1 \\ \vdots \\ v_n \end{bmatrix}$$}@}.\) <!--SR:!2028-01-16,992,350!2028-04-14,1058,350!2028-06-26,1119,350!2030-06-20,1642,330!2027-03-24,738,330!2027-06-25,506,401!2027-05-05,456,381!2027-03-27,421,381!2027-05-03,457,381-->

{@{A __bra__}@} is of {@{the form $\langle f|$}@}. Mathematically it denotes {@{a [linear form](linear%20form.md) $f:V\to \mathbb {C}$}@}, i.e. {@{a [linear map](linear%20map.md) that maps each vector in $V$ to a number in the complex plane $\mathbb {C}$}@}. Letting {@{the linear functional $\langle f|$ act on a vector $|v\rangle$}@} is written as {@{$\langle f|v\rangle \in \mathbb {C}$}@}. \(annotation: A simple case is in {@{the finite-dimensional complex vector space $\mathbb C^n$}@}, {@{a bra}@} can be identified with {@{a [row vector](row%20and%20column%20vectors.md)}@}: {@{$$\langle f| = \mathbf f = \begin{bmatrix} f_1 & \cdots & f_n \end{bmatrix}$$}@}. Applying {@{the linear functional $\langle f|$ on a vector $|v \rangle$}@} is then {@{a [matrix multiplication](matrix%20multiplication.md)}@}: {@{$$\langle f|v \rangle = \mathbf f \mathbf v = \begin{bmatrix} f_1 & \cdots & f_n \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} v_1 \\ \vdots \\ v_n \end{bmatrix} = \sum_{k = 1}^n f_k v_k$$}@}.\) <!--SR:!2027-04-05,430,381!2027-04-07,428,381!2027-05-06,461,381!2027-07-30,535,401!2027-04-27,452,381!2027-12-12,647,409!2027-07-28,534,401!2027-12-08,643,409!2026-03-08,124,389!2027-08-02,538,401!2027-03-16,410,381!2027-07-28,534,401!2027-05-07,458,381-->

Assume that on $V$ there {@{exists an inner product $(\cdot ,\cdot )$}@} with {@{[antilinear](antilinear%20map.md) first argument}@}, which makes {@{$V$ an [inner product space](inner%20product%20space.md)}@}. Then with {@{this inner product}@} {@{each vector ${\boldsymbol {\phi } }\equiv |\phi \rangle$}@} can be {@{identified with a corresponding linear form}@}, by placing {@{the vector in the anti-linear first slot of the inner product}@}: {@{$({\boldsymbol {\phi } },\cdot )\equiv \langle \phi |$}@}. {@{The correspondence between these notations}@} is then {@{$({\boldsymbol {\phi } },{\boldsymbol {\psi } })\equiv \langle \phi |\psi \rangle$}@}. {@{The [linear form](linear%20form.md) $\langle \phi |$}@} is {@{a [covector](covector.md) to $|\phi \rangle$}@}, and {@{the set of all covectors}@} forms {@{a subspace of the [dual vector space](dual%20vector%20space.md) $V^{\vee }$}@}, to {@{the initial vector space $V$}@}. {@{The purpose of this linear form $\langle \phi |$}@} can now be understood in terms of {@{making projections onto the state ${\boldsymbol {\phi } }$}@}, to find {@{how linearly dependent two states are, etc.}@} <!--SR:!2027-04-23,448,381!2027-04-18,439,381!2027-04-21,442,381!2027-04-02,423,381!2027-04-16,437,381!2027-12-08,644,409!2027-07-24,530,401!2027-02-24,402,381!2027-04-17,441,381!2027-04-13,434,381!2027-04-14,438,381!2027-04-09,433,381!2027-07-23,529,401!2027-04-06,431,381!2027-05-01,452,381!2027-04-06,430,381!2027-04-16,441,381!2027-07-04,513,401-->

For {@{the vector space $\mathbb {C} ^{n}$}@}, kets can be {@{identified with column vectors, and bras with row vectors}@}. Combinations of {@{bras, kets, and linear operators}@} are interpreted using {@{[matrix multiplication](matrix%20multiplication.md)}@}. If $\mathbb {C} ^{n}$ has {@{the standard Hermitian inner product $({\boldsymbol {v} },{\boldsymbol {w} })=v^{\dagger }w$}@}, under {@{this identification}@}, {@{the identification of kets and bras and vice versa}@} provided by {@{the inner product}@} is taking {@{the [Hermitian conjugate](Hermitian%20conjugate.md)}@} \(denoted {@{$\dagger$}@}\). <!--SR:!2027-07-02,511,401!2027-04-26,451,381!2027-07-14,522,401!2027-07-08,517,401!2027-04-15,436,381!2027-03-29,422,381!2027-04-27,451,381!2027-03-17,407,381!2027-04-24,448,381!2027-05-01,452,381-->

It is common to {@{suppress the vector or linear form}@} from the bra–ket notation and only use {@{a label inside the typography for the bra or ket}@}. For example, {@{the spin operator ${\hat {\sigma } }_{z}$}@} on {@{a two-dimensional space $\Delta$ of [spinors](spinor.md)}@} has {@{[eigenvalues](eigenvalue.md) $\pm {\frac {1}{2} }$}@} with {@{eigenspinors ${\boldsymbol {\psi } }_{+},{\boldsymbol {\psi } }_{-}\in \Delta$}@}. In {@{bra–ket notation}@}, this is typically denoted as {@{${\boldsymbol {\psi } }_{+}=|+\rangle$, and ${\boldsymbol {\psi } }_{-}=|-\rangle$}@}. As above, {@{kets and bras with the same label}@} are interpreted as {@{kets and bras corresponding to each other using the inner product}@}. In particular, when also {@{identified with row and column vectors}@}, {@{kets and bras with the same label}@} are identified with {@{[Hermitian conjugate](Hermitian%20conjugate.md) column and row vectors}@}. <!--SR:!2027-12-02,638,409!2027-07-13,521,401!2027-08-03,539,401!2027-04-11,432,381!2027-04-13,438,381!2027-05-08,463,381!2027-04-08,429,381!2027-07-30,535,401!2027-05-09,460,381!2026-03-14,129,389!2027-04-18,443,381!2027-06-24,505,401!2027-07-08,516,401-->

Bra–ket notation was effectively {@{established in 1939 by [Paul Dirac](Paul%20Dirac.md)}@};<sup>[\[1\]](#^ref-1)</sup><sup>[\[2\]](#^ref-2)</sup> it is thus also known as {@{Dirac notation}@}, despite the notation having {@{a precursor in [Hermann Grassmann](Hermann%20Grassmann.md)'s use of $[\phi {\mid }\psi ]$}@} for {@{inner products nearly 100 years earlier}@}.<sup>[\[3\]](#^ref-3)</sup><sup>[\[4\]](#^ref-4)</sup> <!--SR:!2027-05-08,459,381!2027-04-20,445,381!2027-04-17,442,381!2027-07-26,532,401-->

## vector spaces

### vectors vs kets

In {@{mathematics}@}, {@{the term "vector"}@} is used for {@{an element of any vector space}@}. In {@{physics}@}, however, {@{the term "vector"}@} tends to refer {@{almost exclusively to quantities}@} like {@{[displacement](displacement%20(vector).md) or [velocity](velocity.md)}@}, which have {@{components that relate directly to the three dimensions of [space](space.md)}@}, or {@{relativistically, to the four of [spacetime](spacetime.md)}@}. {@{Such vectors}@} are typically denoted with {@{over arrows \(${\vec {r} }$\), boldface \($\mathbf {p}$\) or indices \($v^{\mu }$\)}@}. <!--SR:!2027-04-21,446,381!2027-05-06,457,381!2027-04-25,446,381!2027-07-23,529,401!2026-03-14,129,389!2027-07-18,525,401!2027-12-28,661,409!2027-07-25,531,401!2027-07-03,512,401!2027-07-31,536,401!2027-06-26,506,401-->

In {@{quantum mechanics}@}, {@{a quantum state}@} is typically represented as {@{an element of a complex [Hilbert space](Hilbert%20space.md)}@}, for example, {@{the infinite-dimensional vector space}@} of {@{all possible [wavefunctions](wavefunction.md)}@} \({@{square integrable functions}@} mapping {@{each point of 3D space to a complex number}@}\) or some {@{more abstract Hilbert space constructed more algebraically}@}. To distinguish {@{this type of vector from those described above}@}, it is {@{common and useful in physics}@} to denote {@{an element $\phi$ of an abstract complex vector space}@} as {@{a ket $|\phi \rangle$}@}, to refer to it as {@{a "ket" rather than as a vector}@}, and to pronounce it {@{"ket-$\phi$" or "ket-A" for \|_A_<!-- markdown separator -->⟩}@}. <!--SR:!2027-04-24,445,381!2027-07-30,535,401!2027-07-02,512,401!2027-12-09,645,409!2027-05-10,461,381!2026-03-11,126,389!2027-04-28,453,381!2027-04-22,447,381!2027-04-09,434,381!2027-03-25,415,381!2027-04-04,425,381!2026-03-14,129,389!2027-03-18,408,381!2027-07-17,524,401-->

{@{Symbols, letters, numbers, or even words}@}—whatever {@{serves as a convenient label}@}—can be used as {@{the label inside a ket}@}, with {@{the $|\ \rangle$}@} making clear that {@{the label indicates a vector in vector space}@}. In other words, {@{the symbol "\|_A_<!-- markdown separator -->⟩"}@} has {@{a recognizable mathematical meaning}@} as to {@{the kind of variable being represented}@}, while {@{just the "_A_" by itself}@} {@{does not}@}. For example, {@{\|1⟩ + \|2⟩}@} is {@{not necessarily equal to \|3⟩}@}. Nevertheless, for {@{convenience}@}, there is usually {@{some logical scheme behind the labels inside kets}@}, such as {@{the common practice}@} of {@{labeling [energy eigenkets](stationary%20state.md) in quantum mechanics}@} through {@{a listing of their [quantum numbers](quantum%20number.md)}@}. At {@{its simplest}@}, {@{the label inside the ket}@} is {@{the eigenvalue of a physical operator}@}, such as {@{${\hat {x} }$, ${\hat {p} }$, ${\hat {L} }_{z}$, etc.}@} <!--SR:!2027-04-07,428,381!2026-03-11,126,389!2027-11-11,621,409!2026-03-14,129,389!2027-03-26,420,381!2027-04-27,448,381!2027-07-27,533,401!2027-07-30,535,401!2027-07-03,513,401!2027-05-01,456,381!2026-03-14,129,389!2027-04-21,442,381!2027-04-30,451,381!2027-04-22,447,381!2027-04-27,448,381!2027-06-26,507,401!2027-04-05,426,381!2027-04-08,429,381!2027-04-05,426,381!2027-04-04,425,381!2027-04-22,443,381-->

### notation

Since {@{kets}@} are {@{just vectors in a Hermitian vector space}@}, they can be {@{manipulated using the usual rules of linear algebra}@}. For example: {@{$${\begin{aligned}|A\rangle &=|B\rangle +|C\rangle \\|C\rangle &=(-1+2i)|D\rangle \\|D\rangle &=\int _{-\infty }^{\infty }e^{-x^{2} }|x\rangle \,\mathrm {d} x\,.\end{aligned} }$$}@} Note how {@{the last line above}@} involves {@{infinitely many different kets}@}, one for {@{each real number _x_}@}. <!--SR:!2027-07-11,518,401!2027-07-28,534,401!2027-07-27,533,401!2027-07-08,517,401!2027-04-30,455,381!2027-07-26,532,401!2027-05-05,456,381-->

Since {@{the ket}@} is {@{an element of a vector space}@}, {@{a __bra__ $\langle A|$}@} is {@{an element of its [dual space](dual%20space.md)}@}, i.e. a bra is {@{a linear functional which is a linear map from the vector space to the complex numbers}@}. Thus, it is useful to think of {@{kets and bras}@} as being {@{elements of different vector spaces}@} \(see below however\) with both being {@{different useful concepts}@}. <!--SR:!2027-12-11,646,409!2027-04-24,449,381!2027-03-21,411,381!2027-05-03,454,381!2027-08-03,539,401!2027-07-05,514,401!2027-07-21,528,401!2027-04-10,435,381-->

{@{A bra $\langle \phi |$ and a ket $|\psi \rangle$}@} \(i.e. {@{a functional and a vector}@}\), can be {@{combined}@} to {@{an operator $|\psi \rangle \langle \phi |$ of rank one}@} with {@{[outer product](outer%20product.md)}@} {@{$$|\psi \rangle \langle \phi |\colon |\xi \rangle \mapsto |\psi \rangle \langle \phi |\xi \rangle ~.$$}@} <!--SR:!2027-03-23,417,381!2027-03-16,406,381!2027-03-22,416,381!2027-06-27,507,401!2027-04-03,424,381!2027-07-28,534,401-->

### inner product and bra–ket identification on Hilbert space

- Main article: ::@:: [Inner product](inner%20product.md) <!--SR:!2027-06-19,500,401!2027-04-25,449,381-->

The bra–ket notation is particularly {@{useful in Hilbert spaces}@} which have {@{an inner product}@}<sup>[\[5\]](#^ref-5)</sup> that allows {@{[Hermitian conjugation](Hermitian%20conjugation.md) and identifying a vector with a continuous linear functional}@}, i.e. {@{a ket with a bra, and vice versa}@} \(see {@{[Riesz representation theorem](Riesz%20representation%20theorem.md)}@}\). {@{The inner product on Hilbert space $(\ ,\ )$}@} \(with {@{the first argument anti linear}@} as {@{preferred by physicists}@}\) is {@{fully equivalent to an \(anti-linear\) identification}@} between {@{the space of kets and that of bras}@} in the bra–ket notation: for {@{a vector ket $\psi =|\psi \rangle$}@} define {@{a functional \(i.e. bra\) $f_{\phi }=\langle \phi |$}@} by {@{$$(\phi ,\psi )=(|\phi \rangle ,|\psi \rangle )=:f_{\phi }(\psi )=\langle \phi |\,{\bigl (}|\psi \rangle {\bigr )}=:\langle \phi {\mid }\psi \rangle$$}@} <!--SR:!2027-07-03,513,401!2027-04-13,434,381!2026-11-20,309,361!2027-03-20,413,381!2027-04-09,434,381!2027-04-08,429,381!2027-04-10,431,381!2027-08-01,537,401!2027-04-14,435,381!2027-07-29,534,401!2027-03-28,422,381!2027-10-27,609,409!2027-05-02,456,381-->

#### bras and kets as row and column vectors

In {@{the simple case}@} where we consider {@{the vector space $\mathbb {C} ^{n}$}@}, {@{a ket}@} can be {@{identified with a [column vector](column%20vector.md)}@}, and {@{a bra as a [row vector](row%20vector.md)}@}. If, moreover, we use {@{the standard Hermitian inner product on $\mathbb {C} ^{n}$}@}, {@{the bra corresponding to a ket}@}, in particular {@{a bra ⟨<!-- markdown separator -->_m_\| and a ket \|_m_<!-- markdown separator -->⟩ with the same label}@} are {@{[conjugate transpose](conjugate%20transpose.md)}@}. Moreover, {@{conventions are set up}@} in such a way that {@{writing bras, kets, and linear operators}@} {@{next to each other}@} simply {@{imply [matrix multiplication](matrix%20multiplication.md)}@}.<sup>[\[6\]](#^ref-6)</sup> In particular {@{the outer product $|\psi \rangle \langle \phi |$}@} of {@{a column and a row vector ket and bra}@} can be identified with {@{matrix multiplication}@} \({@{column vector times row vector}@} equals {@{matrix}@}\). <!--SR:!2027-04-05,426,381!2027-04-11,432,381!2027-07-20,527,401!2027-04-24,445,381!2027-07-27,533,401!2027-07-09,518,401!2027-11-12,622,409!2027-08-03,539,401!2027-07-04,513,401!2027-07-20,527,401!2027-04-03,427,381!2027-04-19,440,381!2027-07-29,534,401!2027-06-20,501,401!2026-03-14,129,389!2027-07-04,513,401!2027-05-07,462,381!2027-03-28,421,381-->

For {@{a finite-dimensional vector space, using a fixed [orthonormal basis](orthonormal%20basis.md)}@}, {@{the inner product}@} can be written as {@{a matrix multiplication of a row vector with a column vector}@}: {@{$$\langle A|B\rangle \doteq A_{1}^{*}B_{1}+A_{2}^{*}B_{2}+\cdots +A_{N}^{*}B_{N}={\begin{pmatrix}A_{1}^{*}&A_{2}^{*}&\cdots &A_{N}^{*}\end{pmatrix} }{\begin{pmatrix}B_{1}\\B_{2}\\\vdots \\B_{N}\end{pmatrix} }$$}@} Based on this, {@{the bras and kets}@} can be defined as: {@{$${\begin{aligned}\langle A|&\doteq {\begin{pmatrix}A_{1}^{*}&A_{2}^{*}&\cdots &A_{N}^{*}\end{pmatrix} }\\|B\rangle &\doteq {\begin{pmatrix}B_{1}\\B_{2}\\\vdots \\B_{N}\end{pmatrix} }\end{aligned} }$$}@} and then it is understood that {@{a bra next to a ket}@} {@{implies matrix multiplication}@}. <!--SR:!2027-11-02,614,409!2027-04-15,440,381!2027-04-27,451,381!2027-07-13,521,401!2027-08-02,538,401!2027-07-27,533,401!2027-04-14,435,381!2026-11-27,316,361-->

{@{The [conjugate transpose](conjugate%20transpose.md) \(also called _Hermitian conjugate_\)}@} of {@{a bra}@} is {@{the corresponding ket and vice versa}@}: {@{$$\langle A|^{\dagger }=|A\rangle ,\quad |A\rangle ^{\dagger }=\langle A|$$}@} because if one starts with {@{the bra $${\begin{pmatrix}A_{1}^{*}&A_{2}^{*}&\cdots &A_{N}^{*}\end{pmatrix} }\,,$$}@} then {@{performs a [complex conjugation](complex%20conjugation.md), and then a [matrix transpose](matrix%20transpose.md)}@}, one ends up with {@{the ket $${\begin{pmatrix}A_{1}\\A_{2}\\\vdots \\A_{N}\end{pmatrix} }$$}@} <!--SR:!2027-03-19,409,381!2027-04-23,444,381!2027-07-07,515,401!2027-03-23,416,381!2027-06-23,504,401!2027-03-23,417,381!2027-04-19,440,381-->

Writing {@{elements of a finite dimensional \(or [mutatis mutandis](mutatis%20mutandis.md), countably infinite\) vector space}@} as {@{a column vector of numbers}@} requires {@{picking a [basis](basis%20(linear%20algebra).md)}@}. {@{Picking a basis}@} is {@{not always helpful}@} because {@{quantum mechanics calculations}@} involve {@{frequently switching between different bases}@} \(e.g. {@{position basis, momentum basis, energy eigenbasis}@}\), and one can write {@{something like "\|_m_<!-- markdown separator -->⟩"}@} without {@{committing to any particular basis}@}. In situations involving {@{two different important basis vectors}@}, {@{the basis vectors}@} can be {@{taken in the notation explicitly}@} and here will be referred simply as {@{"\|_−_<!-- markdown separator -->⟩" and "\|_+_<!-- markdown separator -->⟩"}@}. <!--SR:!2027-04-11,432,381!2027-11-19,628,409!2027-04-11,432,381!2027-06-28,508,401!2027-07-09,518,401!2027-06-29,509,401!2027-04-01,426,381!2027-07-12,520,401!2026-03-08,124,389!2027-03-22,412,381!2027-03-24,414,381!2027-07-10,518,401!2027-07-08,517,401!2027-04-20,441,381-->

### non-normalizable states and non-Hilbert spaces

{@{Bra–ket notation can be used}@} even if {@{the vector space is not a [Hilbert space](Hilbert%20space.md)}@}. <!--SR:!2027-11-13,623,409!2027-07-01,511,401-->

In {@{quantum mechanics}@}, it is common practice to {@{write down kets which have infinite [norm](norm%20(mathematics).md)}@}, i.e. {@{non-[normalizable wavefunctions](normalizable%20wavefunction.md)}@}. Examples include {@{states whose wavefunctions are [Dirac delta functions](Dirac%20delta%20function.md)}@} or {@{infinite [plane waves](plane%20wave.md)}@}. However, {@{the definition of "Hilbert space"}@} can be {@{broadened to accommodate these states}@} \(see {@{the [Gelfand–Naimark–Segal construction](Gelfand–Naimark–Segal%20construction.md)}@} or {@{[rigged Hilbert spaces](rigged%20Hilbert%20space.md)}@}\). {@{The bra–ket notation}@} continues to {@{work in an analogous way}@} in {@{this more general context}@}. <!--SR:!2027-07-31,536,401!2027-04-29,450,381!2027-07-24,530,401!2027-05-12,463,381!2027-11-26,634,409!2027-02-28,406,381!2027-04-29,450,381!2027-07-07,516,401!2027-04-21,446,381!2027-07-19,526,401!2027-06-27,507,401!2027-07-03,512,401!2027-04-12,433,381-->

{@{[Banach spaces](Banach%20spaces.md)}@} are {@{a different generalization of Hilbert spaces}@}. In {@{a Banach space B}@}, {@{the vectors}@} may be {@{notated by kets}@} and {@{the continuous [linear functionals](linear%20functionals.md) by bras}@}. In {@{these more general contexts}@}, {@{the bracket}@} does not {@{have the meaning of an inner product}@}, because {@{the [Riesz representation theorem](Riesz%20representation%20theorem.md) does not apply}@}. <!--SR:!2027-05-12,463,381!2027-04-26,450,381!2027-04-08,433,381!2027-04-23,447,381!2027-04-07,428,381!2027-06-18,499,401!2027-07-19,526,401!2027-06-25,506,401!2027-11-23,631,409!2026-03-14,129,389!2027-04-04,429,381!2027-04-22,443,381!2027-12-22,656,409-->

## usage in quantum mechanics

{@{The mathematical structure}@} of {@{quantum mechanics}@} is based {@{in large part on [linear algebra](linear%20algebra.md)}@}: <!--SR:!2026-03-14,129,389!2027-06-24,505,401!2027-04-28,449,381-->

- {@{[Wave functions](wave%20function.md) and other quantum states}@} can be represented as {@{vectors in a [separable](separable%20space.md) [complex](complex%20number.md) [Hilbert space](Hilbert%20space.md)}@}. \({@{The exact structure of this Hilbert space}@} depends on {@{the situation}@}.\) In {@{bra–ket notation}@}, for example, {@{an electron}@} might be in {@{the "state" \|_ψ_<!-- markdown separator -->⟩}@}. \(Technically, {@{the quantum states}@} are {@{_[rays](ray%20(quantum%20theory).md)_ of vectors in the Hilbert space}@}, as {@{_c_\|_ψ_<!-- markdown separator -->⟩}@} corresponds to {@{the same state for any nonzero complex number _c_}@}.\)
- {@{[Quantum superpositions](quantum%20superposition.md)}@} can be described as {@{vector sums of the constituent states}@}. For example, {@{an electron}@} in {@{the state $\frac 1 {\sqrt 2} \lvert 1 \rangle + \frac i {\sqrt 2} \lvert 2 \rangle$}@} is in {@{a quantum superposition of the states \|1⟩ and \|2⟩}@}.
- {@{[Measurements](measurement%20in%20quantum%20mechanics.md)}@} are associated with {@{linear operators \(called [observables](observable.md)\)}@} on {@{the Hilbert space of quantum states}@}.
- {@{Dynamics}@} are also described by {@{linear operators on the Hilbert space}@}. For example, in {@{the [Schrödinger picture](Schrödinger%20picture.md)}@}, there is {@{a linear [time evolution](time%20evolution.md) operator _U_}@} with the property that if {@{an electron is in state \|_ψ_<!-- markdown separator -->⟩ right now}@}, at {@{a later time}@} it will be in {@{the state _U_\|_ψ_<!-- markdown separator -->⟩}@}, {@{the same _U_}@} for {@{every possible \|_ψ_<!-- markdown separator -->⟩}@}.
- {@{[Wave function normalization](normalizable%20wave%20function.md#normalization%20condition)}@} is {@{scaling a wave function}@} so that {@{its [norm](norm%20(mathematics).md) is 1}@}. <!--SR:!2027-04-11,435,381!2027-04-19,440,381!2027-07-03,513,401!2027-07-05,514,401!2027-04-10,431,381!2027-12-27,660,409!2027-04-25,446,381!2027-04-02,426,381!2027-04-29,450,381!2026-08-28,254,361!2027-07-21,528,401!2027-03-05,389,381!2027-05-05,456,381!2027-04-03,424,381!2027-05-19,447,389!2027-04-11,436,381!2027-01-28,378,381!2027-04-02,427,381!2027-06-21,502,401!2027-04-18,439,381!2027-07-29,534,401!2027-04-25,446,381!2027-07-21,528,401!2027-07-12,520,401!2027-04-19,440,381!2027-04-13,434,381!2027-04-03,424,381!2027-07-30,535,401!2026-06-06,123,341!2027-11-27,634,409!2026-03-29,142,411-->

{@{The Hilbert space}@} of {@{a [spin](spin%20(physics).md)-0 point particle}@} can be represented in terms of {@{a "position [basis](basis%20(linear%20algebra).md)" { \|__r__<!-- markdown separator -->⟩ }<!-- flashcard separator -->}@}, where {@{the label __r__}@} {@{extends over the set of all points in [position space](position%20space.md)}@}. {@{These states}@} satisfy {@{the eigenvalue equation for the [position operator](position%20operator.md)}@}: {@{$${\hat {\mathbf {r} } }|\mathbf {r} \rangle =\mathbf {r} |\mathbf {r} \rangle .$$}@} {@{The position states}@} are {@{"[generalized eigenvectors](Dirac%20delta%20function.md#quantum%20mechanics)"}@}, not {@{elements of the Hilbert space itself}@}, and do not {@{form a countable orthonormal basis}@}. On {@{the left-hand side}@}, {@{Ψ\(__r__\)}@} is {@{a function mapping any point in space to a complex number}@}; on {@{the right-hand side}@}, {@{$$\left|\Psi \right\rangle =\int d^{3}\mathbf {r} \,\Psi (\mathbf {r} )\left|\mathbf {r} \right\rangle$$}@} is {@{a ket}@} consisting of {@{a superposition of kets}@} with {@{relative coefficients specified by that function}@}. <!--SR:!2027-04-16,440,381!2027-05-09,463,381!2027-05-04,459,381!2027-10-17,599,409!2027-04-01,426,381!2027-03-26,420,381!2027-07-16,523,401!2027-04-08,432,381-->

### changing basis for a spin-1/2 particle

{@{A stationary [spin-1⁄2](spin-1_2.md) particle}@} has {@{a two-dimensional Hilbert space}@}. {@{One [orthonormal basis](orthonormal%20basis.md)}@} is: {@{$$|{\uparrow }_{z}\rangle \,,\;|{\downarrow }_{z}\rangle$$}@} where {@{\|↑<sub>_z_</sub>⟩}@} is {@{the state with a definite value of the [spin operator _S<sub>z</sub>_](angular%20momentum%20operator.md) equal to +1⁄2}@} and {@{\|↓<sub>_z_</sub>⟩}@} is {@{the state with a definite value of the [spin operator _S<sub>z</sub>_](angular%20momentum%20operator.md) equal to −1⁄2}@}. <!--SR:!2027-06-25,506,401!2027-10-12,595,409!2027-07-07,516,401!2027-05-09,460,381!2027-08-02,538,401!2027-05-02,453,381!2027-03-31,424,381!2027-04-04,425,381-->

Since {@{these are a basis}@}, {@{_any_ quantum state of the particle}@} can be expressed as {@{a [linear combination](linear%20combination.md) \(i.e., [quantum superposition](quantum%20superposition.md)\) of these two states}@}: {@{$$|\psi \rangle =a_{\psi }|{\uparrow }_{z}\rangle +b_{\psi }|{\downarrow }_{z}\rangle$$}@} where {@{_a<sub>ψ</sub>_ and _b<sub>ψ</sub>_ are complex numbers}@}. <!--SR:!2027-05-04,455,381!2027-04-23,444,381!2027-04-07,432,381!2027-03-29,423,381!2027-04-07,431,381-->

{@{A _different_ basis}@} for {@{the same Hilbert space}@} is: {@{$$|{\uparrow }_{x}\rangle \,,\;|{\downarrow }_{x}\rangle$$}@} defined in terms of {@{_S<sub>x</sub>_ rather than _S<sub>z</sub>_}@}. <!--SR:!2027-07-19,526,401!2027-07-26,532,401!2027-04-15,440,381!2027-06-24,505,401-->

Again, {@{_any_ state of the particle}@} can be expressed as {@{a linear combination of these two}@}: {@{$$|\psi \rangle =c_{\psi }|{\uparrow }_{x}\rangle +d_{\psi }|{\downarrow }_{x}\rangle$$}@} <!--SR:!2027-04-29,450,381!2027-12-18,652,409!2026-03-27,140,411-->

In {@{vector form}@}, you might write {@{$$|\psi \rangle \doteq {\begin{pmatrix}a_{\psi }\\b_{\psi }\end{pmatrix} }\quad {\text{or} }\quad |\psi \rangle \doteq {\begin{pmatrix}c_{\psi }\\d_{\psi }\end{pmatrix} }$$}@} depending on {@{which basis you are using}@}. In {@{other words}@}, {@{the "coordinates" of a vector}@} depend on {@{the basis used}@}. <!--SR:!2027-04-17,438,381!2027-07-22,528,401!2027-04-29,454,381!2027-04-21,442,381!2027-07-15,523,401!2027-07-18,525,401-->

## pitfalls and ambiguous uses

There are {@{some conventions and uses of notation}@} that may be {@{confusing or ambiguous}@} for {@{the non-initiated or early student}@}. <!--SR:!2027-10-13,596,409!2027-07-12,520,401!2027-04-23,444,381-->

### separation of inner product and vectors

{@{A cause for confusion}@} is that {@{the notation}@} does not {@{separate the inner-product operation}@} from {@{the notation for a \(bra\) vector}@}. If {@{a \(dual space\) bra-vector}@} is constructed as {@{a linear combination of other bra-vectors}@} \(for instance when {@{expressing it in some basis}@}\) {@{the notation}@} {@{creates some ambiguity and hides mathematical details}@}. We can compare {@{bra–ket notation to using bold for vectors}@}, such as {@{${\boldsymbol {\psi } }$}@}, and {@{$(\cdot ,\cdot )$}@} for {@{the inner product}@}. Consider {@{the following dual space bra-vector}@} in {@{the basis $\{|e_{n}\rangle \}$}@}, where {@{$\{\psi _{n}\}$}@} are {@{the complex number coefficients of $\langle \psi |$}@}: {@{$$\langle \psi |=\sum _{n}\langle e_{n}|\psi _{n}$$}@} <!--SR:!2027-04-21,442,381!2027-06-23,504,401!2027-07-25,531,401!2027-04-24,445,381!2027-04-17,441,381!2027-03-30,424,381!2027-04-06,427,381!2027-07-24,530,401!2027-03-30,424,381!2027-08-01,537,401!2027-07-30,535,401!2027-07-13,520,401!2027-06-28,508,401!2027-04-10,431,381!2027-04-16,437,381!2026-11-14,303,341!2026-03-11,126,389!2027-07-10,517,401-->

It has to be {@{determined by convention}@} if {@{the complex numbers $\{\psi _{n}\}$ are inside or outside of the inner product}@}, and each convention {@{gives different results}@}. {@{$$\langle \psi |\equiv ({\boldsymbol {\psi } },\cdot )=\sum _{n}({\boldsymbol {e} }_{n},\cdot )\,\psi _{n}$$}@} <br/> {@{$$\langle \psi |\equiv ({\boldsymbol {\psi } },\cdot )=\sum _{n}({\boldsymbol {e} }_{n}\psi _{n},\cdot )=\sum _{n}({\boldsymbol {e} }_{n},\cdot )\,\psi _{n}^{*}$$}@} <!--SR:!2027-04-16,437,381!2027-05-02,453,381!2026-12-10,310,361!2027-06-28,508,401!2027-10-26,608,409-->

### reuse of symbols

It is common to use {@{the same symbol for _labels_ and _constants_}@}. For example, {@{${\hat {\alpha } }|\alpha \rangle =\alpha |\alpha \rangle$}@}, where {@{the symbol $\alpha$}@} is used simultaneously as {@{the _name of the operator_ ${\hat {\alpha } }$, its _eigenvector_ $|\alpha \rangle$ and the associated _eigenvalue_ $\alpha$}@}. Sometimes {@{the _hat_}@} is {@{also dropped for operators}@}, and one can see notation such as {@{$A|a\rangle =a|a\rangle$}@}.<sup>[\[7\]](#^ref-7)</sup> <!--SR:!2026-03-10,125,389!2027-07-11,519,401!2027-04-10,435,381!2027-05-11,462,381!2027-07-07,516,401!2026-03-08,124,389!2026-03-13,128,389-->

### Hermitian conjugate of kets

It is common to see {@{the usage $|\psi \rangle ^{\dagger }=\langle \psi |$}@}, where {@{the dagger \($\dagger$\) corresponds to the Hermitian conjugate}@}. This is {@{however not correct in a technical sense}@}, since {@{the ket, $|\psi \rangle$}@}, represents {@{a [vector](vector%20(mathematics%20and%20physics).md) in a complex Hilbert-space ${\mathcal {H} }$}@}, and {@{the bra, $\langle \psi |$}@}, is {@{a [linear functional](linear%20functional.md) on vectors in ${\mathcal {H} }$}@}. In other words, {@{$|\psi \rangle$}@} is {@{just a vector}@}, while {@{$\langle \psi |$}@} is {@{the combination of a vector and an inner product}@}. <!--SR:!2027-03-14,404,381!2027-07-01,510,401!2027-08-02,538,401!2027-07-31,536,401!2027-07-14,522,401!2027-04-25,446,381!2027-04-28,449,381!2027-07-12,520,401!2027-12-20,653,409!2027-03-24,417,381!2027-07-14,521,401-->

### operations inside bras and kets

This is done for {@{a fast notation of scaling vectors}@}. For instance, if {@{the vector $|\alpha \rangle$}@} is {@{scaled by $1/{\sqrt {2} }$}@}, it may be {@{denoted $|\alpha /{\sqrt {2} }\rangle$}@}. This can be {@{ambiguous}@} since {@{$\alpha$ is simply a label for a state}@}, and {@{not a mathematical object on which operations can be performed}@}. This usage is {@{more common}@} when denoting {@{vectors as tensor products}@}, where {@{part of the labels}@} are {@{moved __outside__ the designed slot}@}, e.g. {@{$|\alpha \rangle =|\alpha /{\sqrt {2} }\rangle _{1}\otimes |\alpha /{\sqrt {2} }\rangle _{2}$}@}. <!--SR:!2027-05-04,455,381!2027-04-14,439,381!2027-04-14,435,381!2027-04-17,438,381!2027-07-01,511,401!2027-07-13,521,401!2027-07-17,524,401!2027-04-06,427,381!2027-03-15,405,381!2027-07-31,536,401!2027-03-02,408,381!2027-08-01,537,401-->

## linear operators

### linear operators acting on kets

{@{A linear operator}@} is {@{a map that inputs a ket and outputs a ket}@}. \(In order to be {@{called "linear"}@}, it is required to {@{have [certain properties](linear%20operator.md)}@}.\) In other words, if {@{${\hat {A} }$ is a linear operator}@} and {@{$|\psi \rangle$ is a ket-vector}@}, then {@{${\hat {A} }|\psi \rangle$ is another ket-vector}@}. <!--SR:!2027-07-27,533,401!2027-07-24,530,401!2027-04-26,447,381!2027-05-03,458,381!2027-11-01,613,409!2027-06-21,502,401!2027-01-15,365,381-->

In {@{an $N$-dimensional Hilbert space}@}, we can impose {@{a basis on the space}@} and represent {@{$|\psi \rangle$ in terms of its coordinates}@} as {@{a $N\times 1$ [column vector](column%20vector.md)}@}. Using {@{the same basis for ${\hat {A} }$}@}, it is represented by {@{an $N\times N$ complex matrix}@}. {@{The ket-vector ${\hat {A} }|\psi \rangle$}@} can now be {@{computed by matrix multiplication}@}. <!--SR:!2027-06-22,503,401!2027-03-19,413,381!2027-04-03,428,381!2027-06-19,500,401!2027-07-23,529,401!2027-07-27,532,401!2027-07-01,510,401!2027-04-03,428,381-->

{@{Linear operators}@} are {@{ubiquitous in the theory of quantum mechanics}@}. For example, {@{observable physical quantities}@} are represented by {@{[self-adjoint operators](self-adjoint%20operator.md)}@}, such as {@{[energy](energy.md) or [momentum](momentum.md)}@}, whereas {@{transformative processes}@} are represented by {@{[unitary](unitary%20operator.md) linear operators}@} such as {@{rotation or the progression of time}@}. <!--SR:!2027-04-24,445,381!2027-04-13,438,381!2027-04-14,435,381!2026-03-10,125,389!2027-07-29,534,401!2027-08-03,539,401!2027-03-27,421,381!2027-07-06,514,401-->

### linear operators acting on bras

{@{Operators}@} can also be viewed as {@{acting on bras _from the right hand side_}@}. Specifically, if {@{___A___ is a linear operator}@} and {@{⟨<!-- markdown separator -->_φ_\| is a bra}@}, then {@{⟨<!-- markdown separator -->_φ_\|___A___ is another bra defined}@} by the rule {@{$${\bigl (}\langle \phi |{\boldsymbol {A} }{\bigr )}|\psi \rangle =\langle \phi |{\bigl (}{\boldsymbol {A} }|\psi \rangle {\bigr )}\,,$$}@} \(in other words, {@{a [function composition](function%20composition.md)}@}\). {@{This expression}@} is commonly written as \(cf. {@{[energy inner product](energy%20inner%20product.md)}@}\) {@{$$\langle \phi |{\boldsymbol {A} }|\psi \rangle \,.$$}@} <!--SR:!2027-03-31,421,381!2027-07-03,512,401!2027-06-22,503,401!2027-06-30,510,401!2027-08-03,539,401!2027-04-14,439,381!2027-04-08,429,381!2027-12-16,650,409!2027-06-20,501,401!2027-07-25,531,401-->

In {@{an _N_-dimensional Hilbert space}@}, {@{⟨<!-- markdown separator -->_φ_\|}@} can be written as {@{a 1 × _N_ [row vector](row%20vector.md)}@}, and {@{___A___ \(as in the previous section\)}@} is {@{an _N_ × _N_ matrix}@}. Then {@{the bra ⟨<!-- markdown separator -->_φ_\|___A___}@} can be computed by {@{normal matrix multiplication}@}. <!--SR:!2027-04-13,434,381!2027-04-23,444,381!2027-02-25,403,381!2027-06-30,510,401!2027-04-15,436,381!2027-12-14,649,409!2027-04-26,447,381-->

If {@{the same state vector appears on both bra and ket side}@}, {@{$$\langle \psi |{\boldsymbol {A} }|\psi \rangle \,,$$}@} then this expression gives {@{the [expectation value](expectation%20value%20(quantum%20mechanics).md), or mean or average value}@}, of {@{the observable represented by operator ___A___}@} for {@{the physical system in the state \|_ψ_<!-- markdown separator -->⟩}@}. <!--SR:!2027-03-21,414,381!2026-03-14,129,389!2027-07-13,521,401!2026-03-13,128,389!2027-03-28,422,381-->

### outer products

{@{A convenient way}@} to define {@{linear operators on a Hilbert space H}@} is given by {@{the [outer product](outer%20product.md)}@}: if {@{⟨<!-- markdown separator -->_ϕ_\| is a bra}@} and {@{\|_ψ_<!-- markdown separator -->⟩ is a ket}@}, {@{the outer product $$|\phi \rangle \,\langle \psi |$$}@} denotes {@{the [rank-one operator](finite-rank%20operator.md)}@} with the rule {@{$${\bigl (}|\phi \rangle \langle \psi |{\bigr )}(x)=\langle \psi |x\rangle |\phi \rangle .$$}@} <!--SR:!2027-12-21,654,409!2027-04-11,432,381!2027-11-06,617,409!2027-04-12,433,381!2027-04-03,428,381!2027-08-01,537,401!2027-04-30,451,381!2026-11-26,300,361-->

For {@{a finite-dimensional vector space}@}, {@{the outer product}@} can be understood as {@{simple matrix multiplication}@}: {@{$$|\phi \rangle \,\langle \psi |\doteq {\begin{pmatrix}\phi _{1}\\\phi _{2}\\\vdots \\\phi _{N}\end{pmatrix} }{\begin{pmatrix}\psi _{1}^{*}&\psi _{2}^{*}&\cdots &\psi _{N}^{*}\end{pmatrix} }={\begin{pmatrix}\phi _{1}\psi _{1}^{*}&\phi _{1}\psi _{2}^{*}&\cdots &\phi _{1}\psi _{N}^{*}\\\phi _{2}\psi _{1}^{*}&\phi _{2}\psi _{2}^{*}&\cdots &\phi _{2}\psi _{N}^{*}\\\vdots &\vdots &\ddots &\vdots \\\phi _{N}\psi _{1}^{*}&\phi _{N}\psi _{2}^{*}&\cdots &\phi _{N}\psi _{N}^{*}\end{pmatrix} }$$}@} \(annotation: Note {@{the second operand is conjugated}@}, as {@{preferred by physicists}@}.\) The outer product is {@{an _N_ × _N_ matrix}@}, as expected for {@{a linear operator}@}. <!--SR:!2027-03-25,419,381!2027-07-06,515,401!2027-05-04,458,381!2027-05-04,455,381!2027-07-30,535,401!2027-06-30,509,401!2027-04-20,445,381!2027-02-27,405,381-->

{@{One of the uses}@} of the outer product is to {@{construct [projection operators](projection%20operator.md)}@}. Given {@{a ket \|_ψ_<!-- markdown separator -->⟩ of norm 1}@}, {@{the orthogonal projection}@} onto {@{the [subspace](linear%20subspace.md) spanned by \|_ψ_<!-- markdown separator -->⟩}@} is {@{$$|\psi \rangle \,\langle \psi |\,.$$}@} This is {@{an [idempotent](idempotent.md)}@} in {@{the algebra of observables that acts on the Hilbert space}@}. <!--SR:!2027-04-26,447,381!2027-04-18,439,381!2027-07-18,525,401!2027-04-23,448,381!2027-04-02,423,381!2027-07-02,512,401!2027-04-17,438,381!2027-04-11,436,381-->

### Hermitian conjugate operator

Just as {@{kets and bras}@} can be {@{transformed into each other}@} \(making {@{\|_ψ_<!-- markdown separator -->⟩ into ⟨<!-- markdown separator -->_ψ_\|}@}\), {@{the element from the dual space}@} corresponding to {@{_A_\|_ψ_<!-- markdown separator -->⟩}@} is {@{⟨<!-- markdown separator -->_ψ_\|_A_<sup>†</sup>}@}, where {@{_A_<sup>†</sup> denotes the Hermitian conjugate \(or adjoint\) of the operator _A_}@}. ## properties

Bra–ket notation was designed to {@{facilitate the formal manipulation of linear-algebraic expressions}@}. {@{Some of the properties}@} that allow {@{this manipulation}@} are listed herein. In what follows, {@{_c_<sub>1</sub> and _c_<sub>2</sub>}@} denote {@{arbitrary [complex numbers](complex%20number.md)}@}, {@{_c_\*}@} denotes {@{the [complex conjugate](complex%20conjugate.md) of _c_}@}, {@{_A_ and _B_}@} denote {@{arbitrary linear operators}@}, and these properties are to {@{hold for any choice of bras and kets}@}. <!--SR:!2027-04-12,436,381!2027-07-28,534,401!2027-03-27,417,381!2027-03-01,407,381!2027-03-25,418,381!2027-03-16,406,381!2027-04-13,434,381!2027-04-22,443,381!2027-04-21,442,381!2027-07-08,516,401-->

### linearity

- Since bras are linear functionals, ::@:: $$\langle \phi |{\bigl (}c_{1}|\psi _{1}\rangle +c_{2}|\psi _{2}\rangle {\bigr )}=c_{1}\langle \phi |\psi _{1}\rangle +c_{2}\langle \phi |\psi _{2}\rangle \,.$$ <!--SR:!2027-06-24,504,401!2027-03-24,418,381-->
- By the definition of addition and scalar multiplication of linear functionals in the dual space,<sup>[\[8\]](#^ref-8)</sup> ::@:: $${\bigl (}c_{1}\langle \phi _{1}|+c_{2}\langle \phi _{2}|{\bigr )}|\psi \rangle =c_{1}\langle \phi _{1}|\psi \rangle +c_{2}\langle \phi _{2}|\psi \rangle \,.$$ <!--SR:!2027-05-08,459,381!2027-04-04,428,381-->

### associativity

Given {@{any expression}@} involving {@{complex numbers, bras, kets, inner products, outer products, and/or linear operators}@} \(but {@{not addition}@}\), written in {@{bra–ket notation}@}, {@{the parenthetical groupings}@} {@{do not matter}@} \(i.e., {@{the [associative property](associative%20property.md)}@} holds\). For example: {@{$${\begin{aligned}\langle \psi |{\bigl (}A|\phi \rangle {\bigr )}={\bigl (}\langle \psi |A{\bigr )}|\phi \rangle \,&{\stackrel {\text{def} }{=} }\,\langle \psi |A|\phi \rangle \\{\bigl (}A|\psi \rangle {\bigr )}\langle \phi |=A{\bigl (}|\psi \rangle \langle \phi |{\bigr )}\,&{\stackrel {\text{def} }{=} }\,A|\psi \rangle \langle \phi |\end{aligned} }$$}@} and {@{so forth}@}. {@{The expressions on the right}@} \(with {@{no parentheses whatsoever}@}\) are {@{allowed to be written unambiguously}@} _because_ of {@{the equalities on the left}@}. Note that {@{the associative property}@} does _not_ hold for {@{expressions that include nonlinear operators}@}, such as {@{the [antilinear](antilinear.md) [time reversal operator](T-symmetry.md) in physics}@}. <!--SR:!2027-04-20,441,381!2027-04-20,441,381!2027-06-29,509,401!2027-03-26,416,381!2027-03-30,423,381!2027-06-17,498,401!2027-05-02,453,381!2027-03-27,420,381!2027-07-11,519,401!2027-07-22,528,401!2026-03-14,129,389!2027-04-06,427,381!2027-11-25,633,409!2027-04-19,440,381!2027-07-25,531,401!2027-03-31,425,381-->

### Hermitian conjugation

Bra–ket notation makes it {@{particularly easy to compute the Hermitian conjugate}@} \(also {@{called _dagger_, and denoted †}@}\) of expressions. The formal rules are: <!--SR:!2027-04-04,425,381!2027-07-20,527,401-->

- The Hermitian conjugate of a bra ::@:: is the corresponding ket, and vice versa. <!--SR:!2027-03-29,423,381!2027-04-15,436,381-->
- The Hermitian conjugate of a complex number ::@:: is its complex conjugate. <!--SR:!2027-04-13,437,381!2027-05-06,457,381-->
- The Hermitian conjugate of the Hermitian conjugate of anything \(linear operators, bras, kets, numbers\) ::@:: is itself—i.e., $$\left(x^{\dagger }\right)^{\dagger }=x\,.$$ <!--SR:!2027-06-27,507,401!2027-04-23,444,381-->
- Given any combination of complex numbers, bras, kets, inner products, outer products, and/or linear operators, written in bra–ket notation, ::@:: its Hermitian conjugate can be computed by reversing the order of the components, and taking the Hermitian conjugate of each. <!--SR:!2027-03-01,407,381!2027-04-25,446,381-->

These rules are {@{sufficient to formally write the Hermitian conjugate}@} of {@{any such expression}@}; some examples are as follows: <!--SR:!2027-04-09,430,381!2027-08-03,539,401-->

- Kets: ::@:: $${\bigl (}c_{1}|\psi _{1}\rangle +c_{2}|\psi _{2}\rangle {\bigr )}^{\dagger }=c_{1}^{*}\langle \psi _{1}|+c_{2}^{*}\langle \psi _{2}|\,.$$ <!--SR:!2027-04-12,437,381!2027-04-07,428,381-->
- Inner products: ::@:: $$\langle \phi |\psi \rangle ^{*}=\langle \psi |\phi \rangle \,.$$ Note that ⟨<!-- markdown separator -->_φ_\|_ψ_<!-- markdown separator -->⟩ is a scalar, so the Hermitian conjugate is just the complex conjugate, i.e., $${\bigl (}\langle \phi |\psi \rangle {\bigr )}^{\dagger }=\langle \phi |\psi \rangle ^{*}$$ <!--SR:!2027-11-20,629,409!2027-05-10,461,381-->
- Matrix elements: ::@:: $${\begin{aligned}\langle \phi |A|\psi \rangle ^{\dagger }&=\left\langle \psi \left|A^{\dagger }\right|\phi \right\rangle \\\left\langle \phi \left|A^{\dagger }B^{\dagger }\right|\psi \right\rangle ^{\dagger }&=\langle \psi |BA|\phi \rangle \,.\end{aligned} }$$ <!--SR:!2027-04-08,433,381!2027-03-22,416,381-->
- Outer products: ::@:: $${\Big (}{\bigl (}c_{1}|\phi _{1}\rangle \langle \psi _{1}|{\bigr )}+{\bigl (}c_{2}|\phi _{2}\rangle \langle \psi _{2}|{\bigr )}{\Big )}^{\dagger }={\bigl (}c_{1}^{*}|\psi _{1}\rangle \langle \phi _{1}|{\bigr )}+{\bigl (}c_{2}^{*}|\psi _{2}\rangle \langle \phi _{2}|{\bigr )}\,.$$ <!--SR:!2027-04-19,443,381!2027-05-03,457,381-->

## the unit operator

Consider {@{a complete [orthonormal](orthonormal.md) system \(_basis_\)}@}, {@{$$\{e_{i}\ |\ i\in \mathbb {N} \}\,,$$}@} for {@{a Hilbert space _H_}@}, with respect to {@{the norm from an inner product ⟨·,·⟩}@}. <!--SR:!2027-04-29,450,381!2027-02-27,405,381!2027-06-28,508,401!2027-03-28,421,381-->

From {@{basic [functional analysis](functional%20analysis.md)}@}, it is known that {@{any ket $|\psi \rangle$}@} can also be {@{written as $$|\psi \rangle =\sum _{i\in \mathbb {N} }\langle e_{i}|\psi \rangle |e_{i}\rangle ,$$}@} with {@{⟨·\|·⟩ the inner product on the Hilbert space}@}. <!--SR:!2027-01-05,336,361!2027-05-06,457,381!2027-07-07,515,401!2027-04-09,430,381-->

From {@{the commutativity of kets with \(complex\) scalars}@}, it follows that {@{$$\sum _{i\in \mathbb {N} }|e_{i}\rangle \langle e_{i}|=\mathbb {I}$$}@} must be {@{the _identity operator_}@}, which {@{sends each vector to itself}@}. <!--SR:!2027-04-13,437,381!2027-08-02,538,401!2027-04-09,430,381!2027-10-31,613,409-->

This, then, can be {@{inserted in any expression without affecting its value}@}; for example {@{$${\begin{aligned}\langle v|w\rangle &=\langle v|\left(\sum _{i\in \mathbb {N} }|e_{i}\rangle \langle e_{i}|\right)|w\rangle \\&=\langle v|\left(\sum _{i\in \mathbb {N} }|e_{i}\rangle \langle e_{i}|\right)\left(\sum _{j\in \mathbb {N} }|e_{j}\rangle \langle e_{j}|\right)|w\rangle \\&=\langle v|e_{i}\rangle \langle e_{i}|e_{j}\rangle \langle e_{j}|w\rangle \,,\end{aligned} }$$}@} where, in the last line, {@{the [Einstein summation convention](Einstein%20summation%20convention.md)}@} has been {@{used to avoid clutter}@}. <!--SR:!2027-04-12,433,381!2026-03-12,127,389!2027-06-26,507,401!2027-07-06,515,401-->

In {@{quantum mechanics}@}, it often occurs that {@{little or no information about the inner product ⟨<!-- markdown separator -->_ψ_\|_φ_<!-- markdown separator -->⟩}@} of {@{two arbitrary \(state\) kets is present}@}, while it is still possible to {@{say something about the expansion coefficients ⟨<!-- markdown separator -->_ψ_\|_e<sub>i</sub>_<!-- markdown separator -->⟩ = ⟨<!-- markdown separator -->_e<sub>i</sub>_\|_ψ_<!-- markdown separator -->⟩\* and ⟨<!-- markdown separator -->_e<sub>i</sub>_\|_φ_<!-- markdown separator -->⟩}@} of {@{those vectors with respect to a specific \(orthonormalized\) basis}@}. In this case, it is particularly useful to {@{insert the unit operator into the bracket one time or more}@}. <!--SR:!2027-04-20,441,381!2027-07-02,511,401!2027-04-05,429,381!2027-05-02,456,381!2026-03-08,124,389!2026-12-24,324,361-->

Consequently, {@{the corresponding wavefunction}@} is {@{a constant}@}, {@{$\langle x|\varpi \rangle {\sqrt {2\pi \hbar } }=1$}@} \(annotation: Substitute {@{$p = 0$}@}. ## notation used by mathematicians

{@{The object}@} physicists are considering when {@{using bra–ket notation}@} is {@{a Hilbert space}@} \({@{a [complete](complete%20metric%20space.md) inner product space}@}\). <!--SR:!2027-07-26,532,401!2027-05-02,453,381!2027-04-22,447,381!2027-12-17,651,409-->

Let {@{$({\mathcal {H} },\langle \cdot ,\cdot \rangle )$}@} be {@{a Hilbert space}@} and _h_ {@{∈ H a vector in H}@}. What physicists would denote by {@{\|_h_<!-- markdown separator -->⟩}@} is {@{the vector itself}@}. That is, {@{$$|h\rangle \in {\mathcal {H} }.$$}@} <!--SR:!2027-07-31,536,401!2027-05-01,452,381!2027-04-09,430,381!2027-07-28,534,401!2027-04-05,430,381!2027-05-08,459,381-->

Let {@{H\*}@} be {@{the dual space of H}@}. This is {@{the space of linear functionals on H}@}. {@{The [embedding](embedding.md) $\Phi :{\mathcal {H} }\hookrightarrow {\mathcal {H} }^{*}$}@} is defined by {@{$\Phi (h)=\varphi _{h}$}@}, where for {@{every _h_ ∈ H}@} {@{the linear functional $\varphi _{h}:{\mathcal {H} }\to \mathbb {C}$}@} satisfies {@{for every _g_ ∈ H the functional equation $\varphi _{h}(g)=\langle h,g\rangle =\langle h\mid g\rangle$}@}. {@{Notational confusion}@} arises when {@{identifying _φ<sub>h</sub>_ and _g_ with ⟨<!-- markdown separator -->_h_\| and \|_g_<!-- markdown separator -->⟩ respectively}@}. This is because of {@{literal symbolic substitutions}@}. Let {@{$\varphi _{h}=H=\langle h\mid$}@} and let {@{_g_ = G = \|_g_<!-- markdown separator -->⟩}@}. This gives {@{$$\varphi _{h}(g)=H(g)=H(G)=\langle h|(G)=\langle h|{\bigl (}|g\rangle {\bigr )}\,.$$}@} One {@{ignores the parentheses}@} and {@{removes the double bars}@}. <!--SR:!2027-07-19,526,401!2027-04-04,429,381!2027-03-23,417,381!2027-03-30,420,381!2027-06-23,504,401!2027-04-01,426,381!2027-05-05,460,381!2027-04-22,443,381!2027-07-28,534,401!2027-04-22,443,381!2027-04-30,451,381!2026-03-14,129,389!2027-04-20,441,381!2026-12-15,315,361!2027-07-16,523,401!2027-04-12,437,381-->

Moreover, mathematicians usually write {@{the dual entity}@} {@{not at the first place, as the physicists do, but at the second one}@}, and they usually use {@{not an [asterisk](asterisk.md) but an overline}@} \(which the physicists reserve for {@{averages and the [Dirac spinor adjoint](Dirac%20equation.md#conservation%20of%20probability%20current)}@}\) to denote {@{[complex conjugate](complex%20conjugate.md) numbers}@}; i.e., for {@{scalar products}@} mathematicians usually write {@{$$\langle \phi ,\psi \rangle =\int \phi (x){\overline {\psi (x)} }\,dx\,,$$}@} whereas physicists would write for the same quantity {@{$$\langle \psi |\phi \rangle =\int dx\,\psi ^{*}(x)\phi (x)~.$$}@} <!--SR:!2027-11-18,627,409!2027-08-02,538,401!2027-11-05,616,409!2027-04-09,434,381!2027-06-27,507,401!2027-04-25,450,381!2027-04-12,433,381!2027-05-07,458,381-->
