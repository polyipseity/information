{@{The standard __probability axioms__}@} are the foundations of {@{[probability theory](probability%20theory.md) introduced by Russian mathematician [Andrey Kolmogorov](Andrey%20Kolmogorov.md) in 1933}@}.<sup>[\[1\]](#^ref-1)</sup> Like {@{all [axiomatic systems](axiomatic%20system.md)}@}, they outline {@{the basic assumptions underlying the application of probability}@} to fields such as {@{pure mathematics and the physical sciences}@}, while {@{avoiding logical paradoxes}@}.<sup>[\[2\]](#^ref-2)</sup> <!--SR:!2026-03-13,15,290!2026-03-14,16,290!2026-03-12,14,290!2026-03-12,14,290!2026-03-12,14,290!2026-03-14,16,290-->

{@{The probability axioms}@} do not {@{specify or assume any particular [interpretation of probability](probability%20interpretations.md)}@}, but may be motivated by starting {@{from a philosophical definition of probability}@} and arguing that {@{the axioms are satisfied by this definition}@}. For example, <!--SR:!2026-03-12,14,290!2026-03-14,16,290!2026-03-12,14,290!2026-03-14,16,290-->

- {@{[Cox's theorem](Cox's%20theorem.md)}@} derives {@{the laws of probability}@} based on {@{a "logical" definition of probability}@} as {@{the likelihood or credibility of arbitrary logical propositions}@}.<sup>[\[3\]](#^ref-3)</sup><sup>[\[4\]](#^ref-4)</sup>
- {@{The [Dutch book arguments](Dutch%20book%20arguments.md)}@} show that {@{[rational agents](rational%20agent.md)}@} must make {@{bets which are in proportion}@} with {@{a subjective measure of the probability of events}@}. <!--SR:!2026-03-12,14,290!2026-03-12,14,290!2026-03-13,15,290!2026-03-12,14,290!2026-03-12,14,290!2026-03-13,15,290!2026-03-14,16,290!2026-03-12,14,290-->

{@{The third axiom, [σ-additivity](σ-additivity.md)}@}, is relatively {@{modern, and originates with Lebesgue's [measure theory](measure%20(mathematics).md)}@}. {@{Some authors}@} replace this with {@{the strictly weaker axiom of finite additivity}@}, which is {@{sufficient to deal with some applications}@}.<sup>[\[5\]](#^ref-5)</sup> <!--SR:!2026-03-12,14,290!2026-03-12,14,290!2026-03-12,14,290!2026-03-12,14,290!2026-03-14,16,290-->

## Kolmogorov axioms

In order to state {@{the Kolmogorov axioms}@}, {@{the following pieces of data}@} must be specified: \(annotation: 3 items: {@{sample space, event space, probability measure}@}\) <!--SR:!2026-03-14,16,290!2026-03-13,15,290!2026-03-12,14,290-->

- {@{The [sample space](sample%20space.md), $\Omega$}@}, which is {@{the [set](set%20theory.md) of all possible [outcomes](outcome%20(probability).md) or [elementary events](elementary%20event.md)}@}.
- {@{The space of all [events](event%20(probability%20theory).md)}@}, which are each {@{taken to be sets of outcomes \(i.e. subsets of $\Omega$\)}@}. {@{The event space, $F$}@}, must be {@{a [__σ__-algebra](σ-algebra.md) on $\Omega$}@}.
- {@{The probability [measure](measure%20(mathematics).md) $P$}@} which assigns {@{to each event $E\in F$ its probability, $P(E)$}@}. <!--SR:!2026-03-14,16,290!2026-03-14,16,290!2026-03-12,14,290!2026-03-14,16,290!2026-03-13,15,290!2026-03-12,14,290!2026-03-12,14,290!2026-03-13,15,290-->

{@{Taken together}@}, these assumptions mean that {@{$(\Omega ,F,P)$ is a [measure space](measure%20space.md)}@}. It is additionally {@{assumed that $P(\Omega )=1$}@}, making this {@{triple a [probability space](probability%20space.md)}@}.<sup>[\[1\]](#^ref-1)</sup> <!--SR:!2026-03-12,14,290!2026-03-12,14,290!2026-03-12,14,290!2026-03-12,14,290-->

### first axiom

{@{The probability of an event}@} is {@{a non-negative real number}@}. {@{This assumption}@} is implied by the fact that {@{$P$ is a measure on $F$}@}. {@{$$P(E)\geq 0\qquad \forall E\in F$$}@} {@{Theories which assign [negative probability](negative%20probability.md)}@} {@{relax the first axiom}@}. <!--SR:!2026-03-12,14,290!2026-03-13,15,290!2026-03-14,16,290!2026-03-12,14,290!2026-03-13,15,290!2026-03-12,14,290!2026-03-12,14,290-->

### second axiom

This is {@{the assumption of [unit measure](unit%20measure.md)}@}: that {@{the probability that at least one of the [elementary events](elementary%20event.md)}@} in {@{the entire sample space will occur is 1}@}. {@{$$P(\Omega )=1$$}@} From {@{this axiom}@} it {@{follows that $P(E)$ is always finite}@}, in contrast with {@{more general [measure theory](measure%20(mathematics).md)}@}. <!--SR:!2026-03-12,14,290!2026-03-12,14,290!2026-03-13,15,290!2026-03-14,16,290!2026-03-12,14,290!2026-03-13,15,290!2026-03-12,14,290-->

### third axiom

This is {@{the assumption of [σ-additivity](σ-additivity.md)}@}: {@{Any [countable](countable.md) sequence}@} of {@{[disjoint sets](disjoint%20sets.md) \(synonymous with _[mutually exclusive](mutual%20exclusivity.md)_ events\) $E_{1},E_{2},\ldots$}@} satisfies {@{$$P\left(\bigcup _{i=1}^{\infty }E_{i}\right)=\sum _{i=1}^{\infty }P(E_{i}).$$}@} {@{This property}@} again is implied by the fact that {@{$P$ is a measure}@}. Note that, by taking {@{$E_{1}=\Omega$ and $E_{i}=\emptyset$ for all $i>1$}@}, one deduces that {@{$P(\emptyset )=0$}@}. This in turn shows that {@{σ-additivity implies finite additivity}@}. <!--SR:!2026-03-12,14,290!2026-03-12,14,290!2026-03-13,15,290!2026-03-14,16,290!2026-03-12,14,290!2026-03-13,15,290!2026-03-14,16,290!2026-03-12,14,290!2026-03-12,14,290-->

Some authors consider {@{merely [finitely additive](finitely%20additive.md#finitely%20additive%20set%20function) probability spaces}@}, in which case one just needs {@{an [algebra of sets](field%20of%20sets.md), rather than a [σ-algebra](σ-algebra.md)}@}.<sup>[\[6\]](#^ref-6)</sup> {@{[Quasiprobability distributions](quasiprobability%20distribution.md)}@} in general {@{relax the third axiom}@}. <!--SR:!2026-03-12,14,290!2026-03-09,11,270!2026-03-13,15,290!2026-03-13,15,290-->

## elementary consequences

In order to demonstrate that {@{the theory generated by the Kolmogorov axioms}@} corresponds with {@{[classical probability](classical%20definition%20of%20probability.md)}@}, {@{some elementary consequences}@} are {@{typically derived}@}.<sup>[\[7\]](#^ref-7)</sup> (annotation: 4 items: {@{complement rule, empty set, monotonicity, bounds}@}) <!--SR:!2026-03-12,14,290!2026-03-12,14,290!2026-03-13,15,290!2026-03-12,14,290!2026-03-13,15,290-->

- Since $P$ is {@{finitely additive}@}, we have {@{$P(A)+P(A^{c})=P(A\cup A^{c})=P(\Omega )=1$}@}, so {@{$P(A^{c})=1-P(A)$}@}.
- In particular, it follows that {@{$P(\emptyset )=0$}@}. {@{The empty set}@} is interpreted as {@{the event that "no outcome occurs"}@}, which is {@{impossible}@}.
- Similarly, if {@{$A\subseteq B$}@}, then {@{$P(B)=P(A\cup (B\setminus A))=P(A)+P(B\setminus A)\geq P(A)$}@}. In other words, {@{$P$ is [monotone](monotonic%20function.md)}@}.<sup>[\[8\]](#^ref-8)</sup>
- Since {@{$\emptyset \subseteq E\subseteq \Omega$}@} for {@{any event $E$}@}, it follows that {@{$0\leq P(E)\leq 1$}@}. <!--SR:!2026-03-12,14,290!2026-03-12,14,290!2026-03-14,16,290!2026-03-12,14,290!2026-03-12,14,290!2026-03-12,14,290!2026-03-14,16,290!2026-03-12,14,290!2026-03-14,16,290!2026-03-12,14,290!2026-03-12,14,290!2026-03-12,14,290!2026-03-12,14,290-->

By {@{dividing $A\cup B$ into the disjoint sets}@} {@{$A\setminus (A\cap B)$, $B\setminus (A\cap B)$ and $A\cap B$}@}, one arrives at {@{a probabilistic version of the inclusion-exclusion principle}@}<sup>[\[9\]](#^ref-9)</sup> {@{$$P(A\cup B)=P(A)+P(B)-P(A\cap B).$$}@} In the case where {@{$\Omega$ is finite}@}, {@{the two identities \(annotation: the probabilistic version and the set-counting version\) are equivalent}@}. <!--SR:!2026-03-14,16,290!2026-03-13,15,290!2026-03-13,15,290!2026-03-12,14,290!2026-03-14,16,290!2026-03-13,15,290-->

In order to actually do {@{calculations when $\Omega$ is an infinite set}@}, it is sometimes useful to {@{generalize from a finite sample space}@}. For example, if $\Omega$ consists of {@{all infinite sequences of tosses of a fair coin}@}, it is not {@{obvious how to compute the probability of any particular set of sequences \(i.e. an event\)}@}. If the event is {@{"every flip is heads"}@}, then it is {@{intuitive that the probability can be computed}@} as: {@{$$P({\text{infinite sequence of heads} })=\lim _{n\to \infty }P({\text{sequence of n heads} })=\lim _{n\to \infty }2^{-n}=0.$$}@} In order to make {@{this rigorous}@}, one has to prove that {@{$P$ is __continuous__, in the following sense}@}. If {@{$A_{j},\,\,j=1,2,\ldots$}@} is {@{a sequence of events increasing \(or decreasing\) to another event $A$}@}, then<sup>[\[10\]](#^ref-10)</sup> {@{$$\lim _{n\to \infty }P(A_{n})=P(A).$$}@} <!--SR:!2026-03-13,15,290!2026-03-12,14,290!2026-03-12,14,290!2026-03-14,16,290!2026-03-14,16,290!2026-03-12,14,290!2026-03-12,14,290!2026-03-12,14,290!2026-03-14,16,290!2026-03-14,16,290!2026-03-12,14,290!2026-03-12,14,290-->

## simple example: coin toss

Consider {@{a single coin-toss}@}, and assume that the coin will {@{either land heads \(H\) or tails \(T\) \(but not both\)}@}. {@{No assumption is made}@} as to whether {@{the coin is fair}@}.<sup>[\[11\]](#^ref-11)</sup> <!--SR:!2026-03-12,14,290!2026-03-13,15,290!2026-03-12,14,290!2026-03-12,14,290-->

\(annotation: context: {@{a single potentially-non-fair coin-toss}@}\) We may define: {@{$$\Omega =\{H,T\}$$}@} {@{$$F=\{\varnothing ,\{H\},\{T\},\{H,T\}\}$$}@} {@{Kolmogorov's axioms}@} imply that: {@{$$P(\varnothing )=0$$}@} The probability of {@{_neither_ heads _nor_ tails, is 0}@}. {@{$$P(\{H,T\}^{c})=0$$}@} The probability of {@{_either_ heads _or_ tails, is 1}@}. {@{$$P(\{H\})+P(\{T\})=1$$}@} The sum of {@{the probability of heads and the probability of tails, is 1}@}. <!--SR:!2026-03-12,14,290!2026-03-14,16,290!2026-03-13,15,290!2026-03-12,14,290!2026-03-12,14,290!2026-03-13,15,290!2026-03-13,15,290!2026-03-12,14,290!2026-03-13,15,290!2026-03-12,14,290-->
