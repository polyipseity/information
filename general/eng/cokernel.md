# cokernel

- "Coker \(mathematics\)" redirects here. For other uses, see [Coker \(disambiguation\)](coker%20(disambiguation).md).

{@{The __cokernel__}@} of {@{a [linear mapping](linear%20mapping.md) of [vector spaces](vector%20spaces.md) _f_ : _X_ → _Y_}@} is {@{the [quotient space](quotient%20space%20(linear%20algebra).md) _Y_ / im\(_f_\) of the [codomain](codomain.md) of _f_ by the image of _f_}@}. {@{The dimension of the cokernel}@} is called {@{the _corank_ of _f_}@}. <!--SR:!2026-01-05,258,330!2025-09-03,160,310!2025-12-28,253,330!2025-12-27,252,330!2025-12-09,236,330-->

Cokernels are {@{[dual](dual%20(category%20theory).md) to the [kernels of category theory](kernel%20(category%20theory).md)}@}, hence {@{the name}@}: {@{the kernel is a [subobject](subobject.md) of the domain \(it maps to the domain\)}@}, while {@{the cokernel is a [quotient object](quotient%20object.md#quotient%20object) of the codomain \(it maps from the codomain\)}@}. <!--SR:!2026-01-19,270,330!2026-02-07,284,330!2026-08-17,419,310!2026-01-05,258,330-->

Intuitively, given {@{an equation _f_\(_x_\) = _y_ that one is seeking to solve}@}, the cokernel {@{measures the _constraints_ that _y_ must satisfy for this equation to have a solution – the obstructions to a solution}@} – while the kernel {@{measures the _degrees of freedom_ in a solution, if one exists}@}. This is elaborated in [intuition](#intuition), below. <!--SR:!2026-02-13,290,330!2025-12-08,235,330!2026-02-05,282,330-->

More generally, {@{the cokernel of a [morphism](morphism.md) _f_ : _X_ → _Y_ in some [category](category%20theory.md)}@} \(e.g. {@{a [homomorphism](group%20homomorphism.md) between [groups](group%20(mathematics).md) or a [bounded linear operator](bounded%20linear%20operator.md) between [Hilbert spaces](Hilbert%20space.md)}@}\) is {@{an object _Q_ and a morphism _q_ : _Y_ → _Q_}@} such that {@{the composition _q f_ is the [zero morphism](zero%20morphism.md) of the category}@}, and furthermore {@{_q_ is [universal](universal%20mapping%20property.md) with respect to this property}@}. Often {@{the map _q_ is understood (annotation: implicitly implied by _Q_)}@}, and {@{_Q_ itself is called the cokernel of _f_}@}. <!--SR:!2025-09-08,163,310!2026-10-21,465,310!2026-05-18,335,290!2025-12-30,255,330!2026-01-18,269,330!2026-01-04,257,330!2026-09-15,430,310-->

In {@{many situations in [abstract algebra](abstract%20algebra.md)}@}, such as {@{for [abelian groups](abelian%20group.md), [vector spaces](vector%20space.md) or [modules](module%20(mathematics).md)}@}, {@{the cokernel of the [homomorphism](homomorphism.md) _f_ : _X_ → _Y_}@} is {@{the [quotient](quotient%20set.md#quotient%20set) of _Y_ by the [image](image%20(mathematics).md) of _f_}@}. In {@{[topological](topology.md) settings}@}, such as {@{with bounded linear operators between Hilbert spaces}@}, one typically has to {@{take the [closure](closure%20(mathematics).md) of the image before passing to the quotient}@}. <!--SR:!2025-12-22,248,330!2025-10-15,191,310!2026-03-01,280,290!2027-02-07,538,310!2026-01-15,267,330!2025-10-14,190,310!2025-12-06,235,330-->

## formal definition

One can {@{define the cokernel in the general framework of [category theory](category%20theory.md)}@}. In order for {@{the definition to make sense}@} the category in question {@{must have [zero morphisms](zero%20morphism.md)}@}. {@{The __cokernel__ of a [morphism](morphism.md) _f_ : _X_ → _Y_}@} is defined as {@{the [coequalizer](coequalizer.md) of _f_ and the zero morphism 0<sub>_XY_</sub> : _X_ → _Y_}@}. <!--SR:!2026-09-04,419,310!2026-01-03,256,330!2026-01-20,271,330!2025-10-13,189,310!2026-03-26,297,290-->

Explicitly, this means the following. {@{The cokernel of _f_ : _X_ → _Y_}@} is {@{an object _Q_ together with a morphism _q_ : _Y_ → _Q_}@} such that {@{the diagram <p> ![Commutative diagram for [cokernel](cokernel.md).](../../archives/Wikimedia%20Commons/Cokernel-01.svg) <p> [commutes](commutative%20diagram.md)}@}. Moreover, the morphism _q_ {@{must be [universal](universal%20property.md) for this diagram}@}, i.e. {@{any other such _q_<!-- markdown separator -->′ : _Y_ → _Q_<!-- markdown separator -->′ can be obtained by composing _q_ with a unique morphism _u_ : _Q_ → _Q_<!-- markdown separator -->′}@}: <p> {@{![commutative diagram for [cokernel](cokernel.md), showing universality](../../archives/Wikimedia%20Commons/Cokernel-02.png)}@} <p> As with {@{all universal constructions the cokernel}@}, {@{if it exists, is unique [up to](up%20to.md) a unique [isomorphism](isomorphism.md)}@}, or more precisely: {@{if _q_ : _Y_ → _Q_ and _q_<!-- markdown separator -->′ : _Y_ → _Q_<!-- markdown separator -->′ are two cokernels of _f_ : _X_ → _Y_}@}, then {@{there exists a unique isomorphism _u_ : _Q_ → _Q_<!-- markdown separator -->′ with _q'_ = _u_ _q_}@}. <!--SR:!2025-10-16,189,310!2026-12-15,499,310!2027-05-05,626,330!2026-01-25,275,330!2026-01-26,276,330!2025-08-29,140,290!2025-12-04,231,330!2026-01-13,265,330!2026-07-07,355,290!2025-09-11,165,310-->

Like {@{all coequalizers}@}, {@{the cokernel _q_ : _Y_ → _Q_ is necessarily an [epimorphism](epimorphism.md)}@}. Conversely {@{an epimorphism is called _[normal](normal%20morphism.md)_ \(or _conormal_\)}@} {@{if it is the cokernel of some morphism}@}. {@{A category is called _conormal_}@} {@{if every epimorphism is normal \(e.g. the [category of groups](category%20of%20groups.md) is conormal\)}@}. <!--SR:!2026-01-12,264,330!2026-04-23,315,290!2025-10-07,182,310!2025-11-26,191,290!2025-08-29,156,310!2025-10-08,182,310-->

### examples

In {@{the [category of groups](category%20of%20groups.md)}@}, {@{the cokernel of a [group homomorphism](group%20homomorphism.md) _f_ : _G_ → _H_}@} is {@{the [quotient](quotient%20group.md) of _H_ by the [normal closure](normal%20closure%20(group%20theory).md) of the image of _f_}@}. In {@{the case of [abelian groups](abelian%20group.md)}@}, since {@{every [subgroup](subgroup.md) is normal}@}, {@{the cokernel is just _H_ [modulo](ideal%20(ring%20theory).md) the image of _f_}@}: {@{$$\operatorname {coker} (f)=H/\operatorname {im} (f).$$}@} <!--SR:!2025-12-14,241,330!2025-09-01,159,310!2026-06-10,297,250!2026-02-04,281,330!2025-10-14,188,310!2025-12-21,247,330!2026-01-21,272,330-->

### special cases

In {@{a [preadditive category](preadditive%20category.md)}@}, it makes sense to {@{add and subtract morphisms}@}. In such a category, {@{the [coequalizer](coequalizer.md) of two morphisms _f_ and _g_ \(if it exists\)}@} is {@{just the cokernel of their difference: $$\operatorname {coeq} (f,g)=\operatorname {coker} (g-f).$$}@} In {@{an [abelian category](abelian%20category.md) \(a special kind of preadditive category\)}@} {@{the [image](image%20(category%20theory).md) and [coimage](coimage.md) of a morphism _f_}@} are given by {@{$${\begin{aligned}\operatorname {im} (f)&=\ker(\operatorname {coker} f),\\\operatorname {coim} (f)&=\operatorname {coker} (\ker f).\end{aligned} }$$}@} In particular, {@{every abelian category is normal \(and conormal as well\)}@}. That is, {@{every [monomorphism](monomorphism.md) _m_ can be written as the kernel of some morphism}@}. Specifically, _m_ is {@{the kernel of its own cokernel: $$m=\ker(\operatorname {coker} (m))$$}@} <!--SR:!2026-01-03,256,330!2026-02-16,292,330!2026-01-24,274,330!2026-02-17,293,330!2027-06-13,656,330!2026-02-02,279,330!2026-04-09,307,290!2025-10-19,191,310!2025-10-11,187,310!2025-10-12,188,310-->

## intuition

The cokernel can be thought of as {@{the space of _constraints_ that an equation must satisfy, as the space of _obstructions_}@}, just as {@{the [kernel](kernel%20(algebra).md) is the space of _solutions_}@}. <!--SR:!2026-01-04,257,330!2025-12-04,233,330-->

Formally, one may {@{connect the kernel and the cokernel of a map _T_: _V_ → _W_}@} by {@{the [exact sequence](exact%20sequence.md) $$0\to \ker T\to V{\overset {T}{\longrightarrow } }W\to \operatorname {coker} T\to 0.$$}@} These can be interpreted thus: given {@{a linear equation _T_\(_v_\) = _w_ to solve}@}, <!--SR:!2026-01-14,266,330!2025-12-28,212,270!2026-02-09,286,330-->

- the kernel ::@:: is the space of _solutions_ to the _homogeneous_ equation _T_\(_v_\) = 0, and its dimension is the number of _degrees of freedom_ in solutions to _T_\(_v_\) = _w_, if they exist; <!--SR:!2026-01-25,275,330!2025-10-20,192,310-->
- the cokernel ::@:: is the space of _constraints_ on _w_ that must be satisfied if the equation is to have a solution, and its dimension is the number of independent constraints that must be satisfied for the equation to have a solution. <!--SR:!2025-09-02,159,310!2025-12-05,234,330-->

{@{The dimension of the cokernel plus the dimension of the image \(the rank\)}@} {@{add up to the dimension of the target space}@}, as {@{the dimension of the quotient space _W_ / _T_\(_V_\)}@} is {@{simply the dimension of the space _minus_ the dimension of the image}@}. <!--SR:!2027-05-12,631,330!2025-12-17,244,330!2025-10-15,188,310!2025-09-01,157,310-->

As a simple example, consider {@{the map _T_: __R__<sup>2</sup> → __R__<sup>2</sup>}@}, given by {@{_T_\(_x_, _y_\) = \(0, _y_\)}@}. Then for {@{an equation _T_\(_x_, _y_\) = \(_a_, _b_\) to have a solution}@}, we {@{must have _a_ = 0 \(one constraint\)}@}, and in that case the solution space is {@{\(_x_, _b_\), or equivalently, \(0, _b_\) + \(_x_, 0\), \(one degree of freedom\)}@}. The kernel may be {@{expressed as the subspace \(_x_, 0\) ⊆ _V_}@}: the value of _x_ is {@{the freedom in a solution}@}. The cokernel may be {@{expressed via the real valued map _W_: \(_a_, _b_\) → \(_a_\)}@}: given a vector \(_a_, _b_\), {@{the value of _a_ is the _obstruction_ to there being a solution}@}. <!--SR:!2026-02-12,289,330!2026-02-03,280,330!2026-02-08,285,330!2025-12-23,249,330!2025-12-29,254,330!2025-12-16,243,330!2025-12-19,246,330!2025-12-18,245,330!2026-01-05,258,330-->

Additionally, the cokernel can be thought of as {@{something that "detects" [surjections](surjection.md)}@} in the same way that {@{the kernel "detects" [injections](injection%20(mathematics).md)}@}. A map is {@{injective if and only if its kernel is trivial}@}, and a map is {@{surjective if and only if its cokernel is trivial}@}, or in other words, {@{if _W_ = im\(_T_\)}@}. <!--SR:!2027-05-25,641,330!2025-12-20,246,330!2025-09-20,172,310!2026-02-18,294,330!2025-10-17,190,310-->
